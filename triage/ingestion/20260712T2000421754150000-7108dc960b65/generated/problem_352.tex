\noindent\textbf{Problem Statement:}

Is there some $c>0$ such that every measurable $A\subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ of measure $\geq c$ contains the vertices of a triangle of area 1?

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Remarks:}

Erd\H{o}s (unpublished) proved that this is true if $A$ has infinite measure, or if $A$ is an unbounded set of positive measure (stating in \cite{Er78d} and \cite{Er83d} it 'follows easily from the Lebesgue density theorem'). 

In \cite{Er78d} and \cite{Er83d} he speculated that perhaps $C=4\pi/\sqrt{27}\approx 2.418$ works, which would be the best possible, as witnessed by a circle of radius $<2\cdot 3^{-3/4}$.

Further evidence for this is given by a result of Freiling and Mauldin \cite{Ma02}, who proved that if $A$ has outer measure $>4\pi/\sqrt{27}$ then $A$ contains the vertices of a triangle with area $>1$. This also proves the same threshold for the original problem under the assumption that $A$ is a compact convex set. 

Mauldin also discusses this problem in \cite{Ma13}, in which he notes that it suffices to prove this under the assumption that $A$ is the union of the interiors of $n<\infty$ many compact convex sets. Freiling and Mauldin (see \cite{Ma13}) have proved this conjecture if $1\leq n\leq 3$.
