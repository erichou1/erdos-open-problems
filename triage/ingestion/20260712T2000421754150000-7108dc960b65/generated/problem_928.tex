\noindent\textbf{Problem Statement:}

Let $\alpha,\beta\in (0,1)$ and let $P(n)$ denote the largest prime divisor of $n$. Does the density of integers $n$ such that $P(n)<n^{\alpha}$ and $P(n+1)<(n+1)^\beta$ exist?

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Remarks:}

Dickman \cite{Di30} showed the density of smooth $n$, with largest prime factor $<n^\alpha$, is $\rho(1/\alpha)$ where $\rho$ is the Dickman function.

Erd\H{o}s also asked whether infinitely many such $n$ even exist, but Meza has observed that this follows immediately from Schinzel's result \cite{Sc67b} that for infinitely many $n$ the largest prime factor of $n(n+1)$ is at most $n^{O(1/\log\log n)}$.

Erd\H{o}s asked whether the events $P(n)<n^\alpha$ and $P(n+1)<(n+1)^\beta$ are independent, in the sense that the density of $n$ satisfying both conditions is equal to $\rho(1/\alpha)\rho(1/\beta)$. 

Ter\"{a}v\"{a}inen \cite{Te18} has proved the logarithmic density exists and is equal to $\rho(1/\alpha)\rho(1/\beta)$. 

Wang \cite{Wa21} has proved the density is $\rho(1/\alpha)\rho(1/\beta)$ assuming the Elliott-Halberstam conjecture for friable integers.

In \cite{Er76e} Erd\H{o}s conjectured that the density of the set of $n$ such that all of $n,\ldots,n+r-1$ are $n^{\alpha}$ smooth (i.e. $P(n+i)\leq n^{\alpha}$ for $0\leq i<r$) is $\rho(1/\alpha)^r$.

See also [370].
