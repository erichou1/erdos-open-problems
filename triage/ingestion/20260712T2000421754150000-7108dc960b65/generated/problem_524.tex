\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Problem Statement:}

For any $t\in (0,1)$ let $t=\sum_{k=1}^\infty \epsilon_k(t)2^{-k}$ (where $\epsilon_k(t)\in \{0,1\}$). What is the correct order of magnitude (for almost all $t\in(0,1)$) for\[M_n(t)=\max_{x\in [-1,1]}\left\lvert \sum_{k\leq n}(-1)^{\epsilon_k(t)}x^k\right\rvert?\]

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Remarks:}

A problem of Salem and Zygmund \cite{SaZy54}. Chung showed that, for almost all $t$, there exist infinitely many $n$ such that\[M_n(t) \ll \left(\frac{n}{\log\log n}\right)^{1/2}.\]Erd\H{o}s (unpublished) showed that for almost all $t$ and every $\epsilon>0$ we have $\lim_{n\to \infty}M_n(t)/n^{1/2-\epsilon}=\infty$.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{References:}

[SaZy54] Salem, R. and Zygmund, A., Some properties of trigonometric series whose terms have
random signs. Acta Math. (1954), 245-301.
