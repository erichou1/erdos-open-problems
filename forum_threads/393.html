Let $f(n)$ denote the minimal $m\geq 1$ such that\[n! = a_1\cdots a_t\]with $a_1<\cdots <a_t=a_1+m$. What is the behaviour of $f(n)$?
#393: [ErGr80,p.76]
number theory | factorials

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Erdős and Graham write that they do not even know whether $f(n)=1$ infinitely often (i.e. whether a factorial is the product of two consecutive integers infinitely often).

Let $F_m(N)$ count the number of $n\leq N$ such that $f(n)=m$. Berend and Osgood [BeOs92] proved that, for each fixed $m$, $F_m(N)=o(N)$. Bui, Pratt, and Zaharescu [BPZ23] have shown that\[F_m(N)\ll_m N^{33/34}.\]A result of Luca [Lu02] implies that $f(n)\to \infty$ as $n\to \infty$, conditional on the ABC conjecture.

  • Starting with the ideas of Tao in this thread, I put together a write-up that, conditional on abc, settles f(n) between the bounds:
    n - O(log n) $\leq$ f(n) $\leq$ n - 2 for all sufficiently large n.

    The writeup is at this Overleaf link.

    I tried to push this to an abc-unconditional proof, but every reduction I attempted and every sub-case I treated still left a remaining kernel that looks a lot like abc itself: typically a primitive triple of nearby integers with anomalously small radical relative to height, or an arithmetic configuration that morally invokes the same bound. In this sense, the problem is settled at least partially for now, and the abc-unconditional question remains hard.

  • Some initial observations inspired by the numerical data. The obvious factorization $n! = 2 \dots n$ gives $f(n) \leq n-2$ for all $n>2$. It also seems that $f(n) = n-O(\log n)$ for all but a few exceptional $n$. Indeed, suppose that $m=f(n) < n - C \log n$ for a large $C$. By Kummer's theorem, $2$ divides $n!$ $n-O(\log n)$ times, and if $2^k$ is the largest power of $2$ that divides one of the $a_i$, then the number of powers of $2$ dividing $a_1 \dots a_t$ is at most $\sum_{j=1}^k \min( \frac{m}{2^j}+1, t)$, which one can bound by $m+k$ by ignoring the $t$ factor; thus $k > 10 \log n$ (say) by hypothesis, which makes at least one of the $a_i$ of size at least $2^k \geq n^{10}$ hence all of them are at least this size since $m \leq n$. By Stirling's formula this gives the bound $t k \ll n \log n$, so $t$ is less than a small multiple of $n$. The inequality $n - O(\log n) \leq \sum_{j=1}^k \min( \frac{m}{2^j}+1, t) \ll k + t \log \frac{m}{t}$ then forces $k \gg n$, and hence $t = O(\log n)$. Thus $n!$ has somehow been factored into only $O(\log n)$ factors that are distance $O(n)$ apart and size at least $n^{10}$, which seems like quite a rare occurrence. These factors would need to be divisible by a lot of small primes, and perhaps one could rule a lot of scenarios out using the abc conjecture or similar tools.

    • To continue the previous analysis: suppose that one has $t = O(\log n)$ for some large $n$ as above. For any prime $p \leq n^{0.99}$, $p$ divides $n! = a_1 \dots a_t$ about $n/p$ times, but if $l > 10 \log n$ (say) then at most one of the $a_1,\dots,a_t$ can be divisible by $p^l$. From this and the assumption $t = O(\log n)$ one can conclude that one of the $a_i$ is divisible by $p$ at least $0.99 n/p$ times (say). The product $\prod_{p \leq n^{0.99}} p^{0.99 n/p}$ is something like $(n!)^{0.98}$, so by the pigeonhole principle one should be able to find two $a_i,a_j$ each of which is divisible by a product of prime powers $p^{0.99 n/p}$ of size $\gg a_i^{0.95}, a_j^{0.95}$ say, which makes the radical of $a_i$ or $a_j$ of size $O(a_i^{0.1})$ or $O(a_j^{0.1})$. But as $a_i = a_j + O(n)$ and $a_i, a_j \geq n^{10}$, this would violate the abc conjecture. So I think one can get a bound of the form $f(n) = n - O(\log n)$ assuming the abc conjecture.

      Another observation: if one can find some small $j,l$ such that $(j+1) \dots (j+l) | \binom{n+j}{j} = \frac{(j+1) \dots (j+n)}{n!}$, then this almost certainly leads to the bound $f(n) \leq n-l-1$, because $(j+l+1) \dots (j+n)$ is now a moderately large multiple of $n!$ and one should be able to remove some terms from this product to recover $n!$ exactly. Thus for instance the trivial $1 | \binom{n+0}{0}$ gives the trivial bound $f(n) \leq n-2$, $2 \times 3 | \binom{n+1}{1}$ or $3 \times 4 | \binom{n+2}{2}$ should lead to $f(n) \leq n-3$, and so forth.
