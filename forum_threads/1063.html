Let $k\geq 2$ and define $n_k\geq 2k$ to be the least value of $n$ such that $n-i$ divides $\binom{n}{k}$ for all but one $0\leq i<k$. Estimate $n_k$.
#1063: [ErSe83]

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
A problem of Erdős and Selfridge posed in [ErSe83]. Erdős and Selfridge noted (and a proof can be found in [Mo85]) that if $n\geq 2k$ then there must exist at least one $0\leq i<k$ such that $n-i$ does not divide $\binom{n}{k}$.

We have $n_2=4$, $n_3=6$, $n_4=9$, and $n_5=12$. Monier [Mo85] observed that $n_k\leq k!$ for $k\geq 3$, since $\binom{k!}{k}$ is divisible by $k!-i$ for $1\leq i<k$. Cambie observes in the comments that this can be improved to\[n_k\leq k[2,3,\ldots,k-1]\leq e^{(1+o(1))k},\]where $[\cdots]$ is the least common multiple.

This is discussed in problem B31 of Guy's collection [Gu04].

  • We prove the following lower bound:\[
    n_k\ge
    \max\left(2k,\ \prod_{p^a\parallel k}p^{a+\lfloor\log_p(k/2)\rfloor}\right).
    \]Let \(n=n_k\), \(C=\binom nk\), and write \(d_i=n-i\). Let \(e\) be the unique index for which \(d_e\nmid C\). Fix \(p^a\parallel k\), and put\[
    M=\max_{0\le i<k}v_p(d_i),\qquad S=v_p(C).
    \]Since\[
    S=\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}v_p(d_i)-v_p(k!),
    \]and the levels \(p,p^2,\ldots,p^a\) cancel exactly between the \(k\) consecutive integers \(d_i\) and \(k!\), while each higher level contributes at most \(1\), we have\[
    S\le M-a.
    \]Hence every \(d_i\) with \(v_p(d_i)=M\) fails to divide \(C\). Since there is only one failure, this maximum must occur at \(d_e\).

    Now let\[
    t=\lfloor\log_p(k/2)\rfloor .
    \]Because \(p^t\le k/2\), among the \(k\) consecutive integers \(d_i\) at least two are divisible by \(p^t\). One may be \(d_e\), but another one divides \(C\), so \(S\ge t\). Therefore\[
    M\ge a+t.
    \]Thus\[
    p^{a+\lfloor\log_p(k/2)\rfloor}\mid d_e
    \]for every \(p^a\parallel k\). Multiplying over \(p\mid k\) gives\[
    \prod_{p^a\parallel k}p^{a+\lfloor\log_p(k/2)\rfloor}\mid d_e\le n,
    \]and since also \(n\ge2k\), the claimed lower bound follows. I am unsure whether this, combined with Stijn’s upper bound, resolves the problem. I do not think it does, since I would guess Erdős is asking for sharper bounds. (Note: This was AI assisted by GPT-5.5 Thinking)

    This implies an exponential upper bound $\exp((1+o(1))n).$
