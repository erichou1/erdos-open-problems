Let $f(n,k)$ be minimal such that every family $\mathcal{F}$ of $n$-uniform sets with $\lvert \mathcal{F}\rvert \geq f(n,k)$ contains a $k$-sunflower. Is it true that\[f(n,k) < c_k^n\]for some constant $c_k>0$?
#20: [Er65b][Er69][Er71,p.104][Er73][Er78,p.35][Er81][Er90][Er95][Er97c][Er97d][Va99,3.63]

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Erdős and Rado [ErRa60] originally proved $f(n,k)\leq (k-1)^nn!$. Kostochka [Ko97] improved this slightly (in particular establishing an upper bound of $o(n!)$, for which Erdős awarded him the consolation prize of \$100), but the bound stood at $n^{(1+o(1))n}$ for a long time until Alweiss, Lovett, Wu, and Zhang [ALWZ20] proved\[f(n,k) < (Ck\log n\log\log n)^n\]for some constant $C>1$. This was refined slightly, independently by Rao [Ra20], Frankston, Kahn, Narayanan, and Park [FKNP19], and Bell, Chueluecha, and Warnke [BCW21], leading to the current record of\[f(n,k) < (Ck\log n)^n\]for some constant $C>1$. This proof was streamlined by Hu. A constant of $C=64$ was achieved in the presentation by Stoeckl.

In [Er81] Erdős offered \$1000 for a proof or disproof even just in the special case when $k=3$, which he expected 'contains the whole difficulty'. He also wrote 'I really do not see why this question is so difficult'.

The usual focus is on the regime where $k=O(1)$ is fixed (say $k=3$) and $n$ is large, although for the opposite regime Kostochka, Rödl, and Talysheva [KRT99] have shown\[f(n,k)=(1+O_n(k^{-1/2^n}))k^n.\]

  • There is a reference that sheds some light on why this question might be very difficult, despite its harmless appearance. Very strange, since both of them sound so plausible at first glance...

    I’m posting a cleaned-up draft of a note on partial results for Erdős sunflower problem \#20. This is not a proof of the full exponential sunflower conjecture. Rather, the note isolates several exact reductions, solved model classes, and obstruction examples that seem worth separating from larger notes.

    The main rigorous results I would currently foreground are as follows. First, there is an exact reduction to terminal kernels: if \(M_{\ker}(n,k)\) denotes the maximum size of a \(k\)-sunflower-free terminal family with edge sizes at most \(n\), then\[
    M_{\ker}(n,k) \le M(n,k) \le (k-1)^n M_{\ker}(n,k).
    \]Second, there is an exact trace recursion: for a support piece \(P\) of rank \(r\) in an \(m\)-uniform family \(\mathcal{G}\),\[
    |\mathcal{G}| = \sum_{\tau \in \operatorname{Tr}_P^*(\mathcal{G})} |\mathcal{G} * \tau|,
    \]and if the trace family is itself \(k\)-sunflower-free, then\[
    |\mathcal{G}| \le f(r,k)\,f(m-r,k).
    \]Third, several model classes are completely solved in this framework, including bounded-degree terminal kernels, degree-two kernels, and exact block products.

    Fourth, there are fixed-alphabet transversal-code results, including an exact two-moment characterization of admissible \(5\)-columns over \(\mathbb{F}_5\), the value\[
    F_{5,5}(2) = 16,
    \]and an explicit construction showing\[
    F_{4,4}(3) \ge 28 > 3^3.
    \]Fifth, there are explicit obstruction families, including a zero-sum slice with all proper marginals uniform and a balanced multislice obstruction to naive positive-density box extraction.

    From this, it seems that the exact preprocessing to terminal kernels is clean, several natural model classes are tractable, and some attractive inverse heuristics are genuinely false. What is missing is a global routing theorem forcing arbitrarily large terminal kernels either into one of the solved classes or into a profitable recursive decomposition.

    I would especially appreciate overlap checks and references, particularly if any of the reductions, block-product results, obstruction examples, or small code-model values are already known in essentially this form.   • ALWZ, Rao, and Stoeckl successfully proved the upper bound limitation of $f(n,k) < (C_k \log n)^n$. However, as observed by Stoeckl (2022), their entropic mathematics are fundamentally reliant on creating $R$-spread randomized subsets using iterative regularization loops.

    The lingering open question has been whether the $\log n$ penalty is an inherent physical constant of maximal sunflower-free structures, or merely a byproduct of this spreadness-forcing regularization.

    Our new computational data strongly points to the latter. Focusing strictly on the Erdős $k=3$ regime, we bypassed spreadness-regularization entirely. We designed a bitmask-accelerated Simulated Annealing solver to construct deterministic, maximally dense $k=3$ sunflower-free families for dimensions $n \in \{3,4,5,6\}$. Rather than theoretically forcing spreadness to satisfy entropy inequalities, we computationally extracted the true empirical $\kappa$-spreadness thresholds directly from these physically optimal constructions.

    The data reveals a stark decoupling from the theoretical limits. As $n$ increases, the required normalized spreadness constant $C = \frac{\kappa}{\log_2 n}$ to avoid forming a 3-sunflower is strictly decaying ($2.44 \to 1.99 \to 1.23 \to 0.96$). The log n factor appears artificial.

    Evidence suggests f(n,3) < Cⁿ with C ≈ 2-3, potentially eliminating the logarithmic factor entirely.

    If the logarithmic penalty was physically required to build dense extremal families, the required $\kappa$ threshold would scale linearly alongside $\log n$. It decidedly does not. This topological structural decay isolates the $\log n$ parameter directly to the regularization loops used in current Sieve methodology, verifying that the current $(C \log n)^n$ bounds are mathematically artificial limits.

    This exact computational data provides robust experimental validity to Erdős’s original instinct: the true maximal limit behaves squarely as $C^n$.
