Is it true that\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty(-1)^n\frac{n}{p_n}\]converges, where $p_n$ is the sequence of primes?

Erdős suggested that a computer could be used to explore this, and did not see any other method to attack this.

Tao [Ta23] has proved that this series does converge assuming a strong form of the Hardy-Littlewood prime tuples conjecture.

In [Er98] Erdős further conjectures that\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{1}{n(p_{n+1}-p_n)}\]converges and\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{1}{p_{n+1}-p_n}\]diverges. Weisenberg notes that the existence of infinitely many bounded gaps between primes (as proved by Zhang [Zh14]) implies the latter series does not converge. Weisenberg also has an argument which shows that, assuming the Hardy-Littlewood prime $k$-tuples conjecture, the series is unbounded in at least one direction (positive or negative).

Erdős further conjectured that\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^n \frac{1}{n(p_{n+1}-p_n)(\log\log n)^c}\]converges for every $c>0$, and reports that he and Nathanson can prove that this series converges absolutely for $c>2$ (and can show, conditional on 'hopeless' conjectures about the primes, that this sum does not converge absolutely for $c=2$).

Sawhney has provided the following proof that this series converges absolutely for $c>2$: note that, whenever $c>1$, the contribution to the sum from gaps $p_{n+1}-p_n\geq \log n$ is convergent, so it suffices to consider only small gaps. The number of $n\leq X$ such that $p_{n+1}-p_n\in [\epsilon\log n,2\epsilon \log n)$ is bounded above by $\ll \epsilon X$ (this can be proved via the Selberg sieve). In particular, applying this bound for $\frac{1}{\log n}\leq \epsilon \leq 1$ of the shape $2^{-j}$ (of which there are at most $\log\log n$ possibilities) shows the desired convergence, since\[\sum \frac{1}{n(\log n)(\log\log n)^{c-1}}\]converges.

  • Remarks and slight improvements on Tao's results [Ta23]. Let $f(n):=(-1)^{\pi(n)}$ and $F(x):=\sum_{n\le x}f(n)$. Tao (following an observation of Said) records the equivalence\[
    \sum_{n\ge1}(-1)^n\frac{n}{p_n}\ \text{converges}
    \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
    \sum_{n\ge2}\frac{(-1)^{\pi(n)}}{n\log n}
    =\sum_{n\ge2}\frac{f(n)}{n\log n}\ \text{converges}.
    \]By partial summation with $g(x)=1/(x\log x)$,\[
    \sum_{n\le X}\frac{f(n)}{n\log n}
    =F(X)g(X)-F(2)g(2)+\int_2^X F(t)\,(-g'(t))\,dt,
    \qquad -g'(t)\asymp \frac{1}{t^2\log t},
    \]so a minimal analytic sufficient condition is the existence of $\varepsilon>0$ with\[
    F(x)=O\!\left(\frac{x}{(\log x)^{\varepsilon}}\right),
    \]since then $\int_2^\infty \frac{dt}{t(\log t)^{1+\varepsilon}}<\infty$ and the boundary term $F(X)/(X\log X)\to0$; hence Erdős' series converges.

    A natural intermediate conjecture that targets exactly such a bound (and is weaker in spirit than full Hardy--Littlewood for all patterns) is parity mixing in short intervals: there exist functions $\lambda=\lambda(X)\to\infty$ and some $\delta>0$ such that, with $\Delta_\lambda(x):=\pi(x+\lambda\log x)-\pi(x)$,\[
    \mathbb{E}_{x\in[X,2X]}\,(-1)^{\Delta_\lambda(x)}\ \ll\ (\log X)^{-\delta}.
    \]This is a single Fourier-coefficient estimate for the distribution of prime counts in $(x,x+\lambda\log x]$; it asserts that the parity of $\Delta_\lambda(x)$ is asymptotically unbiased on average. Combined with the same van der Corput/Weyl-differencing framework used by Tao, such a decay of $\mathbb{E}(-1)^{\Delta_\lambda(x)}$ at some slowly growing $\lambda(X)$ is designed to force cancellation in $f(n)=(-1)^{\pi(n)}$, plausibly yielding the needed logarithmic power saving $F(x)\ll x(\log x)^{-\varepsilon}$ and hence convergence.

    A second, more distributional intermediate target is an anti-concentration / local limit statement for $\Delta_\lambda(x)$: for some $\lambda=\lambda(X)\to\infty$ and some $\eta>0$,\[
    \sup_{k\in\mathbb{Z}}\ \textrm{Prob} _{x\in[X,2X]}\bigl(\Delta_\lambda(x)=k\bigr)\ \ll\ \lambda^{-\eta}
    \quad\text{or even}\quad
    \|L(\Delta_\lambda)-\mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\to 0.
    \]Either form rules out concentration on one parity and typically implies parity mixing (for Poisson$(\lambda)$ one has $\mathbb{E}[(-1)^{\Delta}]=e^{-2\lambda}$). Thus, proving any averaged local limit theorem (or even robust anti-concentration) for prime counts in length $\lambda\log x$ intervals would give a direct path to the analytic bound on $F(x)$ above, potentially avoiding the need for full high-order Hardy--Littlewood uniformity; Tao’s conditional proof obtains the requisite parity mixing via strong $k$-tuple input, so any alternative route to anti-concentration here would be genuinely new input toward the Erdős convergence problem.

  • The website mentions that I have an argument showing that
    \begin{equation*}
    \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1)^n\frac{1}{p_{n+1} - p_n}
    \end{equation*}
    is unbounded in at least one direction (i.e. positive or negative) assuming the Hardy-Littlewood prime $k$-tuples conjecture. In a comment in [141], I pointed out that (assuming the prime $k$-tuples conjecture) if $A \subseteq \mathbb{N}$ is admissible, then there are infinitely many translations of $A$ into consecutive primes. (EDIT: this result was actually originally due to Schinzel and Sierpinski.) So let $A$ be an admissible set that consists of pairs of numbers that differ by two. We can choose $A$ such that there are lots of pairs, and such that the pairs are spaced widely apart. By making the amount of pairs and the distances between pairs arbitrarily large, we can make it so the series changes by an arbitrarily large amount at every translation of $A$ into a set of consecutive primes. That completes the proof.


    Unfortunately, the prime $k$-tuples conjecture doesn't give us a good way of controlling the index of the primes (i.e. odd or even), so it's not clear which direction the series must be unbounded in or whether it must be unbounded in both directions. Perhaps this could be explored assuming a uniform version of the prime $k$-tuples conjecture?
