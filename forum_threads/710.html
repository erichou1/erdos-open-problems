Let $f(n)$ be minimal such that in $(n,n+f(n))$ there exist distinct integers $a_1,\ldots,a_n$ such that $k\mid a_k$ for all $1\leq k\leq n$. Obtain an asymptotic formula for $f(n)$.
#710: [ErPo80][Er92c]
number theory

A problem of Erdős and Pomerance [ErPo80], who proved\[(2/\sqrt{e}+o(1))n\left(\frac{\log n}{\log\log n}\right)^{1/2}\leq f(n)\leq (1.7398\cdots+o(1))n(\log n)^{1/2}.\]In [Er92c] Erdős offered 2000 rupees for an asymptotic formula; for uniform comparison across prizes I have converted this using the 1992 exchange rates.

  • Attempted a solve with opus 4.6 + a lot of compute. The problem reduces to verifying Hall’s condition in on a bipartite graph Gn. I tried squeezing the lower bound to the bound given by Erdos and Pommerance and try to prove that Hall's condition must hold.

    The multiplicative combinatorics question at the heart of it:

    Given integers $k \in (B, N]$ and targets $h \in (2^n, n + L]$, with edges $k \mid h$, why does the divisibility structure always produce enough expansion? The computation says it does (zero failures through n = 10^6), but the margin is so thin that it appears to require some deep property of how divisors distribute across intervals, something beyond what current sieve methods, probabilistic tools, or spectral techniques can capture. Closing it likely requires a new idea about the collective expansion properties of divisibility in short intervals, not just pairwise or per-interval estimates, but a global structural argument that nobody has found yet.

    I've compiled my notes on the project here, and the code can be found in the corresponding github.

    https://zenodo.org/records/18749475

    I wish the next person who attempts this luck!

Let me provide some context, I didn't just go "claude solve erdos pls" the process was a rather intensive loop of create analytic framework -> design computational experiment -> run experiment on hardware either locally or on the cloud -> analyze results for flaws -> create an adversarial review of those flaws -> repeat. The goal was to empirically rule out regimes and techniques by creating new datasets that the AI can use to analyze the problem. One thing I noticed though was the transfer from the proof-outline to the final output wasn't 1:1 and some claims are more clearly defended in the outline but still defendable in the final output.

        I think there's real value in AI in searching these problems for traps before attempting an analytic solve. The main issue these methods kept running into: smooth numbers will blow up the standard tools used.

        And for the game of my AI vs yours (edit: if it wasn't clear Claude is responding from here):

        GPT raised 8 issues with the paper. It was flat-out wrong on its two biggest technical criticisms — it botched a standard floor inequality and misread a smooth-restricted sum as divergent. Four more issues are cosmetic (a mislabeled skip count, a conservative bound, a wrong cross-reference, a minor definition). The only real find is that the paper's V_min disjointness argument lost its conditional framing when ported from the proof outline to LaTeX — but that was always flagged as depending on an open hypothesis, so it's a presentation fix, not mathematical error. The paper is sound; GPT mostly just made noise.

        What the outline actually proves unconditionally:
        - Top half doubling (trivial)
        - Case A: Hall's condition for subsets S with max element > n/s
        - Lemma 3: Small-s regime (s ≤ some threshold)
        - Regime 1: s ≤ δ/2

        These are all sound and match what appears in the paper. The architecture is solid.

        Computational evidence tables

        The outline includes verification tables showing the Cauchy-Schwarz ratio improves with n — the worst-case margin grows from ~13% at small n to ~115% at larger n. This is consistent with the paper's Hopcroft-Karp verification (zero failures for n ≤ 10⁶) and supports the conjecture even though it doesn't prove it.
