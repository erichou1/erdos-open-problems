OPEN This is open, and cannot be resolved with a finite computation.
Let $k\geq 2$. Does there exist a prime $p$ and consecutive intervals $I_1,\ldots,I_k$ such that\[\prod_{n\in I_i}n \equiv 1\pmod{p}\]for all $1\leq i\leq k$?
#1056: [Gu04]
number theory

There are claims of partial results and/or a path to a solution in the comments.
This is problem A15 in Guy's collection [Gu04], where he reports that in a letter in 1979 Erdős observed that\[3\cdot 4\equiv 5\cdot 6\cdot 7\equiv 1\pmod{11},\]establishing the case $k=2$. Makowski [Ma83] found, for $k=3$,\[2\cdot 3\cdot 4\cdot 5\equiv 6\cdot 7\cdot 8\cdot 9\cdot 10\cdot 11\equiv 12\cdot 13\cdot 14\cdot 15\equiv 1\pmod{17}.\]Noll and Simmons asked, more generally, whether there are solutions to $q_1!\equiv\cdots \equiv q_k!\pmod{p}$ for arbitrarily large $k$ (with $q_1<\cdots<q_k$).

  • Every $2\leq k\leq 14$ has an explicit finite witness for Problem #1056. This follows from the OEIS entry A060427 [2] and its b-file [3], following up on the comment by StijnC — 17:24 on 28 Sep 2025 [1].

    The OEIS entry defines $a(n)$ as the smallest prime $p$ for which there are $n$ strings of consecutive integers whose products are $1 \pmod p$, and the b-file lists $a(2)=11,\ldots,a(14)=10428007$. Here is the case $k=14$ written out explicitly.

    For $p=10428007$, the following fifteen factorials are all congruent to the same residue, $8978998$, modulo $p$: 816488!, 1251081!, 3384225!, 4112650!, 4237275!, 4431559!, 4467010!, 4835062!, 7328694!, 7385077!, 7415726!, 8460938!, 8689396!, 9295594!, 9661614!.

    Hence the adjacent intervals
    [816489,1251081], [1251082,3384225], [3384226,4112650], [4112651,4237275], [4237276,4431559], [4431560,4467010], [4467011,4835062], [4835063,7328694], [7328695,7385077], [7385078,7415726], [7415727,8460938], [8460939,8689396], [8689397,9295594], [9295595,9661614]

    all have product congruent to $1 \pmod {10428007}$. Indeed, if $q_i! \equiv q_{i+1}! \equiv r \pmod p$ with $r\not\equiv 0\pmod p$, then the product over the adjacent interval is $\prod_{n=q_i+1}^{q_{i+1}} n = q_{i+1}!/q_i! \equiv r/r \equiv 1 \pmod p$.
    I also verified directly that $10428007$ is prime and that each of the 14 listed interval products is $1$ modulo $10428007$. The displayed $k=14$ witness also gives witnesses for every smaller $k\geq 2$ by taking the first $k$ intervals. This does not address the arbitrary-$k$ question, but it records the finite range $2\leq k\leq 14$ explicitly.

      • Hardy–Subbarao's construction is indeed related, and Remark 2.8 of their paper in the American Mathematical Monthly explains the use of arithmetic progressions to force $2n+1$ to be composite. In the tetrad problem, however, we need two exceptional values, $q+2$ and $2q+1$. Thus the condition $\gcd(a,2b+1)>1$ alone is insufficient; one also needs to exclude $q+2$, for instance by requiring $\gcd(a,b+2)>1$. Concretely: $\gcd(10,2\cdot 7 +1) = \gcd(10,15)=5>1$, but $q+2=7K+10+2 = 7K+12$ can be prime (taking $K=1$, for example, having $q=17$), while $2q+1=2(7K+10)+1 = 14K+21$ is composite. The choice $q=6t+1$ is a simple one satisfying both requirements.
