Is there some $c>0$ such that every measurable $A\subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ of measure $\geq c$ contains the vertices of a triangle of area 1?
#352: [Er78d,p.122][Er81b,p.30][Er83d,p.323][Va99,2.47]

Comment activity that has not yet been incorporated into the remarks
There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Erdős (unpublished) proved that this is true if $A$ has infinite measure, or if $A$ is an unbounded set of positive measure (stating in [Er78d] and [Er83d] it 'follows easily from the Lebesgue density theorem').

In [Er78d] and [Er83d] he speculated that perhaps $C=4\pi/\sqrt{27}\approx 2.418$ works, which would be the best possible, as witnessed by a circle of radius $<2\cdot 3^{-3/4}$.

Further evidence for this is given by a result of Freiling and Mauldin [Ma02], who proved that if $A$ has outer measure $>4\pi/\sqrt{27}$ then $A$ contains the vertices of a triangle with area $>1$. This also proves the same threshold for the original problem under the assumption that $A$ is a compact convex set.

Mauldin also discusses this problem in [Ma13], in which he notes that it suffices to prove this under the assumption that $A$ is the union of the interiors of $n<\infty$ many compact convex sets. Freiling and Mauldin (see [Ma13]) have proved this conjecture if $1\leq n\leq 3$.

    • One useful fact stated in Mauldin's paper is the following: this problem is equivalent to the one in which we restrict attention to sets $A$ that are unions of finitely many closed disks of equal radius. Indeed, by the inner regularity of the Lebesgue measure, it suffices to consider only compact sets $A$. After covering $A$ by $\varepsilon$-disks centered at points of $A$, one can apply Besicovitch's covering theorem to obtain the desired equivalence.

      • Even slightly better, a further reduction is easy to see: one can assume that $A$ is a finite union of congruent dyadic squares, which are sets of the form\[ \big[2^{-n}k,2^{-n}(k+1)\big) \times\big[2^{-n}l,2^{-n}(l+1)\big) \]for $k,l,n\in\mathbb{Z}$. They are better because they are disjoint and correspond to the integer lattice $\mathbb{Z}^2$.
        After scaling by $2^n$, one can essentially think about the corresponding discrete problem: can one find $\omega(4^n)$ points inside the grid $[4^n]\times[4^n]$ such that no three of them form a triangle of area $\approx \alpha$ for some fixed $\alpha$ of the order $4^n$.
        I'm not being precise here, I'm just saying that constructions of such large discrete sets would lead to the counterexample to the main problem and vice versa. Some careless discrete reformulations can go wrong, of course, e.g., if one targets the precise area $\alpha$ which is an odd number, while $A$ consists of points with both even coordinates.

        • Nice reduction! Based on your idea, I formulated a precise translation into a discrete problem. For an integer $N>0$, we say that a set of lattice points $S\subset \mathbb{Z}^2$ is $N$-avoiding if for every $q_1,q_2,q_3\in S$ we have\[
          \bigl\lvert \mu(\triangle q_1q_2q_3) - N \bigr\rvert > \mathrm{diam}(\triangle q_1q_2q_3).
          \]Let $f(N)$ denote the largest possible size of an $N$-avoiding set. Then the original question is equivalent to asking whether $f(N)=O(N)$ holds. For a proof of the equivalence, please see here.

          • Following Koizumi's discrete $N$-avoiding lattice formulation above, I want to record a small observation about the low-side subproblem.

            The theorem I want to state is the following. Let $S$ be a finite subset of $\mathbb{Z}^2$. I will say that $S$ is low-side for $N$ if every triple $a,b,c\in S$ satisfies $\operatorname{area}(\triangle abc)+\operatorname{diam}(\triangle abc)<N$. Let $F_{\mathrm{low}}(N)$ be the maximum possible size of such a set. Then $F_{\mathrm{low}}(N)=(4\pi/\sqrt{27})N+O(\sqrt{N})$.
            Thus, in the low-side part of Koizumi's model, the disk constant $4\pi/\sqrt{27}$ is the sharp asymptotic constant.

            The lower bound is the disk construction. If $S$ consists of lattice points in a disk of radius $r$, then every triangle has area at most $(3\sqrt{3}/4)r^2$ and diameter at most $2r$. Choosing $r$ so that $(3\sqrt{3}/4)r^2+2r<N$ gives $|S|=(4\pi/\sqrt{27})N-O(\sqrt{N})$.

            For the upper bound, let $K=\operatorname{conv}(S)$, let $D=\operatorname{diam}(K)$, and choose a diameter pair $a,b\in S$. Let $W$ be the width of $K$ perpendicular to $ab$. Degenerate collinear cases are immediate, so assume $K$ has positive area.
            Low-side applied to triangles $abv$, where $v$ is a vertex of $K$, gives $Dh(v)/2+D<N$, where $h(v)$ is the distance from $v$ to the line $ab$. In particular $D<N$ by taking a vertex with positive height, and the same inequalities give $W<4(N-D)/D$. Also $W\le D$.
            Let $T$ be the maximum area of a triangle in $K$, and let $d$ be the diameter of such a maximum triangle. Since $K$ is a lattice polygon, this maximum triangle may be chosen with vertices in $S$. The triangle lies in a strip of width $W$, so $T\le dW/2$. Low-side gives $T+d<N$, hence $T<N/(1+2/W)$.
            Now we use the first external theorem. The Blaschke--Sas theorem [1,2] says that every planar convex body $K$ contains a triangle of area at least $(3\sqrt{3}/(4\pi))\operatorname{area}(K)$. Since $T$ is the largest triangle area in $K$, $T\ge (3\sqrt{3}/(4\pi))\operatorname{area}(K)$, i.e. $\operatorname{area}(K)\le (4\pi/\sqrt{27})T$.
            Next we use Pick's theorem. Let $I$ be the number of lattice points in the interior of $K$, and $B$ the number of lattice points on the boundary of $K$. Pick's theorem [3] gives $\operatorname{area}(K)=I+B/2-1$, so $|K\cap\mathbb{Z}^2|=I+B=\operatorname{area}(K)+B/2+1$. Since $B\le \operatorname{perim}(K)$, and $S\subset K\cap\mathbb{Z}^2$, we get $|S|\le \operatorname{area}(K)+\operatorname{perim}(K)/2+1$. Also $K$ is contained in a $D$ by $W$ rectangle, so $\operatorname{perim}(K)\le 2D+2W$ and hence $\operatorname{perim}(K)/2\le D+W$. Therefore $|S|\le (4\pi/\sqrt{27})N/(1+2/W)+D+W+O(1)$.

            It remains to split into two cases. Write $c=4\pi/\sqrt{27}$.
            If $D\le 2\sqrt{N}$, then $W\le D\le 2\sqrt{N}$ and $cN/(1+2/W)\le cN$, so $|S|\le cN+D+W+O(1)\le cN+O(\sqrt{N})$.

            If $D>2\sqrt{N}$, put $W_0=4(N-D)/D$. Then $W<W_0<4N/D<2\sqrt{N}$, so $W=O(\sqrt{N})$. It remains to show $cN/(1+2/W)+D\le cN$. Since $cN/(1+2/W)=cNW/(W+2)$ is increasing in $W$, $\frac{cN}{1+2/W}+D\le \frac{cN}{1+2/W_0}+D=cN\frac{2(N-D)}{2N-D}+D=cN-\frac{cND}{2N-D}+D$.
            As noted above, $D<N$. Also $c>2$, so $cN>2N>2N-D$, and hence $cND/(2N-D)\ge D$. Therefore the last displayed quantity is at most $cN$, and $|S|\le cN+W+O(1)\le cN+O(\sqrt{N})$.

            References.

            [2] Balko, M.; Jelinek, V.; Valtr, P.; Walczak, B.On the Beer Index of Convexity and Its Variants.
            Discrete & Computational Geometry 57 (2017), 179--214 https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00454-016-9821-3

          • I made an attempt at a finite upper-bound scheme for $f(N)$ in Koizumi's discrete $N$-avoiding lattice model for Erdős #352.

            Start with an arbitrary $N$-avoiding set $S$ and choose a diameter pair in $S$. After translation and symmetry, write it as $A=(0,0)$ and $B=(a,b)$, and put $D=|A-B|$. For this fixed diameter vector $B$, the remaining candidates $q$ are finite.
            The first test is the triangle $A,B,q$. A point $q$ survives only if $|\operatorname{area}(A,B,q)-N|>D$. So each surviving $q$ is classified as follows: low if $\operatorname{area}(A,B,q)<N-D$, high if $\operatorname{area}(A,B,q)>N+D$. Points with $N-D\le\operatorname{area}(A,B,q)\le N+D$ are discarded.

            The compatibility graph is built as follows. Its vertices are the remaining candidates $q$. An edge $q_iq_j$ means that $q_i$ and $q_j$ are still allowed to be chosen together with the fixed points.

            In the no-high branch the fixed points are $A,B$, so the pair $q_i,q_j$ is tested together with $A$ and with $B$, for example by checking the triples $A,q_i,q_j$ and $B,q_i,q_j$. In the high-side branch the fixed points are $A,B,h$, so the triple $h,q_i,q_j$ is also checked.

            For this $B$, I calculate two branch bounds:
            no-high branch: let $G_B^0$ be the compatibility graph on the low candidates. This branch gives $2+\chi(G_B^0)$.
            high-side branch: after fixing one high point $h$, let $G_{B,h}$ be the compatibility graph on the remaining survivors. This branch gives $3+\chi(G_{B,h})$.

            This graph calculation is intentionally relaxed: it does not check every triple $q_i,q_j,q_k$ made only from unfixed candidates. Therefore the color count is not claimed to be the exact maximum size of an avoiding set. It is only an upper bound. The reason it is still valid is that every actual avoiding choice gives a clique in the compatibility graph, and $\omega(G)\le\chi(G)$.

            The case bound for this $B$ is the larger of the no-high bound and all high-side bounds. Finally, the global upper bound for $f(N)$ is the maximum over all normalized diameter vectors $B$.

            I checked the implementation for $N=1,\ldots,7$:
            N: 1 2 3 4 5 6 7
            upper bound: 1 4 4 7 9 10 12
            These match the known lower-bound witnesses in these small cases.
            For $N>7$, the current readable implementation became too slow for me to complete the full runs, so I am reporting only these completed checks.

  • Aleksandar Bulj and I made partial progress on this problem of a different nature in this preprint. One can ask for an upper bound on the measure of every set $A$ contained in a large square $[0,R]^2$ that avoids every triple of points spanning a triangle of area 1. Erdős's conjecture is, of course, that it can be $O(1)$, i.e., uniform in $R$. However, while this is still open, one can still concentrate on upper bounds in terms of $R\to\infty$, i.e., prove the so-called "density theorem" in this context, anything better than the trivial $O(R^2)$. Aleksandar and I showed an upper bound of the form\[ O\Big( R^2 \Big(\frac{\log\log R}{\log R}\Big)^{1/2} \Big). \]Namely, we couldn't find any concrete upper bound in the literature. There is only a very weak $o(R^2)$ that can be deduced from a brief discrete comment by Graham, which we also deciphered in our Appendix B.

    Our proof uses quite a lot of tools from harmonic analysis (trilinear smoothing, induction on scales, etc.). It would be interesting to see if a better upper bound can be shown using other ideas. Our paper would survive even if the full Erdős problem was solved, because we were also concerned with a more rigid configuration, where $O(1)$ is not possible (and we get a slightly worse upper bound).

    Was AI used? Yes, ChatGPT 5.5 Pro helped us increase the exponent, from 1/3 to 1/2. It also helped us decipher Graham's comment.
