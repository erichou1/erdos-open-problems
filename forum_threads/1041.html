FALSIFIABLE Open, but could be disproved with a finite counterexample.
Let $f(z)=\prod_{i=1}^n(z-z_i)\in \mathbb{C}[z]$ with $\lvert z_i\rvert < 1$ for all $i$.

Must there always exist a path of length less than $2$ in\[\{z: \lvert f(z)\rvert < 1\}\]which connects two of the roots of $f$?
#1041: [EHP58,p.139]
analysis | polynomials

  • I have proven the quartic case of this problem: if $f$ is monic of degree four and all zeros of $f$, counted with multiplicity, lie in the open unit disk, then two of these zeros can be joined inside
    $$
    {z : |f(z)| < 1}
    $$
    by a possibly degenerate polygonal path of length less than $2$.

    The preprint is available at https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24875. In addition, using the Aristotle AI based API, the result has been formalized and verified in Lean. I'm currently unsure of how exactly to share the Lean files for reproducibility and scrutiny, but if anyone is interested/or knows how to share them, I'm more than willing to post those.

        • I'm willing to rate the argument as being plausibly correct. With regards to having publication-level quality of exposition and literature review, the manuscript still needs a lot of work however, on a number of fronts. Much of this is tied to the tendency of current-level AI writing to dwell far too much on routine results (often without citing relevant literature), or technical details, and not enough time emphasizing the high level structure.

          In this case, the high-level structure is that there are four key results that resolve the Erdos problem, each of which is independently interesting:

          1. The EHP component lemma (at least one component $U$ with two or more zeroes).

          2. An integral bound $\int_U |f'(z)/f(z)|\ dA \leq 2\pi m$, where $m$ is the number of zeroes (Proposition 4).

          3. A lemma asserting that on any given region $U$, there is a tree connecting the $m$ zeroes of $U$ (formed by following the Morse gradient flow on $\log |f|$, basically) of total length at most $\frac{1}{2\pi} \int_U |f'(z)/f(z)|\ dA$, at least assuming some technical non-degeneracy hypotheses on $f$. (Proposition 12)

          4. A simple combinatorial lemma that asserts that any tree contains a path of length at most twice the total length of the tree, divided by the number of vertices of the tree. (Lemma 17)

          There is also a technical

          0. One can perturb $f$ to obey the non-degeneracy conditions required for the third ingredient to work. (Lemma 14)


          Combining all these ingredients, the Erdos problem follows. But the AI-generated exposition spends a lot of time on the least interesting parts of this process (e.g., Step 0), and many steps could be proven much more quickly. For instance Lemma 3 is obvious because the level sets of $p$ are algebraic sets of dimension less than 2, and thus immediately of measure zero (e.g., by the Crofton formula). Several pages of argument are used to establish the formula $\int_{\varepsilon < u < t} |\nabla u|^2\ dA = 2\pi m(t-\varepsilon)$ on page 5, but this is just the area formula noting that $f$ is $m$-to-one on $U$, so (a suitable branch of) $\log f$ is also $m$-to-one mapping to a fundamental domain of $\{ \varepsilon < \mathrm{Re} z < t \}$.

          I also suspect that there is a less convoluted way to arrive at Proposition 4 from this area formula and the Polya inequality than the one provided in the AI-generated text, although I have not tried too hard to locate such a simplified argument. Similarly, the perturbative component of the argument (Step 0) seems to occupy far more space in the AI-generated document than it needs to: I think the cleanest thing to do is first resolve the Erdos problem for "generic" polynomials f (perhaps with some sort of "padding") and use some sort of Rouche theorem argument to deduce the general case from this, instead of spend so much time constructing explicit perturbations of polynomials.

          • After working with Claude and doing some literature search, I have a shorter proof of Proposition 4 (Step 2). From the area formula (noting that $f$ is an $m$-fold map from $U$ to the disk) one has
            $$ \int_U |f'(z)|^2 / |f(z)|^{2-1/m}\ dA(z) = m \int_{D(0,1)} 1/|w|^{2-1/m}\ dA(w) = 2 \pi m^2$$
            so by Cauchy--Schwarz it suffices to show that
            $$ \int_U |f(z)|^{-1/m}\ dA(z) \leq 2\pi.$$
            By the layer-cake decomposition it suffices to show that the level sets $\{ z \in U: |f(z)| \leq s \}$ have area at most $\pi s^{2/m}$ for all $0 < s \leq 1$. By Theorem 1 of this paper of Dubinin (a Polya type inequality), this area is at most $s^{2/m}$ times the area of $U$; and by the classical Polya inequality, $U$ has area at most $\pi$. The claim follows.

            The arguments used here and in the AI-generated article are also rather similar to those in this paper of Crane.

            I'm going to look at Proposition 12 (Step 3) next. I have a suspicion that one does not need quite so much working out of the topology of the situation, and should be able to proceed by more purely analytic arguments; but I have not digested it yet.

          • Sendov's Conjecture is interesting. Having in mind the "musical chair"
            picture for roots and critical points of polynomial $f$ one can look at
            the following variant of Sendov's:

            Let be $n \ge 1$. Let $f$ be a polynomial of degree n with zeros $z_1, ..., z_n$,
            all in the complex unit ball. Let $f'$ be the derivative (thus with degree $n-1$)
            with roots $y_1$, ..., $y_{n-1}$. Add the auxiliary point $y_n = 0$.

            ** Conjecture: Then there exists a permutation $p$ of {1, 2, ..., n}
            such that distance $(x_i, y_{p(i)}) \le 1$ for $i=1, ..., n$. The
            condition for $i=n$ holds trivially, because $0$ is central in the
            unit ball. The interesting remaining cases are $i=1, ..., n-1$. **

            The conjecture is in some sense weaker than Sendov's,
            because $y_n$ gives a free meal for one root of $f$. But it is
            stronger than Sendov's in another sense, because all the
            other roots of $f$ need different near-distance $f'$-roots as
            their partners.

            For $n=3$ the conjecture has a simple proof by Hall's
            marriage theorem.
