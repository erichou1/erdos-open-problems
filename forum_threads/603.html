Let $(A_i)$ be a family of countably infinite sets such that $\lvert A_i\cap A_j\rvert \neq 2$ for all $i\neq j$. Find the smallest cardinal $C$ such that $\cup A_i$ can always be coloured with at most $C$ colours so that no $A_i$ is monochromatic.
#603: [Er87]
combinatorics | set theory
A problem of Komjáth. If instead we have $\lvert A_i\cap A_j\rvert \neq 1$ then Komjáth showed that this is possible with at most $\aleph_0$ colours.

GPT 5.4 Pro (prompted by Chojecki) proved there is no uniform bound on such $C$: for every cardinal $C$ there is a family of countably infinite sets $(A_i)$ such that $\lvert A_i\cap A_j\rvert \neq 2$ for all $i\neq j$, and in any colouring of $\cup A_i$ with $C$ colours some $A_i$ is monochromatic.

Indeed, by the Erdős-Rado theorem, if $\kappa$ is the successor of $2^C$, any $C$-colouring of the edges of the complete graph on $\kappa$ vertices contains a monochromatic countably infinite complete graph, and hence we can take $(A_i)$ to be the collection of all countably infinite complete graphs (since any two such intersect in either countably infinite or $\binom{n}{2}$ many edges for some finite $n$, and in particular never intersect exactly twice).

    • I wonder if it would be interesting to study a quantitative version of this question?

      Say that $\mathcal{A}$ is $(k, \lambda, \mu)$-family if $\chi(\mathcal{A})>\mu$, $|A|=\lambda$ for all $A\in \mathcal{A}$, and $|A\cap B|\neq k$ for all distinct $A,B\in \mathcal{A}$. Given an integer $k\geq 2$ and infinite cardinals $\lambda, \mu$, let $f(k, \lambda, \mu)=\min\{|\bigcup_{A\in \mathcal{A}}A|: \mathcal{A} \text{ is a $(k, \lambda, \mu)$-family}\}$ and let $g(k, \lambda, \mu)=\min\{|\mathcal{A}|: \mathcal{A} \text{ is a $(k, \lambda, \mu)$-family}\}$. Now the question becomes estimating the values of $f$ and $g$. From the original problem, it feels like bounding the number of sets in the family is most natural (the function $g$), but given your proof, it feels most natural to bound the size of the ground set (the function $f$).

      In this language, you proved that $f(2, \omega, \mu) \leq (2^\mu)^+$ and $g(2, \omega, \mu) \leq ((2^\mu)^+)^{\omega}$. Or if you generalize in the ways I mentioned, you get $f(k, \mu^+, \mu) \leq \beth_{k-1}(\mu)^+$ and $g(k, \mu^+, \mu) \leq (\beth_{k-1}(\mu)^+)^{\mu^+}$.

      Here is the construction in full: let $\kappa$ be the successor of $2^C$, so that by the Erdős-Rado theorem in any $C$-colouring of the edges on the complete graph with $\kappa$ vertices there is a monochromatic countably infinite complete graph.

  • I have made a Lean 4 / Mathlib formalization attempt repository for Problem 603.
    The repository is based on Przemek Chojecki's PDF and on the discussion here. It contains formalization attempts for two mathematical interpretations of the problem.

    The first interpretation is the countable-sequence reading: given a countable sequence of infinite sets, find a finite coloring such that no set in the sequence is monochromatic. The Lean4Web version for this interpretation is here: Erdos603CountableSequenceLean4Web.lean

    The second interpretation is the arbitrary-size-family reading: construct arbitrarily large families of countably infinite sets whose pairwise intersections never have size 2, but for which every coloring with a prescribed number of colors has a monochromatic member. The Lean4Web version for this interpretation is here: Erdos603ArbitrarySizeConditionalLean4Web.lean
    The second formalization is conditional: it assumes the relevant Erdős--Rado / partition-relation input, represented in Lean as ArrowOmegaTwo κ μ. Thus it formalizes the construction from that hypothesis, but it is not yet a Lean proof of Erdős--Rado itself.

  • I wrote up a short note on a strengthening of this problem:

    https://gist.github.com/gavinsherry/ad9b6f85e2afc0a830d015a6b5d6d52a

    The main finite-color statement is the following.

    For every integer $r\geq 1$, there is a family $\mathcal F_r$ of countably infinite subsets of a countable ground set $X$ such that:

    1. for distinct $A,B\in\mathcal F_r$, the intersection $A\cap B$ is either empty or countably infinite, hence
    in particular $|A\cap B|\neq 2$;

    2. every $r$-coloring of $X$ makes some member of $\mathcal F_r$ monochromatic.

    Here is the construction. Let $N=R_r(3)$ and let $P=[N]^2$ be the edge set of $K_N$. Fix a nonprincipal ultrafilter $\mathcal U$ on $\omega$, and set $X=P\times\omega$.

    For each triangle $T\in [N]^3$ and each choice of sets $B_p\in\mathcal U$ for $p\in [T]^2$, define

    $$
    A(T,(B_p))=\bigcup_{p\in [T]^2}(\{p\}\times B_p).
    $$

    Let $\mathcal F_r$ be the family of all such sets.

    Each member of $\mathcal F_r$ is countably infinite. If two triangles are distinct, they share at most one edge. Since $\mathcal U$ is nonprincipal and closed under finite intersections, any nonempty intersection of two members of $\mathcal F_r$ is countably infinite. Thus all pairwise intersections have size either $0$ or $\aleph_0$, never $2$.

    Now take any $r$-coloring of $X$. For each $p\in P$, exactly one color class in the fibre $\{p\}\times\omega$ belongs to $\mathcal U$; call this color $\gamma(p)$. This gives an $r$-edge-coloring of $K_N$. By the definition of $R_r(3)$, there is a triangle $T$ whose three edges have the same $\gamma$-color, say $\alpha$.

    For each $p\in[T]^2$, set

    $$
    B_p=\{n\in\omega:(p,n)\text{ has color }\alpha\}.
    $$

    Then $B_p\in\mathcal U$ for all $p\in[T]^2$, so $A(T,(B_p))\in\mathcal F_r$, and this set is monochromatic of color $\alpha$.

    Thus even on a countable ground set, and even with the stronger condition that pairwise intersections are only $0$ or $\aleph_0$, the finite chromatic number is unbounded.

    This writeup was prepared with AI assistance and checked before posting.

    • I think I must be missing something regarding your example. Isn't the following a much simpler example of the thing you are trying to prove?

      Theorem: There exists a hypergraph $H=(V,E)$ such that $|V|=\omega=\chi(H)$ and for all $e,f\in E$, $|e\cap f|=\omega$.

      Proof: Let $\mathcal{U}$ be a non-principle ultrafilter on $\omega$ and let $H=(\omega, \mathcal{U})$. Note that since $\mathcal{U}$ is an ultrafilter, we have that for all $e,f\in \mathcal{U}, |e\cap f|=\omega$. Now let $r\in \omega$ with $r\geq 1$ and consider $c:\omega\to [r]$. Since $\mathcal{U}$ is an ultrafilter, there exists $i\in [r]$ such that $c^{-1}(i)\in \mathcal{U}$; i.e. some edge is monochromatic. Thus $\chi(H)>r$ for all $r\in \omega$.
