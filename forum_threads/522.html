Let $f(z)=\sum_{0\leq k\leq n} \epsilon_k z^k$ be a random polynomial, where $\epsilon_k\in \{-1,1\}$ independently uniformly at random for $0\leq k\leq n$.

Is it true that, if $R_n$ is the number of roots of $f(z)$ in $\{ z\in \mathbb{C} : \lvert z\rvert \leq 1\}$, then\[\frac{R_n}{n/2}\to 1\]almost surely?
#522: [Er61,p.252]
analysis | polynomials | probability

Random polynomials with independently identically distributed coefficients are sometimes called Kac polynomials - this problem considers the case of Rademacher coefficients, i.e. independent uniform $\pm 1$ values. Erdős and Offord [EO56] showed that the number of real roots of a random degree $n$ polynomial with $\pm 1$ coefficients is $(\frac{2}{\pi}+o(1))\log n$.

There is some ambiguity whether Erdős intended the coefficients to be in $\{-1,1\}$ or $\{0,1\}$ - see the comments section.

A weaker version of this was solved by Yakir [Ya21], who proved that\[\frac{R_n}{n/2}\to 1\]in probability. (This weaker claim was also asked by Erdős, and also appears in a book of Hayman [Ha67].) More precisely,\[\lim_{n\to \infty} \mathbb{P}(\lvert R_n-n/2\rvert \geq n^{9/10}) =0.\]See also [521].

    Very briefly, we establish a uniform fourth-moment concentration estimate for Littlewood polynomials. The argument combines anti-concentration for Rademacher sums, control of small values, a smoothing step, and tools such as Pisier’s inequality and results of Nazarov–Nishry–Sodin. It appears to yield a quantitative almost-sure result with a convergence rate on the order of $n^{7/8+\delta}$ for any $\delta>0$, which I found noteworthy, though this may well not be optimal.   • I've run remarks of Vjeko Kovac and Brayden Letwin through GPT-5.4 Pro and it came up with a claim for a complete solution. There are many analytic estimates, so I'll probably try to formalize it too.

    The proof combines three ingredients. First, Jensen's formula reduces the root count to logarithmic integrals at radii $1$ and $1-n^{-1-\alpha}$, with an elementary reciprocal-polynomial squeeze handling the closed disk exactly. Second, a logarithmic-integrability theorem of Nazarov, Nishry and Sodin controls the singularity of $\log |P_n|$ near zero. Third, a quantitative central-limit argument for angle averages of the normalized polynomial, in the spirit of Angst and Poly, yields almost-sure convergence of suitable smooth truncations of the logarithmic integral.

  • Here are several rather obvious remarks, partly in analogy with problem 521, and produced before thinking about the problem. :-)

    1) I don't think that this problem should be marked as solved, unless another reference is found. Namely, Erdős was precise about what he meant (p.252), and he meant "a strong law", i.e., almost sure convergence of a sequence of random variables:\[ \frac{R'_n}{n/2} \to 1 \ a.s., \]where $R'_n$ counts the number of roots of $\sum_{k=0}^{n}\epsilon_k z^k$ in $|z|\prec 1$ (even though the closed disk $|z|\leq 1$ doesn't make a big difference).
    Yakir's paper proves\[ \frac{R'_n}{n/2} \to 1 \ \text{in probability}, \]which is also commonly phrased (in the spirit of your formulation) as\[ R'_n = \Big(\frac{1}{2}+o(1)\Big) n \quad\text{with large probability} \]and it is a weaker claim, except for the concrete error of order $O(n^{9/10})$.

    2) On the other hand, it is not clear if Erdős meant the coefficients to be $-1,1$ valued coin tosses (i.e., the Rademacher variables) as he emphasizes in the beginning of Section 2) on the same page, or rather the $0,1$ valued coin tosses (i.e., the Bernoulli variables). Strictly speaking he wrote the latter (just as in problem 521), since he constructed $\epsilon_k$ as the $k$-th digit of $t$ taken from the sample space $[0,1)$, by writing $t=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{\epsilon_k(t)}{2^k}$. However, he might have had some identification on mind, or wanted to write $t=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{\epsilon_k(t)+1}{2^{k+1}}$ instead. I am more inclined to think that he meant $-1,1$.

    3) In both cases the a.s. claim seems experimentally quite plausible. An easy simulation shows that in the $-1,1$ case a typical trajectory of $R'_n/(n/2)$ looks like this, while in the $0,1$ case it typically looks like this. In both cases it seems that convergence should in fact hold almost surely as well.

    4) For the source of the problem Yakir actually cites Hayman's book from 1967. Erdős did in fact ask (p.252) about the weaker claim as well, as you can see in the sentence beginning with "Here I cannot even prove that...".
