Let $A(n)=\{a_0<a_1<\cdots\}$ be the sequence defined by $a_0=0$ and $a_1=n$, and for $k\geq 1$ define $a_{k+1}$ as the least positive integer such that there is no three-term arithmetic progression in $\{a_0,\ldots,a_{k+1}\}$.

Can the $a_k$ be explicitly determined? How fast do they grow?
#271: [ErGr80,p.22]
additive combinatorics | arithmetic progressions

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
It is easy to see that $A(1)$ is the set of integers which have no 2 in their base 3 expansion. Odlyzko and Stanley [OdSt78] have found similar characterisations are known for $A(3^k)$ and $A(2\cdot 3^k)$ for any $k\geq 0$ and conjectured in general that such a sequence always eventually either satisfies\[a_k\asymp k^{\log_23}\]or\[a_k \asymp \frac{k^2}{\log k}.\]There is no known sequence which satisfies the second growth rate, but Lindhurst [Li90] gives data which suggests that $A(4)$ has such growth ($A(4)$ is given as A005487 in the OEIS).

Moy [Mo11] has proved that, for all such sequences, for all $\epsilon>0$, $a_k\leq (\frac{1}{2}+\epsilon)k^2$ for all sufficiently large $k$. van Doorn and Sothanaphan have noted in the comment section that Moy's proof can be upgraded to give a fully explicit result of\[a_k\leq \frac{(k-1)(k+2)}{2}+n\]for all $k\geq 0$.

In general, sequences which begin with some initial segment and thereafter are continued in a greedy fashion to avoid three-term arithmetic progressions are known as Stanley sequences.

  • I have computed the sequence $A(4) = S(0,4)$ up to $k=2000$ to test the growth dichotomy numerically.

    Results:
    * $a_{2000} = 259,183$
    * Ratio $a_k / (k^2/2) \approx 0.13$ (Satisfies the unconditional upper bound)

    More importantly, the data supports the original Odlyzko-Stanley conjecture for Type 2 sequences ($a_k \asymp k^2 / \log k$). Specifically, the data fits the curve $a_k \approx \frac{k^2}{2 \ln k}$ remarkably well:
    * Predicted value at $k=2000$ (using $\frac{k^2}{2 \ln k}$): $\approx 262,880$
    * Actual value: $259,183$
    * Ratio: $0.986$

    This provides strong evidence that $A(4)$ is indeed chaotic and does not approach the $k^{\log_2 3}$ growth of regular sequences.

    * A003278: The Szekeres sequence ($S(1)$), which is equivalent to $A(1)$ shifted by +1.

  • I haven't fully checked the paper by Moy (even though it's short and elementary), but we can actually eliminate the $\epsilon$ in their result and make the 'sufficiently large $k$' explicit.

    To see this, asssume $k > 2n$ and define $x = \frac{1}{2}k^2$. Now assume by contradiction $a_k > x$, or equivalently in the notation of Moy, assume $S(\{n\}, x) \le k-1$. Then their Lemma 2.4 gives $x \le \frac{1}{2}k(k-1) + n = x - \frac{1}{2}k + n < x$; contradiction. We therefore deduce $S(\{n\}, x) \ge k$. Translated back into the notation of this page, this gives $a_k \le \frac{1}{2}k^2$ for all $k > 2n$.

    • Slight correction: the $k$th element in this page’s notation is $a_{k-1}$, so actually $a_{k-1} \leq k^2/2$ for $k \geq 2n+1$, or $a_k \leq (k+1)^2/2$ for $k \geq 2n$.

      But actually we can “optimize” to the max as follows.

      In the proof of [Moy]’s Lemma 2.2, since $n = 2a_j - a_i \geq a_j + 1$, we can replace “less than or equal to $x$” with “less than or equal to $x-1$”. This means the statement of Lemma 2.2 can replace $S(A,x)$ with $S(A,x-1)$.

      In the statement of Lemma 2.3, we can replace $\max A$ with $(\max A - |A| + 1)$ by simple counting.

      Then, in Lemma 2.4, if we assume further that $x$ is integer, we can get rid of the bound $\lfloor x\rfloor + 1 \geq x$. Combined with the improved Lemma 2.2 and Lemma 2.3, the statement of Lemma 2.4 changes to:
      $$ x \leq S(A,x-1)(S(A,x-1) - 1)/2 + S(A,x) + \max A - |A| $$
      for integer $x$.

      Now set $x = a_k$. Recall Moy’s notation: $S(A,y) = \# \{ a_i \leq y \}$. Here $A = \{0, n\}$. We arrive at the bound
      $$ a_k \leq k(k-1)/2 + (k+1) + n - 2. $$
      So unconditionally:
      $$ a_k \leq (k-1)(k+2)/2 + n. $$

      (Sanity check: $a_0 \leq n-1$, $a_1 \leq n$, $a_2 \leq n+2$ all make sense and can be tight if $n = 1$.)

  • I wrote a little generator for the Stanley sequence $S(0,n)$, hopefully correct.
    It's checked against the OEIS prefix for $S(0,4)$ (A005487) and it matches the first 50 terms.

    Definition:\[
    a_0 = 0, \quad a_1 = n, \quad
    a_{k+1} = \min\{ m > a_k : \{a_0,\dots,a_k,m\}\ \text{has no 3-term arithmetic progression}\}.
    \]Since $a_{k+1}$ is always the new maximum, the code just tests each candidate $m$ by checking whether\[
    2y - m \in S \quad \text{or} \quad \tfrac{m+y}{2} \in S \quad (m+y \ \text{even}),
    \]for some $y \in S$.
