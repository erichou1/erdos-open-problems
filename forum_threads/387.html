Is there an absolute constant $c>0$ such that, for all $1\leq k< n$, the binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k}$ has a divisor in $(cn,n]$?
#387: [ErGr76b,p.351][Er78g,p.315][ErGr80,p.74]
number theory | binomial coefficients
Erdős once conjectured that $\binom{n}{k}$ must always have a divisor in $(n-k,n]$, but this was disproved by Schinzel and Erdős [Sc58]. A counterexample is given by $n=99215$ and $k=15$. Schinzel conjectured (see problem B34 of [Gu04]) that, for all sufficiently large $k$ which are not prime powers, there exists an $n$ such that $\binom{n}{k}$ is not divisible by any integer in $(n-k,n]$.

In [Er78g] Erdős thought that this question had a negative answer.

It is easy to see that $\binom{n}{k}$ always has a divisor in $[n/k,n]$.

Faulkner [Fa66] proved that, if $p$ is the least prime $>2k$ and $n\geq p$, then $\binom{n}{k}$ has a prime divisor $\geq p$ (except $\binom{9}{2}$ and $\binom{10}{3}$).

This is discussed in problems B33 and B34 of Guy's collection [Gu04], which says that Erdős conjectured this is true for any $c<1$ (if $n$ is sufficiently large).

This was answered negatively for all constant $c>0$ by Bui, Naprienko, Pratt, and Zaharascu [BNPZ26], who prove the existence of infinitely many $\binom{n}{k}$, with $k\asymp \sqrt{\log\log n}$, such that $\binom{n}{k}$ has no divisors in the interval\[\big(\frac{\log\log\log\log n}{\log\log\log n}n, n\big].\]On the other hand, for $k$ large (say $\exp((\log n)^{2/3+\epsilon})\leq k\leq n/2$), they prove in [BNPZ26] that the answer is yes for all $c<1$.

  • Using ChatGPT, I found a second reference where this problem is mentioned: page 351 of P. Erdős, R. L. Graham: On the prime factors of ${n\choose k}$, Fibonacci Quart. 14 (1976) no. 4, 348--352. There are various references establishing the conjecture for almost all $n$ for a fixed $k$, mentioned in the ChatGPT response. However, I actually think the answer to this question is negative if one fixes $k$ and chooses $n$ carefully, and matters seem to reduce to a covering congruence problem (of a type similar to that seen in the "large gaps between primes"/"Jacobsthal function" literature). Namely, it seems the problem morally reduces to:

    Conjecture 1: For any $m$, there exists $k > m$ such that one can cover $\{1,\dots,k\}$ by congruences $a_p \pmod{p}$ for $m \leq p \leq k$, such that $0 \leq a_p < p - (k \% p)$ for all $m \leq p < k$, where $k\% p \in \{0,\dots,p-1\}$ denotes the remainder of $k$ when dividing by $p$.

    Indeed, suppose that this conjecture held. Pick any $m$, let $k$ be as in the conjecture, and then pick a large $n$ with $n = k- a_p \pmod{p^j}$ whenever $p^j \leq k$, with the convention that $a_p=0$ for $p < m$. By Kummer's theorem, $\binom{n}{k}$ is not divisible by any prime $p$ less than or equal to $k$, so one can express
    $$ \binom{n}{k} = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{n-j}{b_j}$$
    where $b_j$ is the product of all the prime powers $p^i$, $p \leq k$ that divide $n-j$, which is some number that depends on $k$ and the $a_p$ but does not otherwise depend on $n$. Because of the covering hypothesis, each of the $b_j$ is divisible by at least one prime $m \leq p \leq k$, and so in particular $b_j \geq m$ and $\frac{n-j}{b_j} < \frac{n}{m}$.

    With further sieving, one can find $n$ in say $[x,2x]$ for a large $x$ such that the integers $(n-j)/b_j$ are in fact coprime to all primes less than say $x^{\varepsilon_k}$ for some $\varepsilon_k > 0$ depending only on $k$. So now the $(n-j)/b_j$, in addition to being bounded by $n/m$, are all almost primes with only $O_k(1)$ prime factors. With luck, a suitable counting argument should show that for most such $x$, the products of the factors of these almost primes all avoid the interval $(n/m,n]$, and so none of the factors of $\binom{n}{k}$ will lie in this interval; as $m$ is arbitrary, this would contradict the claim.

    So the task now splits into two subtasks: firstly, to prove the conjecture, and secondly, to work out the sieve theory step indicated above.

    • I had an interesting conversation with ChatGPT about Conjecture 1 and beyond - link shared. Basically it showed how to arrive at slightly weakened Conjecture 1 using prime-gap machinery, using Maynard + yours results. Then it sketched what remains to be proved. I share the full conversation and an interesting part below. The conversation is in 3 messages:

      - first message is a summary and potential attack strategies
      - second message is on Weak Conjecture 1
      - third message is on attacking the full Conjecture 1.


      From GPT-5.2:

      The Maynard / Ford--et al.\ prime-gap machinery can be viewed as giving \textbf{existence of coverings with forbidden residue sets} provided you can still choose residue classes with decent "hitting power". In your setting, the forbidden set for prime $p$ has size $k \bmod p$, so the allowed set has size $p - (k \bmod p)$.

      A natural weakened target that matches what large-gap constructions already tolerate is:

      Weakened Conjecture 1$\star$ (allow $o(k)$ survivors):
      For every $m$, there exists $k > m$ and choices $r_p \in [k \bmod p, \dots, p-1]$ for primes $m \le p \le k$ such that \textbf{all but $o(k)$} integers $1 \le j \le k$ satisfy $j \equiv r_p \pmod p$ for some such prime $p$.

      Why this is the right weakening:
      Maynard explicitly notes that in the prime-gap construction it is ''permissible'' to leave a controlled number of survivors and then eliminate them later.

      In your binomial application, leaving $o(k)$ ''bad'' indices $j$ (where you don't force a prime $\ge m$ into $b_j$) might still be salvageable: you'd get a product of $(k - o(k))$ factors $\le n/m$ plus a small exceptional set of larger factors, and then you can try to keep divisors out of $(n/m, n]$ by controlling how many ''large'' factors exist.

      What you would need to import from prime-gap land? You'd want a ''covering lemma with constraints'' of this kind:

      Given a set $A \subseteq \{1, \dots, k\}$ of remaining uncovered points, and a prime $p$, among the \textbf{allowed} residue classes $r \in [k \bmod p, \dots, p-1]$, there exists one that hits at least $\approx |A| / (p - (k \bmod p))$ points of $A$.

      Then a greedy sieve over primes $p \in [m, k]$ would shrink the uncovered set by a multiplicative factor roughly\[
      \prod_{m \le p \le k} \left( 1 - \frac{1}{p - (k \bmod p)} \right),
      \]which heuristically behaves like a power of $\log m / \log k$. This is exactly the kind of multiplicative shrinkage analysis that underlies ''greedy sieve'' discussions in the prime-gap literature and expositions.

  • The stronger conjecture is false (in particularly $c\geq 1/2$ is not good) if the prime tuples (Dickson's) conjecture on linear forms is true. Details: let $n=360360m+99215$ and $k=15$ then easily:
    $\binom{n}{k}=(72072m+19843)(180180m+49607)(120120m+33071)(90090m+24803)\\
    (51480m+14173)(12012m+3307)(32760m+9019)(45045m+12401)(40040m+11023)\\
    (180180m+49603)(72072m+19841)(4290m+1181)(27720m+7631)(180180m+49601)\\
    (120120m+33067)$

    Easily these $15$ linear forms are admissible, so if the prime tuples conjecture on linear forms is true, then for infinitely many $m$ integers $\binom{360360m+99215}{15}$ is the product of $15$ prime factors, the smallest is $\frac{n-11}{84}$, the largest is $\frac{n-1}{2}$, giving that these factors are smaller than $\frac{n}{2}$, and the product of at least two of them is way larger than $n$, hence it has no divisor in the $(\frac{n}{2},n]$ interval.

    To see this in practice for one $m$ value, the smallest solution is $m=494660932170922548$, found by wheel sieve+prp test+true primality tests in some ten days of computation. That is $\binom{n}{k}$ has no divisor in the $(\frac{n}{2},n]$ interval for $n=178256013517113649496495,k=15$. Ofcourse we'd need this for infinitely many $m$ to refute $c=\frac{1}{2}$. Btw $k=15$ is the smallest $k$ value for that this type of construction works if we need $k$ prime factors for some fixed $c<1$.

    If Dickson's conjecture is true, then there are $a(k)\geq 0,b(k)>0$ integers for that there is a divisor in the $[f(k),n]=[\frac{n-a(k)}{b(k)},n]$ interval for large $n$, and this is sharp, so it is possible that $(\frac{n-a(k)}{b(k)},n]$ interval already contains no divisor. (and ofcourse this $[f(k),n]$ interval also contains a divisor if some numbers is not prime).

    Furthermore: there is a divisor in the $[\frac{n-k+1}{\sqrt[k]{k!}},n]$ interval, and in lots case this is slightly better than the above $[\frac{n}{k},n]$ interval, since for "large" fixed $k$ as $n$ goes to infinity we have $\frac{n-k+1}{\sqrt[k]{k!}}$ is roughly $\frac{en}{k}$.

    To see this better bound, observe that:
    $\binom{n}{k}=\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}\frac{n-i}{d_{i}}$, where $\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}d_{i}=k!$. From this $\min(d_{i})\leq \sqrt[k]{k!}$ (this is also an Erdos problem, and it is close to optimal), giving that for some $i$ index:
    $n\geq \frac{n-i}{d_{i}}\geq \frac{n-k+1}{\sqrt[k]{k!}}$.
