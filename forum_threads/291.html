Let $n\geq 1$ and define $L_n$ to be the least common multiple of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$ and $a_n$ by\[\sum_{1\leq k\leq n}\frac{1}{k}=\frac{a_n}{L_n}.\]Is it true that $(a_n,L_n)=1$ and $(a_n,L_n)>1$ both occur for infinitely many $n$?
#291: [ErGr80,p.34]

Steinerberger has observed that the answer to the second question is trivially yes: for example, any $n$ which begins with a $2$ in base $3$ has $3\mid (a_n,L_n)$.

More generally, if the leading digit of $n$ in base $p$ is $p-1$ then $p\mid (a_n,L_n)$. There is in fact a necessary and sufficient condition: a prime $p\leq n$ divides $(a_n,L_n)$ if and only if $p$ divides the numerator of $1+\cdots+\frac{1}{k}$, where $k$ is the leading digit of $n$ in base $p$. This can be seen by writing\[a_n = \frac{L_n}{1}+\cdots+\frac{L_n}{n}\]and observing that the right-hand side is congruent to $1+\cdots+1/k$ modulo $p$. (The previous claim about $p-1$ follows immediately from Wolstenholme's theorem.)

This leads to a heuristic prediction (see for example a preprint of Shiu [Sh16]) of $\asymp\frac{x}{\log x}$ for the number of $n\in [1,x]$ such that $(a_n,L_n)=1$. In particular, there should be infinitely many $n$, but the set of such $n$ should have density zero. Unfortunately this heuristic is difficult to turn into a proof.

Wu and Yan [WuYa22] have proved, conditional on $\frac{1}{\log p}$ being linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$ for any finite collection of primes $p$ (itself a consequence of Schanuel's conjecture), that the set of $n$ for which $(a_n,L_n)>1$ has upper density $1$.

  • Let $v_n$ be the denominator of the harmonic sum $\frac{1}{1} + \frac{1}{2} + \ldots + \frac{1}{n}$, and let $a_n$ and $L_n$ be defined as above. Then we note that #290 and #291 are related by the fact that the inequality $v_n < v_{n-1}$ implies $\gcd(a_n, L_n) > 1$. Back in 2010, Ernie Croot asked: what if we instead look at $\frac{r_1}{1} + \frac{r_2}{2} + \ldots + \frac{r_n}{n}$, with $r_i \in \{-1, 1\}$ or even $r_i \in A$ for some finite set of non-zero integers $A$? Can we still conclude that inequalities like $v_n < v_{n-1}$ and $\gcd(a_n, L_n) > 1$ hold infinitely often? It's these types of generalizations I would like to comment on.

    Now, starting with the bad news, this more general case no longer works; it is possible to choose the $r_i \in \{-1, 1\}$ in such a way that (with the appropriate definitions) $v_n = L_n$ (i.e. $\gcd(a_n, L_n) = 1$) holds for all $n$.

    All is not lost, however. As long as we add the extra condition that the $r_i$ are periodic, then one can find infinitely many $n$ for which $v_n < v_{n-1}$, and hence $\gcd(a_n, L_n) > 1$. That is, this more general case of the second part of this problem is true, assuming that the sequence of numerators is periodic.

    In order to prove that there are infinitely many $n$ with $v_n < v_{n-1}$, one needs to find large prime divisors of $a_n$. But as it turns out, by itself such a result does hold more generally, even in the non-periodic case.

    Theorem.
    Let $r_1, r_2, \ldots$ be a bounded sequence of non-zero integers, and let $m$ be an arbitrary integer larger than $\max_i |r_i|$. With $L_n$ the least common multiple of $1, 2, \ldots, n$, let $a_n$ be such that $$\frac{a_n}{L_n} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{r_i}{i}.$$Then there exists an $n = e^{O(m)}$ such that $a_n$ is divisible by a prime larger than $m$.

    Under the assumption of two prime number theorem type results that will presumably eventually follow from the PNT+ Project, thanks to Aristotle I have formalized a proof of this theorem. For the moment you can type-check it online here. A human-readable proof based on [vD24] can be found here.

    Bringing the discussion back in the general direction of the problem at hand, assume now that the $r_i$ are periodic with period $t$ (so that $r_i = r_{i+t}$ for all $i$), and let $p > \max(|r_1|, |r_2|, \ldots, |r_t|, t)$ be an arbitrarily large prime divisor of $a_n$. By the theorem above, such $p$ and $n$ do exist. By the periodicity of the $r_i$, one can then check that $p$ also divides $a_{n'}$ where $n'= np^{\varphi(t)}$. Hence, as a corollary we obtain the following generalization of the (second part of the) original problem:

    Corollary.
    Let $r_1, r_2, \ldots$ be a periodic sequence of non-zero integers, let $L_n$ be the least common multiple of $1, 2, \ldots, n$, and let $a_n$ be such that $$\frac{a_n}{L_n} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{r_i}{i}.$$Then we have $\limsup \gcd(a_n, L_n) = \infty$. In particular, there are infinitely many $n$ for which $\gcd(a_n, L_n) > 1$.

    A proof of this corollary is also included in the formalization I linked. I would like to eventually extend this even further and formalize the entirety of [vD24], but that could take a while.

  • Let $n$ be fixed. For a prime $p \le n$, let $k = k(p) < p$ be such that $$kp^{\lfloor \frac{\log n}{\log p} \rfloor} \le n < (k+1)p^{\lfloor \frac{\log n}{\log p} \rfloor}. \hspace{20pt} (1)$$That is, $k$ is the leading digit of $n$ in base $p$. As you allude to, in order for $\gcd(a_n, L_n)$ to be equal to $1$, we need $p \nmid a_{k}$ for all $p \le n$. The very rough intuition (e.g. ignoring Wolstenholme's theorem) is that $p \nmid a_k$ happens $1 - \frac{1}{p}$ of the time on average, so heuristically you would expect that the probability that $p \nmid a_k$ happens for all $p \le n$ (and hence $\gcd(a_n, L_n) = 1$) is equal to $\prod_{p \le n} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right)$, which by Mertens' third theorem is about $\frac{c}{\log n}$.

    To get ahold of this problem, it makes sense to try to bound the number of positive integers $k < p$ such that $p \mid a_k$. About 10 years ago I proved that for all primes $p$ we have an upper bound of $cp^{2/3}$ with $c = \frac{3^{2/3}}{2} \approx 1.04$ for the cardinality of this set of $k$, and around the same time Wu and Chen independently proved this exact same result, in Lemma 2.4 of their paper On certain properties of harmonic numbers that you can find here. If (way) better upper bounds on the cardinality of this set of $k$ are available, then maybe one can use that to get bounds on the number of $n$ for which $\gcd(a_n, L_n) = 1$.

    If we look at inequalities like $(1)$ for different primes at the same time and one takes the logarithm, one would like to prove that the resulting inequalities have solutions by applying Kronecker's theorem. In order to do this however, you need the corresponding logarithmic terms to be rationally independent, and this can be a major roadblock. See e.g. this question I posted on MathOverflow back in 2011 (for which I had applications like #290 and #291 in mind), which has remained unanswered ever since.

    To cut to the chase: if we allow ourselves to assume e.g. Schanuel's conjecture, then we are in a better spot to make progress. For example, we can then prove that the upper density of $n$ for which $\gcd(a_n, L_n) = 1$ is equal to $1$. This was shown by Wu and Yan in their paper On the denominators of harmonic numbers. IV that you can find here, although these ideas were present in the referenced paper by Shiu too, as well as this paper of myself.

    • In fact this problem of the rational independence of $1/\log p$ also came up recently in work of myself and Croot, building on earlier work of Croot, Mousavi, and Schmidt. In that application (which is also concerned with the behaviour of digits in multiple bases simultaneously) it would have also been useful to have independence of $1/\log p$, which (of course) we could not prove.

      For that application though there is a nice trick that lets you make progress anyway - if there are linear dependencies between the $1/\log p$, just 'bake them in' from the start, and use those linear dependencies in your favour.

      I think that a similar trick could work here as well, giving an unconditional proof that the upper density of $n$ for which $(a_n,L_n)>1$ is equal to $1$.
