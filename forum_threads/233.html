Let $d_n=p_{n+1}-p_n$, where $p_n$ is the $n$th prime. Prove that\[\sum_{1\leq n\leq N}d_n^2 \ll N(\log N)^2.\]
#233: [Er40,p.440][Er55c,p.2][Er65b,p.205]
number theory | primes
The open status of this problem reflects the current belief of the owner of this website. Comment activity that has not yet been incorporated into the remarks
There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Cramer [Cr36] proved an upper bound of $O(N(\log N)^4)$ conditional on the Riemann hypothesis. Selberg [Se43] improved this slightly (still assuming the Riemann hypothesis) to\[\sum_{1\leq n\leq N}\frac{d_n^2}{n}\ll (\log N)^4.\]The prime number theorem immediately implies a lower bound of\[\sum_{1\leq n\leq N}d_n^2\gg N(\log N)^2.\]This would imply in particular that $d_n\ll n^{1/2}\log n$ for all $n$, which is known only the assumption of the Riemann Hypothesis.

The values of the sum are listed at A074741 on the OEIS.

This is discussed in problem A8 of Guy's collection [Gu04].

  • It seems this problem is currently just barely out of reach in the function field model. In $\mathbb F_q[T]$, at least for certain finite fields $\mathbb F_q$, there are "no large gaps between primes" results and uniform versions of the Hardy-Littlewood conjecture for $k=2$ with power savings. Combining these (along the lines of "Distribution of Large Gaps Between Primes by Funkhouser & Goldston & Ledoan), I believe one gets that the second moment of the number of primes in short intervals has the expected size up to the range where one can prove every short interval has a prime. This corresponds to a bound of the form $O ( N (\log N)^3)$. This might demonstate, in a different way, the difficulty of this problem.

    • The upper bound $O(N(\log{N})^3)$ can be easily obtained if we assume a weak form of Cramér's conjecture i.e. $d_n < C\log^2{p_n}$ for some constant $C$ and apply the Pólya–Szegő inequality that $(\sum_{k=1}^{n}x_k^2)(\sum_{k=1}^{n}y_k^2)\le \frac{1}{4}(\sqrt{\frac{m}{M}}+\sqrt{\frac{M}{m}})^2(\sum_{k=1}^{n}x_ky_k)^2$ where $m=m_xm_y$, $M=M_xM_y$, $m_x=\min\{x_1, x_2,\cdots, x_n\}$, $M_x=\max\{x_1, x_2,\cdots, x_n\}$, $m_y=\min\{y_1, y_2,\cdots, y_n\}$, $M_y=\max\{y_1, y_2,\cdots, y_n\}$.

  • Note that a positive solution to this problem would automatically imply the prime gap bound $d_n \ll n^{1/2} \log n$, which was established by Cramer under the Riemann hypothesis but is not known in general; even the strongest forms of the prime tuples conjecture are not known to imply this bound. So it is extremely unlikely that one will be able to establish this result even assuming the prime tuples conjecture, if RH is not also assumed. (*Possibly* one might be able to make some progress assuming *both* RH and the prime tuples conjecture, but it is not clear how these rather different inputs could be combined.)
