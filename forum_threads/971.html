Let $p(a,d)$ be the least prime congruent to $a\pmod{d}$. Does there exist a constant $c>0$ such that, for all large $d$,\[p(a,d) > (1+c)\phi(d)\log d\]for $\gg \phi(d)$ many values of $a$?

Erdős [Er49c] could prove this is true for an infinite sequence of $d$. He also proved that, for any $\epsilon>0$,\[p(a,d)< \epsilon \phi(d)\log d\]for $\gg_\epsilon \phi(d)$ many values of $a$.

  • I asked Claude for a literature review. For the larger quantity $\max_a p(a,d)$ (where one only seeks $p(a,d)$ to be large for a single $a$ rather than a positive fraction of $a$), the problem is analogous to #4: Pomerance in 1980 got a lower bound of $(e^\gamma-o(1)) \phi(d) \log d$, and Li-Shakan-Pratt has the improved lower bound $\gg \phi(k) \log k \log\log k \log\log\log\log k / \log\log\log k$, in parallel with #4, and conjectured that the true order of growth here is around $\phi(k) \log^2 k$. For the stated problem where one needs a positive density of $k$, Claude did not locate any literature, but reproduced (at least at a heuristic level) the Poisson statistic calculation that suggests the answer is positive assuming the prime tuples conjecture.

  • This is true conditionally on Hardy-Littlewoods ("Conditional Poisson law at the Linnik scale"):
    Let $c>0$ be fixed, let $d\to\infty$, and set\[
    x:=(1+c)\phi(d)\log d,\qquad
    N_a:=\pi(x;d,a)=\#\{p\le x:\ p\equiv a\!\!\pmod d\}\quad((a,d)=1).
    \]Assume the following uniform prime $r$-tuple asymptotic for every fixed $r\ge1$: for all distinct integers $0\le k_1,\dots,k_r\le \lfloor x/d\rfloor$, writing $h_i:=(k_i-k_1)d$,\[
    \#\{n\in [1,d]:\ n,\ n+h_2,\dots,n+h_r\ \text{are prime}\}
    =\Bigl(\mathfrak S(\mathbf h)+o(1)\Bigr)\frac{d}{(\log x)^r},
    \]uniformly in $d$ (here $\mathfrak S(\mathbf h)$ is the usual singular series), and in addition the average singular series over such tuples has the expected value\[
    \frac{1}{K^r}\!\!\sum_{\substack{0\le k_1,\dots,k_r\le K\\ \text{distinct}}}\!\!\mathfrak S(\mathbf h)
    =\Bigl(\frac{d}{\phi(d)}\Bigr)^{r-1}(1+o(1)),\qquad K:=\Bigl\lfloor\frac{x}{d}\Bigr\rfloor.
    \]Then expanding factorial moments and averaging over $a\in(\mathbb Z/d\mathbb Z)^\times$ gives\[
    \mathbb E[(N_a)_r]\ :=\ \frac{1}{\phi(d)}\sum_{(a,d)=1} N_a(N_a-1)\cdots(N_a-r+1)
    \ \sim\ \Bigl(\frac{x}{\phi(d)\log x}\Bigr)^r\ =:\ \lambda^r.
    \]Hence (method of moments) $N_a$ is asymptotically $\mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)$ as $a$ varies, with\[
    \lambda=\frac{x}{\phi(d)\log x}\sim 1+c.
    \]In particular,\[
    \#\{a\bmod d:\ (a,d)=1,\ N_a=0\}=(e^{-\lambda}+o(1))\phi(d)\sim e^{-(1+c)}\phi(d),
    \]equivalently for a positive proportion of reduced residue classes one has
    $p(a,d)>(1+c)\phi(d)\log d$.

  • write $q(a,d)$ to be the least $q>0$ so that $qd+a$ is prime. then we have that $p(a,d)=q(a,d)d+a$, unless $p(a,d)=a$.

    now heuristically we expect the values of $q(a,d)$ to distributed like samples from a geometric random variable with success probability $\frac{d}{\phi(d)}\cdot \frac{1}{\log(d)}$. this has mean $\mu = \frac{1}{d}\phi(d)\log(d)$ whence it seems $p(a,d)$ should have expectation $\approx d\mu =\phi(d)\log(d)$.

    and we'd expect this conjecture to be true due to how geometric random variables fluctuate. you'd in fact expect that for each $C$, that $\Omega_C(\phi(d))$ values of $a$ have $p(a,d)>C\phi(d)\log(d)$, if I'm not mistaken.
