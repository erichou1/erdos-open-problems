Let $r\geq 3$, and let $f_r(N)$ denote the size of the largest subset of $\{1,\ldots,N\}$ such that no subset of size $r$ has the same pairwise greatest common divisor between all elements. Estimate $f_r(N)$.
#535: [Er69][Er70][Er73]
number theory

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Erdős [Er64] proved that\[f_r(N) \leq N^{\frac{3}{4}+o(1)},\]and Abbott and Hanson [AbHa70] improved this exponent to $1/2$. Erdős [Er64] proved the lower bound, for any $r \geq 3$,\[f_r(N) > N^{\frac{c_r}{\log\log N}}\]for some constant $c_r>0$, and conjectured this should also be an upper bound.

This is intimately connected with the sunflower problem [20]. Indeed, Erdős [Er64] noted that a positive solution to [20] would imply\[f_r(N) \leq N^{\frac{C_r}{\log\log N}}\]for some constant $C_r>0$. Using the recent bounds of Alweiss, Lovett, Wu, and Zhang [ALWZ20] for the sunflower problem yields\[f_r(N) \leq N^{C_r\frac{\log\log\log N}{\log\log N}},\]and in particular $f_r(N) \leq N^{o(1)}$.

An alternative description of the details of these lower and upper bounds was given by Abbott and Hanson [AbHa67]. (A similar detailed calculation is given in the comments by Hrishi.)

See also [536].

  • We have obtained an estimate for $f_r(N)$, developed in conjunction with ChatGPT-5.5 Thinking, where I worked on the argument with sourish and kireet.

    Our main estimate is:

    $$
    f_r(N) \geq \exp\left((\log(r-1)+o(1))\frac{\log N}{\log\log N}\right)
    $$

    and

    $$
    f_r(N) \leq \exp\left(O_r\left(\frac{\log N\log\log\log N}{\log\log N}\right)\right).
    $$

    In particular,

    $$
    f_r(N)=N^{o(1)}.
    $$

    The key idea is to encode each integer $n$ by the set of its prime-power divisibility layers,

    $$
    S(n)=\{(p,j):1\leq j\leq v_p(n)\}.
    $$

    Then

    $$
    S(a)\cap S(b)=S(\gcd(a,b)),
    $$

    so an $r$-tuple whose pairwise gcds are all equal is exactly an $r$-sunflower in the corresponding set system.

    The lower bound comes from a block construction: partition the primes into blocks of size $r-1$, and choose one prime from each block. The pigeonhole principle prevents $r$ distinct chosen products from having all pairwise gcds equal.

    For the upper bound, we split each integer into its smooth part and rough part. Rankin’s estimate controls the number of possible smooth parts, while the improved sunflower lemma controls each rough fiber, giving the stated upper bound.

    The paper is available here.

  • I looked through the literature after my earlier comment, and I think the right correction is stronger than just adding a squarefree hypothesis.

    The counterexample $A_m=\{2,4,8,\dots,2^m\}$ shows that the current auxiliary formulation with $\omega(n)=k$ is false: for $k=1$, this family can be arbitrarily large while still avoiding an $r$-tuple with constant pairwise gcd.

    What seems to be the correct fix is the one Erdős gives later. In [Er73], he says that Abbott pointed out the ordinary sunflower conjecture does not seem to suffice for deriving the conjectured upper bound for $f_3(x)$. He then replaces it by a slightly stronger conjecture in which the integers have exactly $n$ prime factors counted with multiplicity. In modern notation, this is $\Omega(n)=k$, not $\omega(n)=k$.

    So I think the auxiliary statement here should be updated to:

    estimate the size of the largest set $A$ of integers such that $\Omega(n)=k$ for all $n\in A$, and there do not exist $a_1,\dots,a_r\in A$ and an integer $d$ such that $(a_i,a_j)=d$ for all $i\ne j$ and $\left(\frac{a_i}{d},d\right)=1$ for all $i$.

    The squarefree / $\omega(n)=k$ version is still a natural special case, and it is suggested by the argument in [Er64], but it is not the full corrected auxiliary statement that Erdős later endorsed after Abbott’s objection.

  • I tried to formalize the “stronger sunflower problem” phrasing here: https://github.com/google-deepmind/formal-conjectures/pull/2199

    But as stated, this seems false without an extra restriction: for $k=1$ consider $A_m={2,4,8,\dots,2^m}.$
    Then $\omega(n)=1$ for all $n\in A_m$, and for any $r\ge 3$ there is no $r$-subset with constant pairwise gcd (pairwise gcds vary with the minimal exponent), so it satisfies the “no constant pairwise gcd with coprime quotients” condition. Yet $|A_m|=m$ is arbitrarily large, contradicting any bound $|A|\le c_r^k$ (or even $c_r^k k!$ since $k=1$).

    In Erdős’s 1964 paper, the set-system/sunflower step is applied to squarefree parts / prime-factor sets (he explicitly introduces squarefree numbers and decomposes $a_i=A_iB_i$ with $B_i$ squarefree). That suggests the intended sunflower-style statement meant to restrict to squarefree integers (or radicals) rather than just $\omega(n)=k$?
