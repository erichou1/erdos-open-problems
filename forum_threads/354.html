OPEN This is open, and cannot be resolved with a finite computation.
Let $\alpha,\beta\in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ such that $\alpha/\beta$ is irrational. Is the multiset\[\{ \lfloor \alpha\rfloor,\lfloor 2\alpha\rfloor,\lfloor 4\alpha\rfloor,\ldots\}\cup \{ \lfloor \beta\rfloor,\lfloor 2\beta\rfloor,\lfloor 4\beta\rfloor,\ldots\}\]complete? That is, can all sufficiently large natural numbers $n$ be written as\[n=\sum_{s\in S}\lfloor 2^s\alpha\rfloor+\sum_{t\in T}\lfloor 2^t\beta\rfloor\]for some finite $S,T\subset \mathbb{N}$?

What if $2$ is replaced by some $\gamma\in(1,2)$?
#354: [ErGr80,p.58]
number theory | complete sequences

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
This question was first mentioned by Graham [Gr71].

Hegyvári [He89] proved that this holds if $\alpha=m/2^n$ is a dyadic rational and $\beta$ is not. He later [He91] proved that, for any fixed $\alpha>0$, the set of $\beta$ for which this holds either has measure $0$ or infinite measure. In [He94] he proved that the set of $(\alpha,\beta)$ for which the corresponding set of sums does not contain an infinite arithmetic progression has cardinality continuum.

Hegyvári [He89] proved that the sequence is not complete if $\alpha\geq 2$ and $\beta =2^k\alpha$ for some $k\geq 0$. Jiang and Ma [JiMa24] and Fang and He [FaHe25] prove that the sequence is not complete if $1<\alpha<2$ and $\beta=2^k\alpha$ for some sufficiently large $k$.

It is likely (and Hegyvári conjectures) that the assumption $\alpha/\beta$ irrational can be weakened to $\alpha/\beta \neq 2^k$ and either $\alpha$ or $\beta$ not a dyadic rational.

In the comments van Doorn proves the sequence is complete if $\alpha < 2<\beta<3$, and also proves that if either $\alpha$ or $\beta$ is not a dyadic rational then the corresponding sequence with ceiling functions replacing the floor functions is complete.

  • Using the deep research tool of ChatGPT I managed to find a few references I was not aware of previously, and I think my first comment here is mostly obsolete. Oh well. Long comment incoming! And by the way, as far as I know, in everything that follows the original problem with constant $\gamma = 2$ is considered, unless otherwise stated.

    Okay, to start off,

    N. Hegyvári, Some remarks on a problem of Erdős and Graham, Acta Math. Hung., Volume 53 (1989), 149-154

    proves exactly Proposition 1 in my comment above; if $\alpha$ is a dyadic rational and $\beta$ is not a dyadic rational, then the sequence under consideration is complete. On the other hand, using $\alpha \ge 2$ and $\beta = 2^l \alpha$ with $l \in \mathbb{N}$, he also shows that the set of pairs $(\alpha, \beta)$ for which the sequence is not complete has the cardinality of the continuum.

    Even though I cannot access this paper by Hegyvári, I can find his follow-up paper where he mentions that he had obtained the dyadic rational result:

    N. Hegyvári, On complete sequences, Annales Univ. Sci. Budapest, Volume 34 (1991), 7-10.

    In this second paper (which you can find here, pages 7-10) he furthermore proves that, for any fixed $\alpha > 0$, the set of $\beta$ for which the corresponding sequence is complete either has Lebesgue measure $0$, or has infinite measure. In order to prove this he makes use of the observation in my first comment that $P(2\alpha, \beta)$ implies $P(\alpha, \beta)$. But he didn't stop there!

    N. Hegyvári, On sumset of certain sets, Publ. Math. Debrecen, Volume 45 (1994), 115-122

    which can be found here, shows that the set of pairs $(\alpha, \beta)$ for which the sequence does not even contain an infinite arithmetic progression, also has the cardinality of the continuum. This strengthens the second result from the first paper I mentioned. Both cardinality continuum results make use of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ with $\frac{\beta}{\alpha}$ a power of two (i.e. rational), however. So they don't disprove the conjecture at hand.

    Then, much more recently, we finally have the following two papers

    X. W. Jiang, W. X. Ma, A conjecture of Hegyvári, Int. J. Number Theory, Volume 20 (2024), 915–933.
    J. H. Fang, J. Y. He, On a problem of Erdős and Graham, Acta Math. Hung., Volume 175 (2025), 532-542.

    Thanks to Terence who kindly sent me a copy of the second paper (and see his comment below as well!), both papers seem to extend Hegyvári's non-completeness results in the case that $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}$ is a power of two, but for $\alpha < 2$ and $\beta$ sufficiently large.

    Long story short, I believe the following four results for specific $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are known:

    Proposition 1. If exactly one of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ is a dyadic rational, then the sequence is complete.
    Proposition 2. If $\min(\alpha, \beta) < 2$ and $\max(\alpha, \beta) < 3$, then the sequence is complete.
    Proposition 3. If $\alpha \ge 2$ and $\beta = 2^l \alpha$ with $l \in \mathbb{N}$, then the sequence is not complete.
    Proposition 4. If $\alpha < 2$ and $\beta = 2^l \alpha$ with $l \in \mathbb{N}$ sufficiently large, then the sequence is not complete.

    Here, Propositions 1 and 3 are due to Hegyvári, while Proposition 4 is due to Jiang-Ma and Fang-He.

    Long story even shorter, as far as I can tell there is no solution to the main problem yet. On the other hand, I do suspect that the first paper by Hegyvári either settles the case $\gamma = \sqrt{2}$ or perhaps even $1 < \gamma \le \sqrt{2}$ (possibly implicitly). If anyone has access to this paper and could check, that would be appreciated. And in other news, the hypothesis that $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}$ is irrational can most likely be slightly weakened (if that wasn't clear already); Hegyvári conjectures that the sequence is complete if either $\alpha$ or $\beta$ is not a dyadic rational, and $\beta \neq 2^l \alpha$. In other words, Propositions 3 and 4 are conjecturally close to optimal.

    The result I mentioned in my second post in this comment section (namely, that if we change all floor functions to ceiling functions, then the sequence is complete, as long as either $\alpha$ or $\beta$ is not a dyadic rational) does seem to be new however, so perhaps I actually will keep working on my own paper on this subject.

  • At a first glance, it seems like there should be no fundamental difference in this question if we change all floor functions to ceiling functions, right? A priori there seems to be no reason why proving the completeness for one should be any easier or harder than for the other. Well, as fate would have it, I can resolve this problem.. But only if we use the ceiling function! Isn't that remarkable?

    To elaborate on this, let me define two notions. We say that a sequence $S = (s_1, s_2, \ldots)$ is a $\Sigma$-sequence if a constant $c$ exists such that $s_{n+1} - \sum_{i=1}^n s_i \le c$ for all $n$. And we call a sequence precomplete if, for every $k \in \mathbb{N}$, there are $k$ consecutive integers that can all be written as the sum of distinct elements of $S$.

    In his $1964$ paper 'Complete Sequences of Polynomial Values' (see here), Graham proved that if $A = (a_1, a_2, \ldots)$ is a $\Sigma$-sequence and $B = (b_1, b_2, \ldots)$ is precomplete, then the union $(a_1, b_1, a_2, b_2, \ldots)$ is complete.

    Now, if $a_{n+1} \le 2a_n$ for all large enough $n$, then one can show that $A$ is a $\Sigma$-sequence. And the equality $a_{n+1} \le 2a_n$ is satisfied for all large enough $n$ if $a_n = \lceil 2^{n-1} \alpha \rceil$, but not if $a_n = \lfloor 2^{n-1} \alpha \rfloor$!

    Moreover, to prove that $B = (b_1, b_2, \ldots)$ is precomplete for $b_n = \lceil 2^{n-1} \beta \rceil$, it turns out that it is sufficient to show that there are just two consecutive integers that can both be written as the sum of distinct elements of $B$. And for $b_n = \lceil 2^{n-1} \beta \rceil$ one can check that, unless $\beta$ is a dyadic rational, there is some $n$ such that $b_1 + b_2 + \ldots + b_{n-1}$ is equal to $b_n - 1$, finishing the proof.

    All in all I can prove the following theorem: as long as either $\alpha$ or $\beta$ is not a dyadic rational, then $(\lceil \alpha \rceil, \lceil \beta\rceil, \lceil 2\alpha \rceil, \lceil 2\beta\rceil, \lceil 4\alpha \rceil, \lceil 4\beta\rceil, \ldots)$ is complete.

    For a (first version of a) full write-up, see here.

    I plan to combine this write-up with another project I'm working on, and will submit a proper paper to the arXiv in the coming months. I'll make sure to link it once it's finished. In the mean time, perhaps someone else can use some of these ideas to put the ball into the back of the net ;) As it does feel like we're very close to solving this problem.

  • Let $P(\alpha, \beta)$ denote the statement that the above sequence is complete, where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are assumed to be positive reals. Then I can prove the following two propositions:

    Proposition 1. If $\alpha = \frac{m}{2^n}$ is a dyadic rational (so in particular if $\alpha \in \mathbb{N}$) and $\beta$ is not a dyadic rational, then $P(\alpha, \beta)$ holds.
    Proposition 2. If $\min(\alpha, \beta) < 2$ and $\max(\alpha, \beta) < 3$, then $P(\alpha, \beta)$ holds.

    In general we see that $P(2\alpha, \beta)$ implies $P(\alpha, \beta)$, as the corresponding sequence for the former is a subsequence of the latter. In particular, to prove the first proposition it is sufficient to assume $\alpha \in \mathbb{N}$.

    So assume $\alpha = m \in \mathbb{N}$ and let us first note that the sequence $(\lfloor \alpha \rfloor, \lfloor 2\alpha \rfloor, \lfloor 4\alpha \rfloor, \ldots)$ then generates $m\mathbb{N}$. We therefore need to show that sums of distinct elements from $B = (\lfloor \beta \rfloor, \lfloor 2\beta \rfloor, \lfloor 4\beta \rfloor, \ldots)$ attain all residue classes modulo $m$.

    Now, for every prime divisor $p$ of $m$, I claim that $B$ contains infinitely many elements not divisible by $p$, if $\beta$ is not a dyadic rational. Indeed, if $p$ divides $\lfloor 2^l \beta \rfloor$ for some $l$, let $r \in \mathbb{N}$ and $\epsilon \in (0, 1)$ be such that $2^l \beta = pr + \epsilon$ and choose $l'$ such that $1 \le 2^{l'} \epsilon < 2$. We then get that $\left \lfloor 2^{l+l'} \beta \right \rfloor = 2^{l'}pr + 1$ is not divisible by $p$.

    Let $b_1 \pmod{m}, b_2 \pmod{m}, \ldots, b_k \pmod{m}$ be those residue classes modulo $m$ that appear infinitely often in $B$. We remark that it is not necessarily the case that there exists an $i$ such that $\gcd(b_i, m) = 1$. However, we do have $\gcd(b_1, \ldots, b_k, m) = 1$ by the aforementioned claim on prime divisors $p$ of $m$. We therefore get by the existence of the Frobenius number that all residue classes modulo $m$ appear among sums of the $b_i$, which was what we needed to prove.

    As for the second proposition, note we may assume $\beta \ge \alpha > 1$; the first inequality by symmetry, and the second inequality by the aforementioned fact that $P(2\alpha, \beta)$ implies $P(\alpha, \beta)$. Now define, for $n \ge 1$, $s_{2n-1} = \lfloor 2^{n-1} \alpha \rfloor$ and $s_{2n} = \lfloor 2^{n-1} \beta \rfloor$. We then claim that $(s_1, s_2, \ldots)$ generates the entirety of $\mathbb{N}$, for which it is necessary and sufficient to show $s_{n+1} \le 1 + \sum_{i=1}^n s_i$ for all $n \ge 0$. This follows for $n = 0$ by the fact that $s_1 = \lfloor \alpha \rfloor = 1$, and it follows for $n = 1$ as $s_2 = \lfloor \beta \rfloor \le 2$. For $n \ge 2$ we get by induction
    \begin{align*}
    s_{n+1} &\le 2s_{n-1} + 1 \\
    &\le 2 + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} s_i \\
    &\le 1 + \sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i.
    \end{align*}
    In fact, the proof of this second proposition immediately generalizes to $\gamma \in (1,2)$ instead of $2$, and it even works in some cases for $\gamma \le 3$.
