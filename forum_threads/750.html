Let $f(m)$ be some function such that $f(m)\to \infty$ as $m\to \infty$. Does there exist a graph $G$ of infinite chromatic number such that every subgraph on $m$ vertices contains an independent set of size at least $\frac{m}{2}-f(m)$?
#750: [Er94b][Er95d]
graph theory | chromatic number
The open status of this problem reflects the current belief of the owner of this website. There may be literature on this problem that I am unaware of, which may partially or completely solve the stated problem. Please do your own literature search before expending significant effort on solving this problem. If you find any relevant literature not mentioned here, please add this in a comment.

A claimed solution has been posted in the comments.
In [Er69b] Erdős conjectures this for $f(m)=\epsilon m$ for any fixed $\epsilon>0$. This follows from a result of Erdős, Hajnal, and Szemerédi [EHS82], as described by msellke in the comments.

In [ErHa67b] Erdős and Hajnal prove this for $f(m)\geq cm$ for all $c>1/4$.

See also [75].

          MalekZ — 13:43 on 06 May 2026

          • I'd say the blocker is more "finding the correct machinery" than lack of machinery altogether.

            The implication \(74 \Rightarrow 750\) is quite natural because edge-bipartisation gives a large independent set. If \(\beta(H)\) is the minimum number of edges to delete to make \(H\) bipartite, then\[
            \alpha(H)\ge \frac{|V(H)|-\beta(H)}2.
            \]Indeed, take a minimum bipartising edge set, choose one endpoint from each deleted edge, remove those vertices, and then take the larger side of the remaining bipartite graph. So a positive answer to [74] gives [750] after replacing \(g\) by roughly \(2f\).

            But the reverse direction does not follow from this machinery. A positive answer to [750] only gives large independent sets, or in the stronger Mycielski-style proof, small vertex odd-cycle transversals:\[
            \operatorname{oct}(H)=\min\{|T|:H-T\text{ is bipartite}\}.
            \]That is much weaker than bounding \(\beta(H)\), the number of edges needed to make \(H\) bipartite.

            The obstruction is especially visible in generalized Mycielski graphs. For the vertex version, the apex is cheap: if an odd cycle goes through the apex, you can delete the apex as one vertex. This is why one gets estimates of the form\[
            \operatorname{oct}(M_s(H)[X])
            \le s\,\operatorname{oct}(H[P]) + 1.
            \]That \(+1\) is harmless for [750].

            For [74], however, you cannot delete the apex. You have to delete enough edges from the apex-created odd cycles to destroy all of them, and that can be huge. For example, take \(H=tK_2\), a matching of size \(t\). Then \(H\) is bipartite, so\[
            \operatorname{oct}(H)=0,\qquad \beta(H)=0.
            \]But in the one-step Mycielski/cone construction, the apex creates \(t\) edge-disjoint odd cycles sharing that apex. Deleting the apex kills all of them, so\[
            \operatorname{oct}(M_1(H))=1,
            \]whereas edge-bipartisation must hit each of the \(t\) odd cycles separately, giving\[
            \beta(M_1(H))=t.
            \]So the Mycielski/cone machinery that works for [750] does not directly work for [74].

            So I don't see any way [750] can be reversed into [74] by the same argument. There is shared machinery but for [74] one would need an edge-profile version of that machinery, controlling not just whether odd cycles can be killed by a few vertices, but how many edge deletions the apex-created odd cycles force.

            I, with the help of ChatGPT, also tried looking at a possible colour-forcing/sender route. The idea would be to build bounded-\(\beta\), high-oddgirth gadgets forcing two nonadjacent terminals to receive different colours. But this seems to run into a major issue: for fixed \(K\), \(\beta(S)\le K\) and odd-girth at least \(3K+5\) appear to imply that any two nonadjacent vertices can still be equal-coloured in some proper 3-colouring. So pairwise sender-type gadgets do not seem to be the right way to attack [74] either.

            A longer, mostly AI-generated, proof on that sender obstruction is available here: No bounded-edge-bipartisation high-odd-girth three-colour inequality senders (via archive.org). To be clear: this note is almost fully AI-generated and has not been extensively checked, so it should not be taken at face value as a finished proof. I have not been able to find any issues with the proof myself, though that doesn't make it gospel. I mention it only as a reference for the obstruction I have in mind.

            So my current guess is: the main missing thing missing is an edge-bipartisation analogue of the Mycielski local profile theorem. Either such an edge-profile theorem exists, which would be a plausible route to [74], or the obstruction caused by the apex edge-cost where a counterexample to that route could appear.

  • The correct reference for the $f(m)=\epsilon m$ result seems to be Theorem 1 in (P. Erdős, A. Hajnal and E. Szemerédi, "On almost bipartite large chromatic graphs", Ann. Discrete Math. 12 (1982), 117–123). The stated theorem says you can find a bipartite induced subgraph with at least $(1-\epsilon) n$ vertices. Taking the larger half of the bipartition then confirms the conjecture/claim by [Er69b]/[Er94b].
