Let $f(n)$ be maximal such that, given any $n$ points in $\mathbb{R}^2$, there exist $f(n)$ points such that no two are distance $1$ apart. Estimate $f(n)$. In particular, is it true that $f(n)\geq n/4$?
#1070: [Er87b,p.171]
geometry

There are claims of partial results and/or a path to a solution in the comments.
In other words, estimate the minimal independence number of a unit distance graph with $n$ vertices. If $\omega$ is the independence number and $\chi$ is the chromatic number then $\omega \chi\geq n$, and hence $f(n)\geq n/\chi$, where $\chi$ is the answer to the Hadwiger-Nelson problem [508].

The Moser spindle shows $f(n)\leq \frac{2}{7}n\approx 0.285n$. Larman and Rogers [LaRo72] noted that if $m_1$ is the supremum of the upper densities of measurable subsets of $\mathbb{R}^2$ which have no unit distance pairs then\[f(n)\geq m_1n.\]Croft [Cr67] gave the best-known lower bound of $m_1\geq 0.22936$ and hence\[0.22936n \leq f(n) \leq \frac{2}{7}n\approx 0.285n.\]Ambrus, Csiszárik, Matolcsi, Varga, and Zsámboki [ACMVZ23] have proved that $m_1\leq 0.247$, and hence this approach cannot achieve $f(n)\geq n/4$. See [232] for more on $m_1$.

Matolcsi, Ruzsa, Varga, and Zsámboki [MRVZ23] have improved the upper bound to\[f(n) \leq \left(\frac{1}{4}+o(1)\right)n.\]They conjecture that $m_1=0.22936\cdots$ (the lower bound of Croft mentioned above) and $f(n)=(1/4+o(1))n$.

If we also insist that no two points are distance $<1$ apart then this is problem becomes [1066].

  • Let me note that our not-yet-peer-reviewed paper [FCN4] makes progress on this question by presenting unit distance graphs with independence ratio arbitrarily close to 1/4. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first improvement of the trivial 2n/7 upper bound, to n/4+o(n). It is reasonable to conjecture that either Croft's 0.22936 or our 1/4 is sharp, [FCN4] Conjecture 1 states the latter.

  • The problem above was solved by G.~Csizmadia in his paper
    \emph{On the independence number of minimum distance graphs},
    Discrete Comput. Geom. \textbf{20}, 179--187 (1998).
    \url{https://doi.org/10.1007/PL00009381}. In this work, Csizmadia studies the
    unit-distance graph defined on a finite planar point set whose minimum
    pairwise distance is~1, and asks for the size of the largest independent
    set, that is, a subset containing no two points at distance exactly~1.

    Csizmadia improved an earlier result of Pollack by proving that every
    such set of \(n\) points contains an independent subset of size at least\[
    \frac{9}{35}\,n \approx 0.2571\,n.
    \]In particular,\[
    f(n) \ge \frac{9}{35}\,n > \frac{1}{4}\,n,
    \]so the conjectured lower bound \(f(n) \ge n/4\) is true (and even
    slightly improved). The paper also provides an \(O(n\log n)\) algorithm
    which constructs such a large independent subset for any given
    configuration of \(n\) points.

    \bigskip
    \noindent
    References

    [Cs98] G. Csizmadia, \emph{On the independence number of minimum distance graphs}, Discrete Comput. Geom. \textbf{20}, 179--187 (1998). \url{https://doi.org/10.1007/PL00009381}.

    [ACMVZ23] G. Ambrus, A. Csiszárik, M. Matolcsi, D. Varga, and P. Zsámboki, \emph{The density of planar sets avoiding unit distances}, arXiv:2207.14179 (2023).

    [Cr67] H. T. Croft, \emph{Incidence incidents}, Eureka (1967), 22--26.

    [LaRo72] D. G. Larman and C. A. Rogers, \emph{The realization of distances within sets in Euclidean space}, Mathematika (1972), 1--24.
