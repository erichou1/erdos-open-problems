Is every sufficiently large integer of the form\[ap^2+b\]for some prime $p$ and integer $a\geq 1$ and $0\leq b<p$?
#676: [Er79][Er79d][Er80,p.107]

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
The sieve of Eratosthenes implies that almost all integers are of this form, and the Brun-Selberg sieve implies the number of exceptions in $[1,x]$ is $\ll x/(\log x)^c$ for some constant $c>0$. Erdős [Er79] believed it is 'rather unlikely' that all large integers are of this form.

What if the condition that $p$ is prime is omitted? Selfridge and Wagstaff made a 'preliminary computer search' and suggested that there are infinitely many $n$ not of this form even without the condition that $p$ is prime. It should be true that the number of exceptions in $[1,x]$ is $<x^c$ for some constant $c<1$.

Most generally, given some infinite set $A\subseteq \mathbb{N}$ and function $f:A\to \mathbb{N}$ one can ask for sufficient conditions on $A$ and $f$ that guarantee every large number (or almost all numbers) can be written as\[am^2+b\]for some $m\in A$ and $a\geq 1$ and $0\leq b<f(m)$.

In another direction, one can ask what is the minimal $c_n$ such that $n$ can be written as $n=ap^2+b$ with $0\leq b<c_np$ for some $p\leq \sqrt{n}$. This problem asks whether $c_n\leq 1$ eventually, but in [Er79d] Erdős suggests that in fact $\limsup c_n=\infty$. Is it true that $c_n<n^{o(1)}$?

  • I would like to add some larger computational evidence for the exceptional
    integers in this problem.

    Recall that $n$ is representable in the form\[
    n=ap^2+b,\qquad p\ \text{prime},\quad a\geq 1,\quad 0\leq b<p
    \]if and only if there is a prime $p\leq \sqrt n$ such that\[
    n\bmod p^2 < p.
    \]Thus $n$ is an exception if and only if\[
    n\bmod p^2 \geq p
    \]for every prime $p\leq \sqrt n$.

    Using this test, I found the following larger exception:\[
    n=10000001000000006.
    \]This is larger than the previously mentioned example\[
    100000078386123.
    \]Indeed,\[
    10000001000000006 > 100000078386123.
    \]For\[
    n=10000001000000006
    \]we have\[
    \lfloor \sqrt n\rfloor = 100000004.
    \]The computation checks every prime $p\leq 100000004$ and finds that\[
    n\bmod p^2 \geq p
    \]in every case. Therefore this $n$ has no representation of the required form.

    Some further exceptions found in the same range are\[
    \begin{aligned}
    10000001000000006,\quad
    10000001000000010,\quad
    10000001000000011,\quad
    10000001000000014,\quad
    10000001000000015,\\
    10000001000000019,\quad
    10000001000000022,\quad
    10000001000000023,\quad
    10000001000952655,\quad
    10000001000952662,\\
    10000001000952663,\quad
    10000001000952670,\quad
    10000001000952671,\quad
    10000001000952674,\quad
    10000001000952690,\\
    10000001000952691,\quad
    10000001000952698,\quad
    10000001000952699,\quad
    10000001000952706,\quad
    10000001000952707.
    \end{aligned}
    \]These examples suggest defining the exceptional sequence\[
    E=\{n\in\mathbb N:\ n\bmod p^2\geq p
    \text{ for every prime }p\leq \sqrt n\}.
    \]Equivalently, if $E=\{e_1<e_2<e_3<\cdots\}$, then $e_k$ is the $k$-th integer
    which is not representable as\[
    ap^2+b,\qquad p\ \text{prime},\quad a\geq 1,\quad 0\leq b<p.
    \]This gives a natural sequence of exceptions.

    At present I do not know a genuine closed-form infinite family of such
    exceptions. Producing such a closed-form infinite family would essentially
    amount to major progress on the problem, since it would prove infinitely many
    exceptions. The safest closed-form description is therefore the defining
    condition\[
    e_k=\text{the $k$-th positive integer }n
    \text{ such that }n\bmod p^2\geq p
    \text{ for every prime }p\leq \sqrt n.
    \]The algorithm used for the computation was the following.\[
    \begin{array}{l}
    \textbf{Input: } N.\\[1mm]
    \textbf{Step 1. } \text{Compute all primes }p\leq \sqrt N
    \text{ using the sieve of Eratosthenes.}\\[1mm]
    \textbf{Step 2. } \text{For each such prime }p,\text{ compute }
    r=N\bmod p^2.\\[1mm]
    \textbf{Step 3. } \text{If }r<p\text{ for some }p,\text{ then }
    N=ap^2+r\text{ is representable.}\\[1mm]
    \textbf{Step 4. } \text{If }r\geq p\text{ for every prime }p\leq \sqrt N,
    \text{ then }N\text{ is an exception.}
    \end{array}
    \]For interval searches, I used the equivalent marking algorithm. In an interval
    $[X,X+L)$, for each prime $p\leq \sqrt{X+L}$, mark all integers of the form\[
    kp^2+r,\qquad 0\leq r<p,
    \]which lie in the interval. The unmarked integers are precisely the exceptions
    in that interval. Each unmarked candidate was then checked again directly by
    the residue test\[
    N\bmod p^2\geq p
    \]for every prime $p\leq \sqrt N$.

    The computations and the preparation of this algorithmic description were
    assisted by ChatGPT 5.5. The verification itself is elementary and reproducible:
    one only needs to generate the primes up to $\sqrt N$ and check the congruence
    condition above.

    The heuristic remains the same. For a fixed prime $p$, the good residue classes
    modulo $p^2$ are\[
    0,1,\ldots,p-1,
    \]so the good proportion is $1/p$, and the proportion avoiding this prime is
    $1-1/p$. Assuming approximate independence over primes $p\leq \sqrt x$, the
    expected density of exceptions is\[
    \prod_{p\leq \sqrt x}\left(1-\frac1p\right).
    \]By Mertens' theorem,\[
    \prod_{p\leq y}\left(1-\frac1p\right)\sim \frac{e^{-\gamma}}{\log y}.
    \]Putting $y=\sqrt x$ gives\[
    \prod_{p\leq \sqrt x}\left(1-\frac1p\right)
    \sim \frac{2e^{-\gamma}}{\log x}.
    \]Thus the heuristic prediction is\[
    \#\{n\leq x:n\in E\}
    \sim \frac{2e^{-\gamma}x}{\log x}.
    \]In particular, the heuristic predicts infinitely many exceptions, and even
    about a constant multiple of $x/\log x$ exceptions up to $x$.

    • In a highly experimental use, I have tried with ChatGPT to assess the direction of the preprint (without judging the author one way or the other). I realize this more of an art than science...

      First, we discussed the general background, such as the "almost all" result and known sieve heuristics. The main obstacle is identified: transitioning from "rare" to "impossible".

      The paper's strategy is identified as studying the events $E_p(n) := \{ n \mod p^2 < p \}$ with vector of indicators $X_p(n) := 1_{E_p(n)}$. We want to compare the true joint law $P$ of $(X_p)_{p \leq X}$ to the product law $Q = \prod P_p$. The quantity to control is Kullback-Leibler divergence $D(P \parallel Q)$. The author then posited the Uniformity Hypothesis (UH) and strong Uniformity Hypothesis (sUH) which are bounds on $D(P \parallel Q)$. In particular, sUH is of importance that it should imply the finiteness of the exceptional set $E$ (Claim 1). Moreover, the author claims that either the Elliott-Halberstam conjecture or generalized Riemann Hypothesis imply sUH (Claim 2).

      We then see that Claims 1 and 2 are the two main pieces to assess.

      After thinking for over 15 minutes, ChatGPT assessed the two claims as follows.

      $\textbf{Claim 1}$. The main claim here is Theorem 6.9, whose proof is only 4 lines (!) and invokes Borel-Cantelli. ChatGPT assessed the direction of the proof as:

      "- Even a very strong average bound like $\Pr_{n\le x}(n\in E)\ll 1/(\log x)^{1+\varepsilon}$ would not by itself imply the deterministic set $E$ is finite; it would only say it’s sparse on average. The paper’s proof sketch invokes Borel–Cantelli in a way that (as written) seems to apply to a random sequence of sampled integers, not to the actual set of all exceptions.
      - More fundamentally: with “independence-like” behavior, the heuristic size of the exceptional set is typically $\sim x/\log x$ (because $\prod_{p\le X}(1-1/p)\sim e^{-\gamma}/\log X$). Independence pushes you toward infinitely many exceptions, not finiteness. The paper is trying to conclude the opposite from an “almost-independence” hypothesis, which is already a big red flag relative to the mechanism it sets up."

      Moreover, the proof of Theorem 6.9 claims bounds $\ll 1/(\log X)^{1+\varepsilon}$. ChatGPT sketched arguments that the actual machinery should give $\ll 1/(\log X)^{1-\varepsilon}$; even under "perfect independence" that'd only get you $\ll 1/\log X$.

      Conclusion: the claimed exponent appears too strong from existing machinery, and, even if we do get this exponent, it's only an average bound and that doesn't bridge to finiteness. Moreover, the "almost-independence" heuristic seems to be going in the wrong direction.

      $\textbf{Claim 2}$. The implication from EH/GRH to sUH.

      ChatGPT argued this is problematic for a "simpler reason": the $N(x;q,b)$ is defined as the count of integers in a residue class. Since these counts and their second moments are already elementary, we do not need EH/GRH to control them. In fact, EH/GRH is about primes in arithmetic progressions and likely irrelevant here. In ChatGPT's words:

      "“EH/GRH ⇒ strong second-moment control for primes in progressions” is true in the right context, and “second-moment control ⇒ small KL divergence” is a real information-theoretic theme (they even state a χ²/KL control via an energy functional).
      But the paper’s specific EH/GRH ⇒ sUH claim doesn’t line up with the objects it defined (integer residue counts), and the modulus/scale issues are not resolved."

      Conclusion: there could be some right conceptual ideas, but the connections to EH/GRH are likely irrelevant.

      $\textbf{Overall}$. It seems to me that both claims are difficult to fix. EH/GRH is not likely to get to Uniformity because they don't seem to apply to this setting; on the other hand, Uniformity also doesn't seem to get to finiteness due to the kind of argument used.

      As always, I write from my best understanding and do not claim accuracy in the chat's statements.

  • Greeting! I would like to share a recent attempt on [79] (quadratic prime representations).

    In my preprint I introduce an **Entropy--Sieve Method**, which combines sieve theory with information-theoretic tools (relative entropy/KL divergence and a quadratic energy functional). The key reduction shows that finiteness of the exceptional set may follow if one can establish the uniformity estimate\[
    E_2(P) \;=\; \frac{1+o(1)}{M},
    \qquad M=\prod_{p\le Y} p^2,
    \]for the quadratic energy of the residue distribution. A change-of-measure argument via KL divergence then transforms this into a sharp tail bound for the sieve sum.

    What is important here is that this framework isolates precisely the analytic input needed: if the above uniformity holds, then the \emph{exceptional set} $E$ (integers with no quadratic prime representation) is finite. In other words, only finitely many large integers could fail Erdős's representation. This directly addresses the central question of [79]. Our numerical experiments further confirm that many large numbers remain outside the representable set, showing that unconditional tools do not yet shrink $E$ to finite --- highlighting both the sharpness of our reduction and the difficulty of the problem.

    Thus, our contribution is twofold: (i) it provides a conditional path to prove finiteness of $E$ under standard distributional conjectures (EH or GRH), and (ii) it clarifies why unconditional finiteness is still out of reach, since in practice large integers without representation persist far beyond current analytic barriers.

    \textbf{Preprint:}
    ResearchGate link,
    DOI: 10.13140/RG.2.2.14599.05283.

    1. The explicit derivation and control of the divisor-weights $w(q,M)$ in the variance decomposition.
    2. The uniform lower bound $Q_r \gg 1/M$ required when passing from $\chi^2$ divergence to KL divergence.
