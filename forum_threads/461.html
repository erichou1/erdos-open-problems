Let $s_t(n)$ be the $t$-smooth component of $n$ - that is, the product of all primes $p$ (with multiplicity) dividing $n$ such that $p<t$. Let $f(n,t)$ count the number of distinct possible values for $s_t(m)$ for $m\in [n+1,n+t]$. Is it true that\[f(n,t)\gg t\](uniformly, for all $t$ and $n$)?
#461: [ErGr80,p.92]
number theory | primes

  • Here is a guess to Erdos #461: the bound $f(n,t)\ge t/2$ cannot hold asymptotically.

    Let $s_t(m)$ denote the $t$-smooth component of $m$, and let
    $$
    f(n,t)=|\{s_t(n+1),\ldots,s_t(n+t)\}|.
    $$
    For $y<t$,
    $$
    f(n,t)\le y+|\{1\le i\le t:s_t(n+i)>y\}|,
    $$
    since at most $y$ distinct values can lie in $[1,y]$. Put
    $$
    P_t=\prod_{p<t}p,\qquad Q=P_t\operatorname{lcm}(1,\ldots,y).
    $$
    For each $u\le y$, $u$ is automatically $t$-smooth, and
    $$
    s_t(m)=u
    \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
    m=ur,\ (r,P_t)=1.
    $$
    Thus, over one period modulo $Q$,
    $$
    \frac1Q|\{m\bmod Q:s_t(m)=u\}| =
    \frac1u\prod_{p<t}\left(1-\frac1p\right).
    $$
    Averaging the truncation inequality over $n\bmod Q$, and using that $n\mapsto n+i$ permutes residue classes modulo $Q$, gives
    $$
    \inf_n f(n,t)
    \le
    y+t\left(1-\prod_{p<t}\left(1-\frac1p\right)H_y\right),
    $$
    where $H_y=\sum_{u\le y}1/u$. Taking
    $$
    y\sim \frac{e^{-\gamma}t}{\log t}
    $$
    and using Mertens’ theorem yields
    $$
    \inf_n f(n,t)\le (1-e^{-\gamma}+o(1))t.
    $$
    Hence no universal lower bound $f(n,t)\ge ct)$ can hold with $c>1-e^{-\gamma}\approx0.4385$; in particular, the $c=1/2$ strengthening is false. This does not settle Problem #461, which only asks whether $f(n,t)\gg t$.

    Proof assisted by GPT 5.5 Pro and Claude Fable. Would be thankful for any proposals to amend.

        • Thank you for your kind observations! I should clarify that the \(t/2\) threshold was not meant to be attributed to Erdős--Graham, and does not settle the original question \(f(n,t)\gg t\).

          It came from Woett's idea from the comment below: if the proposed bipartite graph always had a matching covering \(\{1,\ldots,\lfloor t/2\rfloor\}\), then one would get \(f(n,t)\ge\lfloor t/2\rfloor\). Since Woett already showed that matching statement is false, I treated \(t/2\) only as a temporary strengthened guess suggested by the heuristic.

          The averaging argument only rules out that strengthened \(1/2\) guess, and more generally any lower bound \(f(n,t)\ge c t\) with \(c>1-e^{-\gamma}\).

          I also agree this is likely a standard first-moment/smooth-part calculation without novelty. The local density\[
          \Pr(s_t(m)=u)=\frac1u\prod_{p<t}(1-1/p)
          \]is the usual count for integers with a prescribed \(t\)-smooth part / largest smooth divisor, followed by Mertens' theorem. This reply is checked against GPT 5.5 pro and opus 4.8. Many thanks!

  • An idea I had that doesn't quite work as stated: for a given $n$ and $t$, consider the bipartite graph with left vertex set $\mathcal{L} := \{1, 2, \ldots, \lfloor t/2 \rfloor\}$, right vertex set $\mathcal{R} := \{s_t(n+1), \ldots, s_t(n+t)\}$, and where we draw an edge between $i \in \mathcal{L}$ and $s_t(m) \in \mathcal{R}$ if $i$ divides $s_t(m)$ (which in this case is equivalent to $i$ dividing $m$, as $i < t$). If there is a matching that covers all vertices of the left set, then we would be done.

    However, such a matching unfortunately does not always exist. ChatGPT managed to find the counterexample $n = 1407302$, $t = 38$. Here, with $$D := \{13, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19\} \subset \mathcal{L},$$the neighbourhood of $D$ in $\mathcal{R}$ only contains the $5 < |D|$ integers $s_t(m)$ with $$m - n \in \{9, 10, 13, 26, 28\}.$$
