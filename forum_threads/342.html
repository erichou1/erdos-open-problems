With $a_1=1$ and $a_2=2$ let $a_{n+1}$ for $n\geq 2$ be the least integer $>a_n$ which can be expressed uniquely as $a_i+a_j$ for $i<j\leq n$.

What can be said about this sequence? Do infinitely many pairs $a,a+2$ occur? Does this sequence eventually have periodic differences? Is the density $0$?

  • After a longer back and forth with GPT-5.4 I've got some new partial results in the direction I wanted, namely: L-packets as an explanation for "hidden signal". Here's the note that proves the following:

    If the classical Ulam sequence \(U(1,2)\) really has positive upper density, then the strange Fourier spike people see numerically is not a fluke. After passing to a Furstenberg limit, that spike turns into an actual eigenfrequency of a shift system built from the sequence. So the “hidden signal” in the Ulam numbers gets upgraded from an experiment to a real structural feature of a limiting dynamical system.

    The key finite combinatorial input is that the initial segments \(A_{N} = U(1,2)\cap [1,N]\) contain very few ordered Schur triples \(x+y=z\), in fact at most \(3|A_{N}|\). By Green’s theorem, that means \(A_{N}\) is very close to a genuinely sum-free set \(B_{N}\). Informally, the Ulam sequence is behaving a lot like an almost sum-free set, and that is what forces a big Fourier coefficient to appear.

    The last part explains what this says about the preferred Fourier phases. Those maximizing phases cannot wander all over \(\mathbb{T} = \mathbb{R}/2\pi\mathbb{Z}\); any limit point has to come from a finite spectral atom set. In the irrational case, the paper even gets a circle-factor picture and a non-uniform limiting phase distribution for the Ulam numbers and for the nearby sum-free cores. So the overall message is: assuming positive density, the Ulam sequence has real hidden harmonic structure, not just mysterious numerics.

    • Actually there's a lot of cool numerical computations in A Hidden Signal in the Ulam sequence by Steinerberger, especially related to the title hidden signal which comes from Fourier series, and here's something worth computing. I had this idea coming from Langlands program:

      Let $(a_n)_{n\ge 1}$ be the Ulam sequence with $a_1=1$, $a_2=2$, and for $n\ge 2$ let $a_{n+1}$ be the least integer $>a_n$ that has a unique representation $a_i+a_j$ with $1\le i<j\le n$. For each $n$ write $A_n=\{a_1,\dots,a_n\}$ and view $A_n$ as a subset of the cyclic group $\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z$ by choosing a prime $p>2a_n$ (so that there is no wraparound for sums in $[0,2a_n]$). Define the discrete Fourier transform of the indicator $1_{A_n}$ by $S_n(k)=\sum_{x\in A_n} e^{2\pi i kx/p}$ for $k\in\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z$.

      Numerically there is a striking ''hidden signal'': for large $n$ one finds a frequency $k_n^*\in\{1,\dots,(p-1)/2\}$ maximizing $|S_n(k)|$ such that the associated angle $\alpha_n=2\pi k_n^*/p$ stabilizes near an irrational constant $\alpha\approx 2.571447\ldots$, and the normalized peak height $|S_n(k_n^*)|/n$ remains macroscopic (around $0.7$-$0.8$ at the scales tested).

      A convenient way to package this phenomenon is via an $L$-packet (harmonic packet) hypothesis: not only is the fundamental mode $k_n^*$ large, but several harmonics $mk_n^*$ are simultaneously large, i.e. $|S_n(mk_n^*)|/n$ is far above the random baseline $n^{-1/2}$ for $1\le m\le L$. In our computations, for many values of $n$ one observes that the harmonics $m=2,3,4,5,6$ are themselves among the top few Fourier peaks and can have normalized magnitudes comparable to $0.2$-$0.6$, suggesting a genuinely structured packet rather than a single isolated spike. This connects directly with the numerical claim in arXiv1507.00267 that, at large scales, the spectrum appears to be dominated by (essentially) two peaks and their harmonic footprints; in practice one can test this by ranking the values of $|S_n(k)|$ for $1\le k\le (p-1)/2$ and tracking the ranks of the harmonic family $mk_n^*$.

      A further experiment (useful for probing whether $\alpha$ admits any simple ''closed form'' approximation) is to fit the observed maximizers $\alpha_n$ by low-complexity models suggested by the $L$-packet picture, e.g. by approximating $\theta_n=\alpha_n/(2\pi)$ with short eventually periodic continued fractions (quadratic irrationals), or by rational multiples of $\pi$. Concretely, one may compare candidate angles $\widehat\alpha$ by computing the packet statistics $n^{-1}\big|\sum_{j\le n} e^{i m\widehat\alpha a_j}\big|$ for $1\le m\le L$ together with the sign-bias $\#\{j\le n:\cos(\widehat\alpha a_j)<0\}/n$, which is near $1$ for angles tightly aligned to the hidden signal. The main point is that these computations are straightforward to reproduce (generate $A_n$, choose $p>2a_n$, FFT the indicator of $A_n$), and they provide a concrete starting point for community exploration of packet structure, harmonic stability, and possible arithmetic constraints on the limiting frequency.

  • First of all, it's worth to add 3 main references on the problem:
    [1] D. Ross, The Ulam Sequence: Structure and Randomness, PhD thesis, University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign, 2016
    [2] S. Steinerberger. A Hidden Signal in the Ulam sequence. arXiv:1507.00267, 2015.
    [3] F. Clément and S. Steinerberger. Small Gaps in the Ulam sequence. arXiv:2501.16285, 2025

    Steinerberger's results suggest the Ulam sequence exhibits a "hidden signal"—a strong Fourier peak at an irrational frequency $\alpha \approx 2.571$. Not much is known really and it seems the problem wasn't researched extensively.

    I managed to get an explanation with GPT-5.2 of why a "hidden signal" might arise and show some other heurestics towards the final answers. Below is a mean-field analysis explaining the stability of this signal and deriving a theoretical growth rate that closely matches empirical data (this is new, there was no explanation of this phenomenon before as I can see so far). You can see the full write up here.

    1. The Fourier Setup
    Let $A_n = \{a_1, \dots, a_n\}$ be the Ulam sequence. Define the Fourier sum at frequency $\alpha$ as $S_n = \sum_{j=1}^n e^{i \alpha a_j}$. Empirically, $S_n$ grows linearly along the negative real axis: $S_n \approx -c n$ with $c \approx 0.79$.

    The number of representations of an integer $m$ as a sum of two terms, $s_n(m)$, is given via Fourier inversion (dominated by the DC term and the frequency $\alpha$):\[
    s_n(m) \approx \frac{n^2}{q} + \frac{2 |S_n|^2}{q} \cos(\alpha m + \text{phase}(S_n)).
    \]For $S_n$ to be stable on the negative real axis, its phase is $\pi$. Thus:\[
    s_n(m) \approx \frac{n^2}{q} \left( 1 + 2c^2 \cos(\alpha m) \right).
    \]2. The Phase-Locking Mechanism
    The Ulam rule selects the smallest $m > a_n$ such that $s_n(m) = 2$ (uniquely representable). Since the average representation count is large ($\sim n$), finding a value as low as $2$ requires "destructive interference" from the dominant mode to cancel the baseline.\[
    1 + 2c^2 \cos(\alpha m) \le 0 \implies \cos(\alpha m) \le -\frac{1}{2c^2}.
    \]This inequality creates a feedback loop:

    Existence Threshold: For a solution to exist, the cosine must be able to dip low enough. This requires $-1/(2c^2) \ge -1$, implying $c \ge 1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707$. The empirical $c \approx 0.79$ satisfies this stability criterion.
    Phase Trapping: The new term $a_{n+1}$ is forced to have a phase $\phi = \alpha a_{n+1}$ inside a specific "trough" arc centered at $\pi$.


    3. The Self-Consistent Field Equation
    We can solve for the theoretical growth rate $c$. Let the "allowed arc" of phases be $[\pi - \delta, \pi + \delta]$.
    The boundary of this arc is defined by the threshold condition:\[
    \cos(\delta) = \frac{1}{2c^2} \quad (*).
    \]Assuming the sequence samples phases uniformly within this allowed window (due to noise from other frequencies), the growth rate of the peak $S_n$ is the average projection of these new unit vectors onto the negative real axis:\[
    c = \frac{1}{2\delta} \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} \cos(x) \, dx = \frac{\sin(\delta)}{\delta} \quad (**).
    \]4. Results and Comparison
    Solving the system of equations (*) and (**) numerically:\[
    2 \cos(\delta) \sin^2(\delta) = \delta^2
    \]yields the solution:\[
    \delta \approx 0.867 \text{ rad} \quad (49.7^\circ), \qquad c_{\text{theory}} \approx 0.879.
    \]This theoretical upper bound ($c \approx 0.88$) is remarkably close to the empirical value ($c \approx 0.79$). The discrepancy is naturally explained by "noise" ($L^2$ error terms) which effectively broadens the trapping window $\delta$, lowering the average cosine.

    5. Implications for Open Problems
    Infinitely many pairs $ a, a+2 $: Likely. The non-uniform distribution on the circle (with positive density in the support arc) implies that phases recur infinitely often in regions where the next trough (gap) is exactly 2. Since $ \alpha $ is irrational, ergodicity ensures infinite visits to such phase sets. This is conjectural but fits the quasi-periodic picture.

    Eventually periodic differences: Unlikely. The irrational rotation on the circle (modulo the trapping arc) prevents strict periodicity in gaps.

    Density zero?: No—the persistent Fourier bias and positive $ c $ are inconsistent with zero density (which would imply more random-like behavior). Computations stabilize around $ \sim 0.076 $.
