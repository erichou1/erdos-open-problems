Let $h(n)$ be such that, for any set $A\subseteq \mathbb{N}$ of size $n$, the set\[\left\{ \frac{a}{(a,b)}: a,b\in A\right\}\]has size at least $h(n)$. Estimate $h(n)$.
#539: [Er73,p.125]
additive combinatorics | number theory

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Erdős and Szemerédi proved that\[n^{1/2} \ll h(n) \ll n^{1-c}\]for some constant $c>0$. The upper bound has been improved to $h(n)\ll n^{2/3}$ by Freiman and Lev. A proof of these bounds can be found in a paper of Granville and Roesler [GrRo99].

Granville and Roesler also reformulate this problem as one in combinatorial geometry: given $A\subseteq \mathbb{Z}^d$ of size $n$ what is the minimum size possible of\[\{ \delta(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}) : \mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}\in A\}\]where $\delta(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b})$ has $i$th coordinate $\max(0,a_i-b_i)$. Using this formulation they obtained improved lower bounds in fixed small dimension $d$.

Using this reformulation, ProofCouncil has proved that\[h(n) \leq e^{O(\sqrt{\log n})} n^{1/2},\]and hence $h(n)=n^{1/2+o(1)}$.

  • During the preliminary testing/evaluation of ProofCouncil, an LLM-agent system developed for the second FirstProof challenge, we ran the system on several public Erdős problems. The system uses multiple LLMs, with the main driver being GPT 5.5 Pro. For this problem it produced the following improved bound.

    Let\[
    Q(A)=\left\{\frac{a}{\gcd(a,b)}:a,b\in A\right\},\qquad
    h(n)=\min_{|A|=n}|Q(A)|.
    \]We prove that there is an absolute constant \(C>0\) such that, for every \(n\ge 2\),\[
    \frac{1+\sqrt{8n-7}}2 \le h(n)\le n^{1/2}\exp(C\sqrt{\log n}).
    \]In particular,\[
    \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\log h(n)}{\log n}=\frac12,
    \]so this determines the dimension-free power exponent, while leaving the exact order open within a subexponential factor.

    The proof uses the Granville--Roesler positive-projection formulation. Writing\[
    D(F)=(F-F)^+,
    \]one starts from the usual two-dimensional antidiagonal strip and iterates a separated suspension construction. At suspension depth \(s\), this gives fixed-dimensional examples with exponent\[
    \alpha_s=\frac{2^{s+1}}{2^{s+2}-1}
    =\frac12+\frac{1}{2(2^{s+2}-1)}.
    \]Optimizing \(s\) as a function of \(n\) gives the displayed
    \(n^{1/2}\exp(O(\sqrt{\log n}))\) upper bound.

    A self-contained proof is in Appendix A.1 of our paper here:

    https://github.com/eth-sri/proof-council

    There is also an accompanying Lean 4 formalization in the repository.


    We also mention there that Bollobás and Leader had earlier announced unpublished joint work giving a negative answer to the \(n^{2/3}\) question. We are not aware of a published written account of that work; Appendix A.1 provides a citable self-contained proof of the stronger \(n^{1/2+o(1)}\) upper bound.

    • Nice, thanks! Like many of these proofs, the idea is simple and very natural, with the benefit of hindsight. As usual I find the AI-proof style quite grating to read (in particular the overuse of catchy phrases like 'positive-projection' and 'separated suspension'). This is just a difference of culture - human mathematics does introduce helpful phrases like this of course, but much less often, generally reserving it for the really important stuff.

      Also I don't think one needs to start from any two-dimensional strip, just starting from something trivial like $\{0\}$ suffices. Here is my sketch of the construction, after simplifying/rewriting for my own understanding:

      In the equivalent formuation of Granville and Roesler, we want to construct some $A\subset \mathbb{Z}^d$ (for some $d$) of size $n$ such that $D(A) = \{ (x-y)^+ : x,y\in A\}$ has size $\lvert D(A)\rvert \leq n^{1/2+o(1)}$.

      This is done iteratively. Let $K$ be some large integer, and let $A_0=\{0\}$. In general, let\[A_{k+1}=\{ (j, x+jM,y-jM) : 0\leq j<K\textrm{ and }x,y\in A_k\},\]where $M$ is some large integer (maybe depending on $A_k$) and e.g. $x+jM$ means $(x_1+jM,\ldots,x_d+jM)$. It is trivial that $\lvert A_{k+1}\rvert= K\lvert A_k\rvert^2$ and\[\lvert A_{k+1}-A_{k+1}\rvert \ll K\lvert A_k-A_k\rvert^2.\]Furthermore, provided $M$ is large enough,\[\lvert D_{k+1}\rvert\leq \lvert D_k\rvert^2+2K\lvert A_k-A_k\rvert.\](The first summand is where the $j$-component is the same, and otherwise the $^+$ operation ensures half of the coordinates are $0$.)

      Thus after $t$ iterations we have a set $A$ of size $\lvert A\rvert=K^{2^{t}-1}$ and $\lvert A-A\rvert \leq O(K)^{2^t-1}$, and so\[\lvert D(A)\rvert \leq O(K)^{2^{t-1}} \leq O(1)^{2^t}K^{1/2}\lvert A\rvert^{1/2}.\]To optimise we choose $K\asymp \exp(\sqrt{\log n})$ and $2^t\asymp \sqrt{\log n}$, so that $\lvert A\rvert =n$ and\[\lvert D(A)\rvert \leq \exp(O(\sqrt{\log n})) n^{1/2}.\]

  • Congratulations to the ProofCouncil team - Theorem A.1 is a beautiful resolution of the dimension-free exponent. I would like to record how it interacts with the two extrapolations (P1) and (P2) from my comment of 22 May, and to point out a curious structural coincidence that may be worth pursuing.

    1. The dichotomy is now half-settled. In my earlier comment I noted that HLP's closing speculation (P1: $e_k = 2/3$ uniformly in $k$, i.e. "$\delta = 1/6$ will do") and the pattern fit (P2: $e_k = E_k := d_k/(2d_k-1)$ with $d_k = \binom{k}{\lfloor k/2\rfloor}$) make contradictory predictions for $k \ge 3$. Since $e_k$ is non-increasing in $k$ (a set in $\mathbb{Z}^k$ embeds in $\mathbb{Z}^{k+1}$ without changing $D(F)$), the depth-one suspension - which lives in $\mathbb{Z}^5$ and has exponent $4/7$ - gives\[ e_k \le 4/7 < 2/3 \quad \text{for all } k \ge 5, \]so (P1) is refuted for $k\ge5$ (and asymptotically: the dimension-free exponent is $1/2$, not $2/3$). The qualitative negative answer to the $n^{2/3}$ question was, as TimG and the ProofCouncil paper note, already announced in unpublished joint work of Bollobás and Leader (Leader's 2009 Warwick and 2012 Oxford "Positive Projections" abstracts); what Theorem A.1 adds for the present purpose is the explicit fixed-dimensional exponent $4/7$ in $\mathbb{Z}^5$, which is what pins the refutation of (P1) to $k\ge5$ quantitatively. For $k = 3, 4$ the question ($e_3 \in [3/5, 2/3]$, $e_4 \in [6/11, e_3]$) remains open. Meanwhile (P2) is untouched: its prediction $e_5 = 10/19 \approx 0.526$ sits inside the new window $[1/2, 4/7]$, and its asymptote $E_k \to 1/2$ now matches the proven dimension-free exponent (two logically independent facts - the match supports the fit only in the weak sense of not refuting it).

    2. A structural coincidence. The separated-suspension step sends a construction with $|F| \asymp W^a$, $|D(F)| \asymp W^b$ to one with parameters $(a, b) \mapsto (2a+1, 2b)$. This map preserves the relation $a = 2b - 1$. Since the base strip has $(a,b) = (3,2)$, every iterated-suspension exponent automatically has the form\[ \alpha = \frac{b}{2b-1}, \qquad b = 2^{s+1}, \]i.e. the proven fixed-dimensional exponents all lie on the curve $x \mapsto x/(2x-1)$ - which is exactly the functional form of $E_k = d_k/(2d_k - 1)$. Moreover the base strip itself is the $k=2$ member of the conjectured family: $(a, b) = (3, 2) = (2d_2 - 1, d_2)$. So the remaining disagreement between the proven bounds and (P2) is only about how large $b$ can be taken in a given dimension: iterated suspension achieves $b = 2^{s+1}$ in dimension $3\cdot 2^s - 1$, while (P2) amounts to the assertion that dimension $k$ admits $b = d_k$, the width of the Boolean lattice (maximum antichain) on $k$ coordinates.

    3. A concrete test case. In this language (P2) becomes a clean construction problem: does there exist $F \subseteq \mathbb{Z}^3$ with $|F| \asymp W^5$ and $|D(F)| \ll W^3$ (as $W \to \infty$)? If yes, then $h_3(n) \ll n^{3/5}$, which together with the HLP lower bound $h_3(n) \gg n^{3/5}$ would give the first exact fixed-dimension exponent beyond $k=2$: $e_3 = 3/5$ - and would be strong evidence for the antichain-width reading of the pattern. If instead one can push the suspension machinery (or any other construction) below $n^{3/5}$ in dimension 3, then (P2) dies at $k=3$, just as it would die at $k=5$ if $e_5 \ne 10/19$.

    As before: (P2) is a pattern fit, not a theorem, and the Sperner-width interpretation I attached to it remains speculative. But Theorem A.1 at least shows that the true fixed-dimensional exponents live on the same one-parameter curve, and kills the competing uniform-$2/3$ extrapolation from $k = 5$ onwards.

  • Define $E_k := \binom{k}{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor} / (2\binom{k}{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor} - 1)$. Then $E_2 = 2/3$, $E_3 = 3/5$ and $E_4 = 6/11$ — matching exactly the three currently known restricted-prime exponents for $h_k(n)$ from Granville–Roesler and Holzman–Lev–Pinchasi.

    This 3-of-3 pattern fit suggests the conjecture $e_k = E_k$ for every $k \ge 2$, with the (so far untested) predictions $e_5 = 10/19$ and $e_6 = 20/39$, and the asymptotic $E_k \to 1/2$ matching the universal Erdős–Szemerédi lower bound $h(n) \gg n^{1/2}$.

    I have no proof, only the numerical fit and a Sperner-width heuristic relating $d_k = \binom{k}{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor}$ to the largest level of the divisibility poset on $\{0,1\}^k$. A short note (7 pages) with the symbolic verification (sympy) and forum-cleanliness search log is at: <https://czechu.blog/erdos/problems/539/proof.pdf>.

    If $e_5 = 10/19$ turns out to be wrong, the conjecture is dead at $k=5$ and the fit at $k=2,3,4$ becomes coincidental — so this is concretely falsifiable on the next data point.

    Follow-up to my comment of 22 May 2026 proposing $E_k = \binom{k}{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor}/(2\binom{k}{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor} - 1)$.

    After re-reading Holzman–Lev–Pinchasi (2008) carefully and testing the natural "Sperner-level" construction $\{a \in \{0,\ldots,M\}^k : \sum a_i = \lfloor k/2 \rfloor M\}$ numerically, I want to weaken the original claim in two ways:

    **(1)** The "Sperner-width heuristic" I cited has no empirical support. For $k = 2$ the Sperner-level slice gives $|Q(V)|/|V| > 1$ (worse than a multiplicative box, which is itself far from the Freiman–Lev $n^{2/3}$ extremal — that one is a thin strip $|x_1 + x_2 - N| < \delta$, structurally unrelated to Sperner antichains). So $d_k = \binom{k}{\lfloor k/2 \rfloor}$ is not visibly the right combinatorial quantity, even though the formula happens to fit three data points.

    **(2)** HLP themselves end their introduction with a competing speculation: *"it is even possible that $\delta = 1/6$ will do"*, i.e., $e_k = 2/3$ uniformly in $k$. My original comment did not mention this. Under HLP's own speculation, $e_5 = 2/3 \neq 10/19$, so HLP and my pattern make contradictory predictions for $k \ge 3$.

    The honest summary: the values $2/3, 3/5, 6/11$ in HLP are *proved lower bounds*, not known exponents. Two natural extrapolations to general $k$ exist:

    - **(P1)** HLP's $e_k = 2/3$ for all $k$ (their stated speculation; matches Freiman–Lev upper bound uniformly).
    - **(P2)** $e_k = d_k/(2 d_k - 1)$ (my fit; matches HLP's *proved LBs*; predicts $e_5 = 10/19$).

    These are mutually exclusive on $k \ge 3$. I have no proof of either, and the Sperner-width interpretation I attached to (P2) was empirically unfounded — (P2) should be treated as a pure pattern fit on three points. Whoever first computes $e_5$ rigorously settles the disagreement.

  • The problem has been studied in the meantime and the best known bounds are\[ n^{1/2} \ll h(n) \ll n^{2/3}. \]Both are easy and are elaborated in this paper by Granville and Roesler from 1999. The lower bound is, as stated, probably an observation of Erdős and Szemerédi, while for the upper bound the authors credit Freiman and Lev.
    Granville and Roesler nicely reformulate the problem as a problem in combinatorial geometry, which is also the viewpoint of Holzman, Lev, and Pinchasi. The two papers also showed (in number theoretic language) that:
    If $A$ consists of numbers with prime divisors $2,3$, then $h_2(n)\gg n^{2/3}$.
    If $A$ consists of numbers with prime divisors $2,3,5$, then $h_3(n)\gg n^{3/5}$.
    If $A$ consists of numbers with prime divisors $2,3,5,7$, then $h_4(n)\gg n^{6/11-\epsilon}$.
