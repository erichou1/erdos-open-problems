If $A\subseteq \mathbb{N}$ is such that $A+A$ contains all but finitely many integers then $\limsup 1_A\ast 1_A(n)=\infty$.

Conjectured by Erdős and Turán. They also suggest the stronger conjecture that $\limsup 1_A\ast 1_A(n)/\log n>0$.

Another stronger conjecture would be that the hypothesis $\lvert A\cap [1,N]\rvert \gg N^{1/2}$ for all large $N$ suffices.

See also [40], and [1145] for a stronger generalisation.

This is discussed in problem C9 of Guy's collection [Gu04].

    • Partially, yes. If we allow $0 \in A$ (as otherwise $1$ is not representable), then the conjectures are indeed equivalent. But to me the modified conjecture does not sound better, to be honest. When considering additive bases (e.g. Waring's problem or Goldbach's conjecture) one generally only cares about being able to represent large enough integers. Perhaps because of that, the assumption $\mathbb{N} \subseteq A + A$ feels at first glance unusually strong to me, weakening the (perceived) strength of the conjecture. Even though, as you note, they are actually equivalent. But as so often in maths; there's many ways to prove or state a result, and it sometimes just comes down to personal preference :)

      By the way, in this regard it is interesting to note that for the Erdős and Sárközy conjecture (Edit: which has since been moved to #1145) the equality $A + B = \mathbb{N}$ is written.
