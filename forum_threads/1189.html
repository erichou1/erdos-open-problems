Call a set of distinct integers $1<n_1<\cdots<n_k$ a covering set if there is a choice of $a_i\pmod{n_i}$ for $1\leq i\leq k$ such that every integer satisfies at least one of these congruences. A set is an irreducible covering set if no proper subset is a covering set.

How many irreducible covering sets of size $k$ are there?

What is the minimum and maximum that $n_k$ can be?

Determine or estimate $\max \sum\frac{1}{n_i}$, where the maximum ranges over all irreducible covering sets of size $k$.

Are there infinitely many $n$ such that the divisors of $n$ (which are $>1$) form an irreducible covering set?

This is subtly different from the (more usually studied) notion of minimal covering system: an irreducible covering set corresponds to the set of moduli of a minimal covering system, but not necessarily vice versa.

The divisors of $12$ (which are $>1$) are an example of an irreducible covering set. Sun [Su07] proved more generally that, for any odd prime $p$, the divisors (which are $>1$) of $2^{p-1}p$ form an irreducible covering set, settling the final question.

Simpson [Si85] proved $n_k\leq 2^{k-1}$.

Let $I(k)$ count the number of irreducible covering sets of size $k$. Balister, Bollobás, Morris, Sahasrabudhe, and Tiba [BBMST24] proved that\[I(k) \leq \exp\left((c+o(1))\frac{k^{3/2}}{(\log k)^{1/2}}\right)\]by proving that the right-hand side is an asymptotic for the number of minimal covering systems on $k$ moduli.

It is trivial that $\sum\frac{1}{n_i}>1$ for any irreducible covering set.

See also [1188].

  • This is certainly not new, but to get close to Simpson's upper bound, let $k \ge 5$, set $a_i = 2^{i-1}$ with $n_i = 2^{i}$ for $1 \le i \le k-4$. Then for the final four congruences we take
    \begin{align*}
    a_{k-3} &= 0, \qquad & n_{k-3} &= 3 \cdot 2^{k-5}, \\
    a_{k-2} &= 0, \qquad & n_{k-2} &= 2^{k-3}, \\
    a_{k-1} &= 2^{k-4}, \qquad & n_{k-1} &= 3 \cdot 2^{k-4}, \\
    a_{k} &= 5 \cdot 2^{k-4}, \qquad & n_k &= 3 \cdot 2^{k-3}.
    \end{align*}This gives $n_k \ge 3 \cdot 2^{k-3}$, and I expect that the optimal $n_k$ does exist somewhere in the literature.

    EDIT 2026-06-21: A fairly easy argument actually gives that $3 \cdot 2^{k-3}$ is optimal. I'll try to write up the details shortly.

      • I think this example is irreducible, but cannot wholly prove it. Let $n=\prod_{i=1}^t p_i^{\alpha_i}$ and $f(n)=\sum_{i=1}^t \alpha_i(p_i-1)$. Then the Simpson paper proves that if $A=\{a_i\bmod d_i: 1\leq i\leq k\}$ is a minimal covering system, then $|A|\geq f(P)+1$ where $P=lcm(d_1,\ldots,d_k)$.

        Assume the example above is not irreducible. Then it is possible to remove a modulus, reassign the residues to the remaining moduli, and create a new covering system. IF the residues can be assigned in a way such that the resulting sytem is minimal, then this is a minimal covering system $A'$. Whichever modulus you remove, the lcm of the moduli is the same as before. However, $A'$ fails the inequality $|A'|\geq f(P)+1$.

        Given a minimal, non irreducible covering system, is it always possible to remove a modulus and form a minimal covering system with the remaining moduli?

      • The congruence classes mod $2, 4, \ldots, 2^{k-3}$ (that is, using the moduli $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_{k-4}, n_{k-2}$) together leave at least one residue class modulo $2^{k-3}$ uncovered. That is, we have at least three residue classes modulo $3 \cdot 2^{k-3}$, say $a, a+2^{k-3}, a+2^{k-2} \pmod{3 \cdot 2^{k-3}}$ still uncovered. But each of the three remaining moduli $3 \cdot 2^{k-5}$, $3 \cdot 2^{k-4}$, $3 \cdot 2^{k-3}$ (ie $n_{k-3}, n_{k-1}, n_k$) can only cover at most one of the three still uncovered classes. So the example actually does constitue an irreducible set.

        And in any case, even if it weren't irreducible, because of the required inequality $\sum \frac{1}{n_i} > 1$, one can check that you can never remove $n_i$ for $i \le k-3$ in this case. So without even verifying whether it's irreducible, any irreducible subset would still give the lower bound $n_k \gg 2^k$.

        • Yes, I was just thinking of this. There must be an irreducible subset of $A$, which would be the moduli of a minimal covering system and so satisfy Simpson's inequality. If $P:=lcm(n_1,\ldots,n_k)$ then $f(P)+1=k$. Therefore the lcm of any proper subset of $A$ which form the moduli of a minimal covering system must be $<P$.

          You cannot remove any but the largest three moduli while keeping the sum of the reciprocals of the remaining moduli greater than 1. If all three of the largest moduli are removed, then the sum of the reciprocals of the remaining moduli is less than 1. The only other proper subset of $A$ which is created by removing a subset of the three largest moduli and has lcm less than $P$ is $A\backslash\{n_{k-2},n_k\}$, which has lcm $3\times 2^{k-4}$, but this has $k-2$ moduli and $f(3\times 2^{k-4})+1=k-1$.
