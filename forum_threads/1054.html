Let $f(n)$ be the minimal integer $m$ such that $n$ is the sum of the $k$ smallest divisors of $m$ for some $k\geq 1$.

Is it true that $f(n)=o(n)$? Or is this true only for almost all $n$, and $\limsup f(n)/n=\infty$?
#1054: [Gu04]
number theory | divisors

A question of Erdős reported in problem B2 of Guy's collection [Gu04]. The function $f(n)$ is undefined for $n=2$ and $n=5$, but is likely well-defined for all $n\geq 6$ (which would follow from a strong form of Goldbach's conjecture).

The sequence of values of $f(n)$ is given by A167485 in the OEIS.

See also [468].

The strong claim that $f(n)=o(n)$ was disproved by Tao in the comments to [468], in which he proves that the upper density of $\{ n : f(n)\leq \delta n\}$ is $\ll \delta^2$.

    Let \(f(N)\) be the least positive integer \(m\) such that \(N\) is the sum of the \(k\) smallest positive divisors of \(m\), for some \(k\geq 1\). We prove that, for every fixed \(A\geq 1\), the set of \(N\) satisfying\[
    f(N)>AN
    \]has positive lower density. Consequently,\[
    \limsup_{N\to\infty}\frac{f(N)}{N}=\infty.
    \]Here is the latex solution, as well as the Lean formalization of our claim. The Lean file isolates two classical analytic inputs that do not appear to be available in Mathlib: a quantitative Mertens product estimate and an almost-all binary Goldbach theorem. These are stated as explicit assumptions rather than formalized from first principles.

    I think one can disprove that $f(N) = o(N)$ for all $N$ as follows. Let $\sigma_k(n)$ denote the sum of the divisors of $n$ excluding the top $k$ factors (or $0$, if the number of divisors $\tau(n)$ is at most $k$). It is a standard calculation (factoring $n=dm$) that
    $$ \sum_{n \leq x} \sigma(n) = \sum_{d \leq x} d \sum_{m \leq x/d} 1 \ll \sum_{d \leq x} x \ll x^2,$$
    so $\sigma(n) = O(x)$ on the average. Suppose that we can more generally show
    $$ \sum_{n \leq x} \sigma_k(n) \ll x^2 / k^{1.1} \quad (1)$$
    for all $k \geq 1$. By Markov's inequality and the union bound, and the summability of $\sum_k 1/k^{1.1}$, we conclude that for any $C_0$, the number of $n \leq x$ such that $\sigma_k(n) \geq C_0 x$ for at least one $k$ is at most $O( x / C_0 )$, hence we have $f(N) \leq x$ for at most $O(x/C_0)$ of the $N \in [C_0 x, 2C_0 x]$, which is enough to disprove $f(N) = o(N)$ by taking $C_0$ large enough.

    It remains to prove (1). In order for a factor $d$ of $n=dm$ to contribute to $\sigma_k(n)$, there must be at least $k$ factors of $dm$ that are at most $m$, so either $\tau(m) \gg k^{0.1}$ or $d$ has $\gg k^{0.9}$ factors of size at most $m$. The contribution of the first case is at most
    $$ \sum_{m \leq x} (\tau(m)/k^{0.1})^{100} \sum_{d \leq x/m} d \ll \frac{x^2}{k^{10}} \sum_m \frac{\tau(m)^{100}}{m^2} \ll \frac{x^2}{k^{10}}$$
    which is acceptable (one could also have used the divisor bound $\tau(m) = m^{o(1)}$ here). The contribution of the second case is at most
    $$ \sum_{m \leq x} \sum_{d \leq x/m} d (\sum_{r \leq m: r|d} 1 / k^{0.9})^2$$
    $$ \ll \sum_{m } \sum_{r_1,r_2 \leq m} \frac{x^2}{m^2 [r_1,r_2] k^{1.8}}$$
    $$ \ll \sum_{m } \sum_{r_1,r_2} \frac{x^2}{m^{1.8} [r_1,r_2]^{1.1} k^{1.8}}$$
    $$ \ll \frac{x^2}{k^{1.8}}$$
    which is also acceptable (there is a lot of room to spare here ultimately coming from the fact that divisor functions are typically logarithmic in size, so presumably one could clean up this argument somehow; in particular, standard anatomy of integers heuristics predicts that (1) should in fact exhibit exponential decay in $k$).

The "trivial" bound controls each individual event $f_k(N) \leq x$, but the event $f(N) \leq x$ is the union of all such events and so one needs to sum in $k$ if one wishes to use the union bound.

  • I think the well-definedness issue can be closed, using the published constants from Helfgott and Rosser-Schoenfeld, I compiled a small verifier in this repo.

    Let \(d_1(m)<d_2(m)<\cdots\) be the divisors of \(m\). Say that \(n\) is represented if \(n=d_1(m)+\cdots+d_k(m)\) for some \(m,k\).

    The basic gadget is this:
    If \(M<p_1<\cdots<p_t\le X<M^2\) are primes, then for \(m=p_1\cdots p_t\), the divisors begin \(1,p_1,\ldots,p_t\), because every composite divisor is \(>M^2\). More generally, any subset sum \(N=p_{i_1}+\cdots+p_{i_s}\) of primes in \((M,X]\) gives a representation of \(N+1\), by taking \(m=p_{i_1}\cdots p_{i_s}\).

    The other elementary ingredient is the usual interval-extension trick. If subset sums cover \([C,U]\), and the next prime \(p\) satisfies \(p\le U-C+1\), then after adding \(p\) they cover \([C,U+p]\), since \([C,U]\) overlaps \([C+p,U+p]\).

    The verifier script checks these pieces (see the repo for instructions to run it and versioned output).

    1. A direct \(Bq\) divisor-prefix search covers every \(6\le n\le10\,000\,000\), except \(7\). The remaining value is \(7=1+2+4\), represented by \(m=4\).

    2. A first prime-window certificate with \(M=10\,000\) and \(X=99\,000\,000<M^2\) gives representations for every \(469\,616\le n\le 273\,803\,744\,799\,154.\)

    3. A larger seed proves every integer in \([105\,000\,000,156\,000\,000]\) is a sum of distinct primes in \((20\,000\,000,40\,000\,000)\). The seed length is \(51\,000\,001\), while the next prime after \(40\,
    000\,000\) is \(40\,000\,003\), so the interval-extension induction starts. After that it continues by Bertrand's postulate: once a prime \(p\) has been adjoined, the interval length is at least \(2p\), and the next
    prime is \(<2p\).

    Using Rosser--Schoenfeld bounds for \(\pi(x)\), the verifier lower-bounds the prime mass up to \(X=399\,000\,000\,000\,000<20\,000\,000^2\). This gives representations for every\[
    105\,000\,001\le n\le
    1\,111\,351\,202\,532\,220\,892\,436\,000\,001.
    \]4. For the tail, I use an explicit consequence of Helfgott's ternary Goldbach proof: every odd \(N\ge 10^{27}\) is a sum of three distinct odd primes, each \(>N/(30000\log N)\). In Helfgott's Section 7 weighted odd-prime triple count, the lower bound is at least \(0.000422N^2\); we only need the weaker margin \(0.0002443N^2\). Using the published sup-norm bounds for the weights and Rosser-Schoenfeld's bound \(\sum_{n\le x}\Lambda(n)\le 1.03883x\), the verifier checks that triples with a coordinate \(\le N/(30000\log N)\) contribute \(<0.000178N^2\), and triples with a repeated coordinate contribute \(<10^{-20}N^2\). So at least one distinct prime triple remains with all three primes above \(N/(30000\log N)\).

    For even \(n\), write \(n-1=p+q+r\). Since \(pq>n-1>r\), the number \(m=pqr\) has initial divisors \(1,p,q,r\) and represents \(n\).

    For odd \(n\), choose a prime \(60000\log n<\ell<120000\log n\), and write \(n-1-\ell=p+q+r\). For \(n\ge 10^{27}+10^8\), the verifier checks the slack ensuring \(\ell<p\) and \(\ell p>r\). So \(m=\ell pqr\) has initial divisors \(1,\ell,p,q,r\) and represents \(n\).

    For odd \(n\), by Bertrand's postulate choose a prime \(60000\log n<\ell<120000\log n\), and write \(N=n-1-\ell\). Since \(\ell<10^8-1\) in this range, \(N\) is odd and at least \(10^{27}\), so the Helfgott consequence applies; write \(N=p+q+r\). For \(n\ge 10^{27}+10^8\), the verifier checks the slack ensuring \(\ell<p\) and \(\ell p>r\). So \(m=\ell pqr\) has initial divisors \(1,\ell,p,q,r\) and represents \(n\).

    So the covered intervals are:

    - \([6,10\,000\,000]\);
    - \([469\,616,273\,803\,744\,799\,154]\);
    - \([105\,000\,001,
    1\,111\,351\,202\,532\,220\,892\,436\,000\,001]\);
    - \([10^{27}+10^8,\infty)\).

    They overlap, so every \(n\ge6\) is represented.

    Finally, \(2\) and \(5\) really are impossible. The first prefix is \(1\), and any longer prefix is at least \(1+2=3\), so \(2\) is impossible. For \(5\), a two-term prefix would have to be \(1+4\), but \(4\) cannot be the smallest divisor after \(1\); with at least three terms the sum is already at least \(6\). Also \(1,3,4\) are represented by \(m=1,2,3\).

    Thus the represented positive integers are exactly \(\mathbb N\setminus \{2,5\}\).

    References used:
    Helfgott, "The ternary Goldbach conjecture is true", Section 7.
    Rosser--Schoenfeld, "Approximate formulas for some functions of prime numbers".

  • This was disproved (quite strongly) by Terry Tao in the comments on [468], where he showed that the upper density of all $\{n\in\mathbb{N} : f(n)\leq \delta n\}$ is $\ll \delta^2$ (in a slightly different but equivalent formulation).

    Namely, denoting by $\sigma_k(n)$ the sum of divisors of $n$ excluding the $k$ largest ones, Terry proved that\[ \sum_{k\geq 0} \sum_{n\leq x} \sigma_k(n) \ll x^2. \]Therefore, for any $x,C>0$ we get
    \begin{align*}
    & \# \{N\geq Cx : f(N)\leq x\} \\
    & \leq \# \{(n,k) : n\leq x, k\geq0, \sigma_k(n)\geq Cx\} \\
    & = \sum_k \# \{ n\leq x : \sigma_k(n)\geq Cx\} \\
    & \ll \frac{1}{Cx} \sum_{k\geq0} \sum_{n\leq x} \sigma_k(n) \ll \frac{x}{C}.
    \end{align*}
