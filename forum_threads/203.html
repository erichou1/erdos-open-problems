Is there an integer $m\geq 1$ with $(m,6)=1$ such that none of $2^k3^\ell m+1$ are prime, for any $k,\ell\geq 0$?
#203: [ErGr80,p.27]
primes | covering systems
The open status of this problem reflects the current belief of the owner of this website. There may be literature on this problem that I am unaware of, which may partially or completely solve the stated problem. There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Positive odd integers $m$ such that none of $2^km+1$ are prime are called Sierpinski numbers - see [1113] for more details.

Erdős and Graham also ask more generally about $p_1^{k_1}\cdots p_r^{k_r}m+1$ for distinct primes $p_i$, or $q_1\cdots q_rm+1$ where the $q_i$ are primes congruent to $1\pmod{4}$. (Dogmachine has noted in the comments this latter question has the trivial answer $m=1$ - perhaps some condition such as $m$ even is meant.)

  • I have been studying the \(r=2\) case of this problem: whether there exists
    \(m\geq 1\), with \(\gcd(m,6)=1\), such that\[
    2^k3^\ell m+1
    \]is composite for every \(k,\ell\geq 0\).

    I am not claiming a solution. The existence question remains open. My claim is a
    partial result: a general density obstruction for finite 2D covering sets, plus
    some computationally bounded exclusions.

    For each prime \(p\nmid 6\), define\[
    H_p=\langle 2,3\rangle\subseteq(\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^\times .
    \]I prove\[
    |H_p|=\operatorname{lcm}(\operatorname{ord}_p(2),\operatorname{ord}_p(3)).
    \]It follows that if a finite set of primes \(P\) gives a covering proof for some
    \(m\), i.e. if for every \(k,\ell\geq 0\) some \(p\in P\) divides\[
    2^k3^\ell m+1,
    \]then necessarily\[
    \sum_{p\in P}\frac{1}{|H_p|}\geq 1.
    \]Using this density condition, I rule out several possible covering regimes,
    including the four-prime set \(\{5,11,23,13\}\), all linear primes with
    \(\operatorname{ord}_p(2)\leq 60\), and linear-prime coverings with\[
    \operatorname{lcm}(\operatorname{ord}_p(2))\leq 720720.
    \]The strongest current computational exclusion is: among primes
    \(p\leq 50{,}000{,}000\), no covering set \(P\) with\[
    \operatorname{lcm}(|H_p|:p\in P)<5040
    \]can exist, since the corresponding density sum is still \(<1\). This is a
    computational theorem within that prime bound, not an unconditional theorem over
    all primes.

    I also checked natural candidate families at moduli \(5040\) and \(10080\). No
    covering was found; the best coverage stayed around \(71\%-72\%\). This is
    computational evidence only, not a proof of nonexistence.

    A useful structural observation is that any such 2D Sierpiński number would also
    be a classical 1D Sierpiński number, by setting \(\ell=0\). The natural candidate
    \(m=78557\) does not work, since\[
    2^1\cdot 3^2\cdot 78557+1=1414027
    \]is prime.

    So the exact status is:

    - Proved: the subgroup formula for \(|H_p|\).
    - Proved: the density necessary condition for finite 2D covering sets.
    - Proved/computed within scope: no covering with \(\operatorname{lcm}(|H_p|)<5040\) using primes \(p\leq 50{,}000{,}000\).
    - Not proved: existence of \(m\).
    - Not proved: nonexistence of \(m\).
    - Therefore the original \(r=2\) problem remains open.

  • Note that the question regarding the primes $q$ is equivalent to asking for an even positive integer $m$ such that the quadratic form $g(x,y)=mx^2+my^2+1$ is free of primes whenever $x,y$ are coprime positive integers and $x+y$ odd. If we remove such restrictions and simply allows any Integers as $x,y$, such $m$ does not exist by classical results of Iwaniec (see H. Iwaniec, Primes representes by quadratic polynomials in two variables, Acta Arith. 24 (1974), 435-459), since the form $g$ obeys some caveats, including being genuinely a function of two variables, that force It to contain primes. Perhaps someone well acquainted with the analytic theory can prove such $g$ will have primes even with the aforementioned restrictions on $x,y$?

  • The belief Dogmachine alludes to was made explicit in this paper by Filaseta, Finch and Kozek. They conjecture the following:

    Conjecture. If $m$ is such that $2^km + 1$ is composite for all $k \ge 1$, then either $m$ is a perfect power, or the smallest prime factor of $2^km + 1$ remains bounded as $k$ tends to infinity.

    They furthermore show the following result, which is somewhat related to the problem at hand:

    Theorem. For every $l \in \mathbb{N}$ there is an $m$ such that $2^km^i+1$ is composite for all $1 \le i \le l$ and all $k \in \mathbb{N}$.
