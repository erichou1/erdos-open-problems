Let $F(n)$ be the maximum possible size of a subset $A\subseteq\{1,\ldots,N\}$ such that the products $ab$ are distinct for all $a<b$. Is there a constant $c$ such that\[F(n)=\pi(n)+(c+o(1))n^{3/4}(\log n)^{-3/2}?\]If $A\subseteq \{1,\ldots,n\}$ is such that all products $a_1\cdots a_r$ are distinct for $a_1<\cdots <a_r$ then is it true that\[\lvert A\rvert \leq \pi(n)+O(n^{\frac{r+1}{2r}})?\]
#425: [Er69,p.78][Er70b,p.136][Er73,p.131][Er77,p.40][Er77c,p.67][Er80,p.102][ErGr80,p.84]

There are claims of partial results and/or a path to a solution in the comments.
Erdős [Er68] proved that there exist some constants $0<c_1\leq c_2$ such that\[\pi(n)+c_1 n^{3/4}(\log n)^{-3/2}\leq F(n)\leq \pi(n)+c_2 n^{3/4}(\log n)^{-3/2}.\](The lower bound was proved earlier in [Er38].)

This problem can also be considered in the real numbers: that is, what is the size of the the largest $A\subset [1,x]$ such that for any distinct $a,b,c,d\in A$ we have $\lvert ab-cd\rvert \geq 1$? Erdős had conjectured (see [Er73] and [Er77c]) that $\lvert A\rvert=o(x)$.

In [ErGr80] Erdős and Graham report that Alexander had given a construction disproving this conjecture, establishing that $\lvert A\rvert\gg x$ is possible. Alexander's construction is given in [Er80], and we sketch a simplified version. Let $B\subseteq [1,X^2]$ be a Sidon set of integers of size $\gg X$ and\[A=\{ X e^{b/X^2} : b\in B\}.\]It is easy to check that\[\lvert ab-cd\rvert \geq X^2\lvert 1-e^{1/X^2}\rvert \gg 1\]for distinct $a,b,c,d\in A$, and (after rescaling $A$ by some constant factor) this produces a set of size $\gg X$ with the desired property in the interval $[X,O(X)]$. Furthermore, a simple modification allows for $A$ to also be $1$-separated.

In [Er77c] Erdős considers a similar generalisation for sets of complex numbers or complex integers.


See also [490], [793], and [796].

  • I want to share an attempt report for the following claim, with Lean 4, assuming only PNT.
    Claim: For every fixed $c<3.499$ and all sufficiently large $n$, $F(n)\ge\pi(n)+c\frac{n^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}}$.
    Lean4Web: click here (PNT enters as a named hypothesis; everything else is verified on the spot).
    Github: KitaKen1/erdos-425-lower-bound-3499-attempt.

    The proof is intended to improve the lower coefficient $2.95$ in the bounds displayed in Woett's comment of 17:01 on 17 May 2026 above, by an incremental refinement of the underlying layered projective-plane construction.
    The construction works in the stronger product-Sidon convention (all products $ab$ with $a\le b$ distinct), hence implies the strict-pair version of #425.

    The ingredients are the same as in the constructions above: PNT counting of primes near $\sqrt n$, and $C_4$-free graphs from projective planes (a semiprime collision $(pq)(rs)=(ps)(rq)$ is a $C_4$). Two pieces are upgraded, per layer: (i) the bipartite point–line incidence block becomes the non-bipartite polarity graph $ER_q$ on the union of the layer's lower and upper prime windows, so lower–lower semiprimes are harvested too; (ii) the block is no longer confined to a complete rectangle of layer pairs — an averaging embedding lets it use every pair under the hyperbola $pq\le n$. Both upgrades matter: $\Lambda=2^{11/4}/3^{3/4}=2.9511\ldots$ is sharp within the rectangular layered ansatz (see my 06 Jun comment in this thread).

    A band with windows $(a\sqrt n,b\sqrt n]$, $(c\sqrt n,d\sqrt n]$ then contributes $G=\frac{\sqrt2}{\sqrt{P+D}}(P^2+2J)$ in units of $n^{3/4}/(\log n)^{3/2}$, where $P=b-a$, $D=d-c$, $J=\int_c^d(\min(b,x^{-1})-a)_+\,dx$. Twelve bands paired in reverse order plus a small tail give $C_{12}=3.49901\ldots>3.499$; the formal proof runs through a uniform 20-band variant with $C_{20}=3.49901\ldots>3.499$. The full chain — structural theorem, $ER_q$, the embedding, gluing, the derivation of all counts from PNT, and the $\varepsilon$-management — is laid out in the repository README and the Lean files.

  • The lower bound was actually proven in this 1938 paper by Erdős, using a result of Klein. In order to get both the known lower and upper bounds formalized by Aristotle, I asked ChatGPT to write up the proofs and further optimize them as much as possible. The result is that here you can find a formalization that gives $$F(n) > \pi(n) + \frac{cn^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}}$$for all $$c < \frac{2^{11/4}}{3^{3/4}} \approx 2.95$$and all large enough $n$. This formalization depends on the prime number theorem and the end result is about two orders of magnitude better than the $c = \frac{1}{36}$ that Erdős claims in his paper. Besides PNT no other external results are required.

The plan is to formalize a proof that $$F(n) < \pi(n) + \frac{cn^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}}$$holds for all $c$ larger than some constant $C_* < 13.1$ and all large enough $n$, but I haven't quite managed to finish off this project.     • Interesting result. Can you describe the construction in some detail, please? I have been thinking about this problem in recent weeks and I only got $c\approx 1.703$ with a rather convoluted adjustment to Erdős's 1938 construction. Here is a sketch of a simple argument which gives a lower bound with $c=2^{3/4}\approx 1.681$, based on Erdős's 1938 paper :
      Take sets $A_1,A_2$ formed respectively of the primes below $\sqrt{n/2}$, and between $\sqrt{n/2}$ and $\sqrt{2n}$, so that $A_1 A_2 \subset \{1, \cdots,n\}$. By the PNT, these sets have approximately equal cardinality $M=\dfrac{\sqrt{2n}}{\log n}$. We form a projective plane of order $\approx M$ on $A_1$, and to each element $q$ of $A_2$ we associate a line of this plane, then form the products $pq$ for the $\approx\sqrt{M}$ primes $p$ on this line. There are $\approx M^{3/2}=\dfrac{2^{3/4}n^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}}$ such products in total, to which we append the primes above $\sqrt{2n}$. The set so constructed has cardinality about $\pi(n)+\dfrac{2^{3/4}n^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}}$ and has distinct products.

    • Using Aristotle I have essentially finished the formalization of the upper bound $$F(n) < \pi(n) + \frac{cn^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}}$$for all $c > C_*$ and all large enough $n$, for some constant $C_* < 13.1$.

      The formalization of this upper bound result depends on the following additional axioms:

      buchstab_estimate_23
      integralI_upper_bound
      dusart_pi_lower
      dusart_pi_upper

      The latter two of these axioms are explicit prime bounds from Dusart. The first one is an estimate on the Buchstab function, which I could have probably taken from the literature as well, but also decided to formalize here. Finally, the second axiom concerns upper bounding the scary-looking integral $$I = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-x} \sqrt{(e^x - e^{-x}) \prod_{p \le e^{2x}} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p} \right)^{-1}} dx.$$With this integral, the constant $C_*$ I mentioned earlier is defined as $C_* := 2^{3/2} I$. Using an explicit Mertens' type result from Rosser-Schoenfeld and freely making use of the tactic native_decide, here you can find a formalization of $I < \frac{9263}{2000}$, which is sufficient to deduce $C_* < 13.1$. I have tried to make Aristotle prove this without using native_decide as well, but the resulting file keeps crashing.

      Hence, modulo the use of native_decide to find the value of an integral, and under the assumption of various prime results from the literature, we now have the following bounds formally proven for all large enough $n$: $$\pi(n) + \frac{2.95n^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}} < F(n) < \pi(n) + \frac{13.1n^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}}.$$

    • I want to give a natural-language proof of the following statement.
      Let $\Lambda = \frac{2^{11/4}}{3^{3/4}} = 2.9511517859\ldots$ Then for every $\lambda < \Lambda$, the standard layered semiprime construction gives, for all sufficiently large $n$, $\pi(n) + \lambda\,\frac{n^{3/4}}{(\log n)^{3/2}} < F(n).$ Moreover, within the standard layered semiprime ansatz, $\Lambda$ is sharp: no coefficient $c > \Lambda$ is attainable.
      I am not claiming the endpoint $c=\Lambda$, and I am not claiming a global upper bound for $F(n)$.

      The baseline $\pi(n)$ comes from taking all primes up to $n$. The extra terms are selected semiprimes $pq$, where $p$ and $q$ are primes and $pq \le n$. The multiplicative Sidon obstruction is easy to see in the $p$-by-$q$ table. If two $p$-primes and two $q$-primes form a complete $2$-by-$2$ rectangle, then $(pq)(rs) = (ps)(rq).$ Thus we cannot simply take all admissible pairs under $pq \le n$.

      The layered construction first divides the $p$-side primes by size. Choose cutoffs $0 = a_0 < a_1 < \cdots < a_k < 1.$ The $j$-th $p$-layer is $a_{j-1}\sqrt n < p \le a_j\sqrt n,$ and its scaled width is $d_j = a_j - a_{j-1}.$
      For this $p$-layer, the matching $q$-layer is chosen under the hyperbola $pq \le n$. For $j < k$, take $a_{j+1}^{-1}\sqrt n < q \le a_j^{-1}\sqrt n,$ and for the last layer take $a_k\sqrt n < q \le a_k^{-1}\sqrt n.$ Thus the scaled $q$-width is $b_j = a_j^{-1} - a_{j+1}^{-1}$ for $j < k$, $b_k = a_k^{-1} - a_k.$

      Within each such $p/q$ layer pair, we then choose a $C_4$-free bipartite graph between the $p$-side primes and the $q$-side primes.

      Put $X_n = \frac{\sqrt n}{\log n}.$ By the prime number theorem, the $j$-th $p$-side and $q$-side have $|P_j| = (2d_j+o(1))X_n,$ $|Q_j| = (2b_j+o(1))X_n.$

      Now insert the standard projective-plane incidence block in one layer. Map $p$-side primes to points of a finite projective plane and $q$-side primes to lines. If a point lies on a line, include the corresponding semiprime $pq$.
      A projective plane of order $r$ has $r^2+r+1$ points, $r^2+r+1$ lines, and each point is incident with $r+1$ lines. The $+1$ is the projective contribution: compared with an affine $r$-by-$r$ grid, one extra direction at infinity is added. Also, two distinct points lie on at most one common line, so the incidence graph is $C_4$-free: a $C_4$ would mean that the same two points lie together on two distinct common lines.

      If the $q$-side has size $M$, choose $r$ so that $r^2$ is about $M$. Then each $p$-side prime can be connected to about $r+1 \sim \sqrt M$ $q$-side primes. Therefore one layer contributes, at leading order, $(\text{p-side size})\sqrt{\text{q-side size}}$ semiprimes. For the $j$-th layer this gives $|P_j|\sqrt{|Q_j|} = (2d_j+o(1))X_n\sqrt{(2b_j+o(1))X_n} = (2d_j\sqrt{2b_j}+o(1))X_n^{3/2}.$
      Summing over the finitely many layers, the asymptotic coefficient attached to this cutoff chain is $L_k = 2\sum_j d_j\sqrt{2b_j}.$

      For this fixed finite chain, the construction gives every coefficient $\lambda < L_k$ after the usual $o(1)$ losses. The main claim is that every finite cutoff chain satisfies $L_k \le \Lambda.$
      The certificate is the Bellman inequality $(1-y)\sqrt{1-x} + \sqrt y\sqrt{2-y} \le \sqrt{2-x}.$ Indeed, set $s=1-y$ and $A=\sqrt{1-x}$. The left side becomes $sA+\sqrt{1-s^2},$ which is at most $\sqrt{A^2+1} = \sqrt{2-x}$ by Cauchy-Schwarz.
      Applying this inequality backwards along the cutoff chain gives $\sum_j d_j\sqrt{b_j} \le \sqrt{a_k}\sqrt{2-a_k^2}.$ Since $L_k = 2\sqrt 2\sum_j d_j\sqrt{b_j},$ we get $L_k \le 2\sqrt 2\sqrt{a_k}\sqrt{2-a_k^2}.$
      The one-variable expression on the right is maximized at $a_k = \sqrt{2/3},$ and the maximum value is $\frac{2^{11/4}}{3^{3/4}}.$ Thus $L_k \le \Lambda.$

      Together with the known layered construction approaching $\Lambda$ from below, this gives the exact supremum of the standard layered cutoff functional.
      So merely adding more layers or re-optimizing the same layered rectangular $C_4$-free block ansatz cannot beat $2.9511517859\ldots$. More general semiprime host-graph constructions are not ruled out by this argument.
