If $n(n+1)=2^k3^lm$, where $(m,6)=1$, then is it true that\[\limsup_{n\to \infty} \frac{2^k3^l}{n\log n}=\infty?\]
#933: [Er76d]

Mahler proved (a more general result that implies in particular) that\[2^k3^l<n^{1+o(1)}.\]Erdős [Er76d] wrote 'it is easy to see' that for infinitely many $n$\[2^k3^l>n\log n.\]Steinerberger has noted a simple proof of this fact follows from taking $n=2^{3^r}$ for any integer $r\geq 1$, when $k=3^r$ and $l=r+1$.

  • It seems like conditionally on uniform $3$-adic expansion of residue classes (expected behavior), this is true. Here's GPT-5.2 argument:

    Fix an odd integer $a$ with $(a,3)=1$ and assume $a \equiv 1 \pmod 3$. Put $u=-1/(2a)\in 1+3\mathbb{Z}_3$. For each integer $\delta\ge 1$ there is a unique residue class $t \bmod 3^{\delta-1}$ such that $4^t \equiv u \pmod{3^\delta}$; let $t_\delta\in\{0,1,\dots,3^{\delta-1}-1\}$ be its least representative. Equivalently, if $T=\log_3(u)/\log_3(4)\in\mathbb{Z}_3$ (so that $4^T=u$ inside $1+3\mathbb{Z}_3$), then $t_\delta \equiv T \pmod{3^{\delta-1}}$. Now set $\alpha_\delta=2t_\delta+1$ and $n_\delta=a2^{\alpha_\delta}$; then $4^{t_\delta}\equiv u \pmod{3^\delta}$ implies $a2^{\alpha_\delta}+1\equiv 0 \pmod{3^\delta}$, hence $3^\delta$ divides $n_\delta+1$, while $3$ does not divide $n_\delta$ and $2$ does not divide $n_\delta+1$.

    Write the base-$3$ expansion $T=\sum_{j\ge 0} d_j 3^j$ with digits $d_j\in\{0,1,2\}$, so $t_\delta=\sum_{j=0}^{\delta-2} d_j 3^j$ is the $(\delta-1)$-digit truncation. Assume (THIS IS CONDITIONAL) that the digits $d_j$ behave like independent uniform random variables on $\{0,1,2\}$. For each $\delta$ let $L(\delta)=\lfloor \log_3 \delta \rfloor$ and let $E_\delta$ be the event that the top $L(\delta)$ digits of this truncation are zero, namely $d_{\delta-2}=d_{\delta-3}=\cdots=d_{\delta-1-L(\delta)}=0$. Then $\mathbb{P}(E_\delta)=3^{-L(\delta)}$, and since $3^{L(\delta)}\le \delta<3^{L(\delta)+1}$ we have $1/\delta \le \mathbb{P}(E_\delta)<3/\delta$. Choose a subsequence $\delta_1<\delta_2<\cdots$ by $\delta_1=3$ and $\delta_{j+1}=\delta_j+2L(\delta_j)+4$; then the digit blocks defining $E_{\delta_j}$ are disjoint, so the events $E_{\delta_j}$ are independent, and also $\delta_j \le C j \log j$ for some constant $C>0$, hence $\sum_j \mathbb{P}(E_{\delta_j})$ diverges by comparison with $\sum_j 1/(j\log j)$. By the second Borel-Cantelli lemma, infinitely many $E_{\delta_j}$ occur, and on each occurrence we have $t_{\delta_j}<3^{\delta_j-1-L(\delta_j)}\le 3^{\delta_j}/\delta_j$.

    For $n_\delta=a2^{2t_\delta+1}$ we have a factorization $n_\delta(n_\delta+1)=2^k 3^l m$ with $(m,6)=1$ where $k=v_2(n_\delta)=2t_\delta+1$ and $l=v_3(n_\delta+1)\ge \delta$. Therefore
    $$\frac{2^k 3^l}{n_\delta \log n_\delta}=\frac{2^{2t_\delta+1}3^l}{a2^{2t_\delta+1}\log(a2^{2t_\delta+1})}=\frac{3^l}{a\log(a2^{2t_\delta+1})}\ge \frac{3^\delta}{a\log(a2^{2t_\delta+1})}.$$
    For $t_\delta\ge 1$ we have $\log(a2^{2t_\delta+1})=\log a+(2t_\delta+1)\log 2\le (\log a+3\log 2)t_\delta$, hence
    $$\frac{2^k 3^l}{n_\delta \log n_\delta}\ge \frac{3^\delta}{a(\log a+3\log 2)t_\delta}.$$
    Along the infinitely many indices $\delta=\delta_j$ for which $t_{\delta_j}\le 3^{\delta_j}/\delta_j$, this gives
    $$\frac{2^k 3^l}{n_{\delta_j}\log n_{\delta_j}} \ge \frac{3^\delta}{a(\log a+3\log 2)(3^{\delta-1}/\delta)}=\frac{\delta}{a(\log a+3\log 2)}.$$

    Since the right-hand side tends to infinity with $\delta$, the values of $(2^k3^l)/(n\log n)$ along the subsequence $n=n_{\delta_j}$ are unbounded, and in particular $\limsup_{n\to\infty}(2^k3^l)/(n\log n)=\infty$. The only conditional input is the digit hypothesis on $T=\log_3(-1/(2a))/\log_3(4)$: it is enough that the base-$3$ digits of $T$ have the Borel-Cantelli property that blocks of $L(\delta)=\lfloor\log_3\delta\rfloor$ consecutive zeros at the top of the truncation occur infinitely often (independence is a convenient sufficient condition).

      • Yes, this is a heuristic argument. Here is a simplified version (taking $a=7$ for concreteness):

        In the 3-adics ${\bf Z}_3$, let $t = \frac{\log (1-3 \times 5/14)}{\log(1+3)} \in {\bf Z}_3$ be the unique solution to the equation $(1 + 3)^t = 1 - \frac{3 \times 5}{14}$ in ${\bf Z}_3$, or equivalently $14 \times (1+3)^t+1=0$. This is well-defined due to the convergence of the power series for the logarithm $\log(1+3x)$ in the 3-adics, and it is a routine matter to compute the first few (ternary) digits of this 3-adic number. A quick ChatGPT query for instance gives

        $$ t = \dots 2102120122012_3$$
        Now make the plausible heuristic assumption that this number contains arbitrarily long blocks of 0s in its ternary expansion. Thus, for any $L$, there exists some $l$ such that the $l-L,\dots,l-1$ ternary digits of $t$ vanish, so that $t = 3^l q + r$ for some $q \in {\bf Z}_3$ and $0 \leq r < 3^{l-L}$. If one sets $k := 2r+1$ and $n := 7 \times 2^k$, then
        $$n+1 = 14 \times (1+3)^r+1 = 14 \times (1+3)^t + 1 = 0 \pmod{3^l}.$$
        So $n(n+1)=2^k 3^l m$ for some $m$ coprime to $6$, and
        $$ \frac{2^k 3^l}{n \log n} \asymp \frac{2^k 3^l}{2^k r} \gg 3^L$$
        and so this quantity goes to infinity conditionally on the hypothesis of arbitrarily long blocks of zeroes.

        But this type of hypothesis is comparable to infamous questions about whether standard irrational numbers such as $e$, $\pi$, or $\sqrt{2}$ are normal in various bases, and seems well out of reach of current methods. (There is a slim chance that one could get some mileage out of also varying $a$ and using Diophantine approximation tools, but this looks delicate at best - the gains in $L$ one might make by this method could be more than offset by the losses in the $a$ factor (which is suppressed in the above analysis as we took $a=7$).)

    • It looks like you are only checking the cases where $\{n, n+1\}$ contains either a power of two or a power of three. But this is not required; it is possible that $\{n, n+1\} = \{2^x a, 3^y b\}$ with $a,b$ both larger than $1$.

      EDIT: I found a counterexample to your main claim that the ratio is uniformly bounded by $\frac{3}{\log 2}$. The integer $n = 1487503359$ is divisible by $3^{14}$, while $n+1$ is divisible by $2^{15}$, giving $\frac{2^x 3^y}{n \log n} \approx 4.99 > \frac{3}{\log{2}}$
