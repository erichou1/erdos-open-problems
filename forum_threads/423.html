Let $a_1=1$ and $a_2=2$ and for $k\geq 3$ choose $a_k$ to be the least integer $>a_{k-1}$ which is the sum of at least two consecutive terms of the sequence. What is the asymptotic behaviour of this sequence?
#423: [Er77c,p.71][ErGr80,p.83]
number theory

Comment activity that has not yet been incorporated into the remarks
There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Asked by Hofstadter (in [Er77c] Erdős says Hofstadter was inspired by a similar question of Ulam). The sequence begins\[1,2,3,5,6,8,10,11,\ldots\]and is A005243 in the OEIS.

Bolan and Tang [Ta26] have independently proved that there are infinitely many integers which do not appear in this sequence. In fact, the sequence $a_n-n$ is nondecreasing and unbounded.

Tang [Ta26] further proved that $a_n \ll n^{\frac{1}{c-1}+o(1)}$, where $c$ is any exponent such that $\lvert A-A\rvert \geq \lvert A\rvert^{c-o(1)}$ for all convex sets $A$. In particular, the latest bounds of Cushman [Cu25] yield\[a_n \ll n^{\frac{688}{413}+o(1)}\leq n^{1.6659+o(1)},\]and the conjecture of Erdős and Hegyvári that one can take $c=2$ in the convex set problem would imply $a_n\leq n^{1+o(1)}$. It seems likely that $a_n=n+o(n)$ is the truth.

Tang has further proved an improved lower bound of\[a_n=n+\Omega(\log\log n).\]

    • This upper bound is awesome!

      A while ago I was able to find a simple argument showing a quadratic upper bound, I have not studied your paper so perhaps the argument is already known to you. I never was able to beat quadratic by much, and certainly not to the degree you have.

      The point of my argument is that there is an anti-correlation between the twofold sums and the fourfold sums. Suppose there are $k$ elements of the sequence in the interval $(x, 2x]$. Then there are at least $k-3$ elements in $(4x, 8x]$ coming from fourfold sums. We want to show that there is a twofold sum in each interval $(a_{i} + a_{i+1} + a_{i+2} + a_{i+3}, a_{i+1} + a_{i+2} + a_{i+3} + a_{i+4}).$ This follows as there is a portion of the sequence which looks like $(a_{i+1} + a_{i+2}) = c_0, c_1, ..., c_s =(a_{i+2} + a_{i+3})$, and selecting a $j < s$ such that $c_j \le a_{i+1} + a_{i+3}$ and $c_{j+1} \ge a_{i+1} + a_{i+3}$ we have $c_j + c_{j+1}$ in the needed interval. We conclude that there are at least $2k - 7$ entries of the sequence in $(4x, 8x]$ (or $2k-5$ if one handles the boundaries better). This implies that if there are at least $c2^n + 14$ entries of the sequence less than $4^n$, then there are at least $c2^{n+1} + 14$ entries less than $4^{n+1}$, so the sequence exhibits at most quadratic growth.

  • In the OEIS entry A005243, the COMMENTS section records the conjecture that there are infinitely many nonmembers (i.e. positive integers that never appear in this sequence). I have written a short note proving this conjecture.

              • This may relate to a property I remember proving in my comp math days: A number cannot be written as sum of consecutive numbers if and only if it is a power of 2, and if $n = 2^k pm$ where $p$ is the minimal odd prime dividing $n$ and $m$ is odd, then the length of the minimal length representation is $\min(p, 2^{k+1})$.                   old-bielefelder — 16:26 on 17 Jan 2026

                  • > What do you mean by "the length of the minimal length representation"?

                    The "length" is $\ell$ in the following classic argument:

                    The sum of the $\ell$ consecutive integers starting at $m$ is $(m)+(m+1)+\cdots+(m+\ell-1)=\ell\cdot(2m+\ell-1)/2$. If this sum equals $n$, then we have $2n=\ell\cdot(2m+\ell-1)$. Moreover, note that the sum of the two factors $\ell$ and $2m+\ell-1$ is $2(m+\ell)-1$, which is odd; it follows that exactly one of the factors is odd. Conversely, for every odd factor $d$ of $2n$, we can either let $\ell=d$ and $2m+\ell-1=2n/d$ or vice versa, and this corresponds to a decomposition.

                    For example, if $n=15$, then the possible odd factors are $1$, $3$, $5$, and $15$ and the possible decompositions are: $d=1$ giving rise to $15$ (the single-term sum) and $-14+\cdots+14+15$; $d=3$ giving rise to $4+5+6$ and $-3+\cdots+6$; $d=5$ giving rise to $1+2+3+4+5$ and $0+1+2+3+4+5$; and $d=15$ giving rise to $-6+\cdots+8$ and $7+8$.

                    If we restrict to sums of positive integers, then exactly one decomposition from each pair works. Moreover, its length is the smaller of the two factors $\min(d,2n/d)$. Also, this means that the number of ways of writing $n$ as the sum of consecutive positive integers equals the number of odd divisors of $n$.

                    If we further insist the sum have at least two terms, then this excludes exactly one decomposition (the one with $n$ by itself, corresponding to the odd divisor $1$ of $2n$). In particular, an integer $n$ has a nontrivial decomposition iff $2n$ has any odd divisor other than $1$. This happens iff $n$ is not a power of $2$.

                    If we try to analyze the smallest possible length $\ell$ (other than the single-term decomposition), we get the claim above: it comes either from the smallest nontrivial odd divisor $d$ of $2n$ (which is the smallest odd prime $p$ dividing $n$) or from the largest odd divisor $d$ of $2n$ (which then ends up having a length one more than the 2-adic valuation of $n$).
