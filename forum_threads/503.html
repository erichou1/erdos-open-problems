What is the size of the largest $A\subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$ such that every three points from $A$ determine an isosceles triangle? That is, for any three points $x,y,z$ from $A$, at least two of the distances $\lvert x-y\rvert,\lvert y-z\rvert,\lvert x-z\rvert$ are equal.
#503: [ErKe47][Er61,p.244]
geometry | distances

There are claims of partial results and/or a path to a solution in the comments.
When $d=2$ the answer is $6$ (due to Kelly [ErKe47] - an alternative proof is given by Kovács [Ko24c]). When $d=3$ the answer is $8$ (due to Croft [Cr62]). The best upper bound known in general is due to Blokhuis [Bl84] who showed that\[\lvert A\rvert \leq \binom{d+2}{2}.\]Alweiss has observed a lower bound of $\binom{d+1}{2}$ follows from considering the subset of $\mathbb{R}^{d+1}$ formed of all vectors $e_i+e_j$ where $e_i,e_j$ are distinct coordinate vectors. This set can be viewed as a subset of some $\mathbb{R}^d$, and is easily checked to have the required property.

Weisenberg observed in the comments that an additional point can be added to Alweiss' construction, giving a lower bound of $\binom{d+1}{2}+1$.

The fact that the truth for $d=3$ is $8$ suggests that neither of these bounds is the truth.

See also [1088] for a generalisation.

  • I've managed to get with GPT-5.4 Pro a very nice formula for the size. Here's a note and here's a formalization of it with Aristotle (EDIT: the new note is here).

    Let $f(d)$ be the largest size of a finite set $S\subset \mathbb R^d$ such that every three points of $S$ form an isosceles triangle. Let $g(d)$ be the largest size of a Euclidean $2$-distance set in $\mathbb R^d$, let $s(d)$ be the largest size of a spherical $2$-distance set in $\mathbb R^d$, and let $h(d)$ be the largest size of an isosceles set in $\mathbb R^d$ with more than two distances. We prove that for every $d\ge 2$,\[
    h(d)\le \max\{s(d)+1,\ s(d-1)+3\},
    \]and\[
    f(d)=\max\{g(d),\ s(d)+1,\ s(d-1)+3\}.
    \]Thus the full Euclidean isosceles-set problem is reduced exactly to the Euclidean and spherical $2$-distance extremal problems.

    EDIT: as noted by Koizumi the above note misses one extremal case which is quite a bit of technical work. I've managed to fix the gap with GPT-5.5 Pro and here's the rewritten note.

      • You are right - there's a missing extremal case. I've given an updated note in the main comment above. I managed to fix the gap with GPT-5.5 Pro.

        Basically Ionin's complete decomposition only gives\[
        |S_i|\ge 2
        \]for non-last blocks, so a non-last block may consist of two points and have affine dimension \(1\).

        The argument can be repaired in all non-extremal cases. Suppose the first block is\[
        S_1=\{p,q\}.
        \]All later points lie in the perpendicular bisector \(H\cong\mathbb R^{d-1}\) of \(pq\). If the remaining set is not a two-distance set, then induction in dimension \(d-1\) gives\[
        |S|\le h(d-1)+2\le \max\{s(d-1)+3,\ s(d-2)+5\}.
        \]Since\[
        s(d-2)+5\le U(d-2)+5\le \binom{d+1}{2}+1\le s(d)+1
        \]for \(d\ge 3\), this case is covered.

        It remains to consider\[
        S=\{p,q\}\cup X,
        \]where \(X\subset H\) is a two-distance set. Let \(\Delta\) be the set of distances occurring in \(X\), and put\[
        E=\{x\in X:\ |p-x|\notin\Delta\}.
        \]Then \(E\) is spherical with center the midpoint of \(pq\), and every point of \(X\setminus E\) is a center of a sphere containing \(E\). Hence, unless \(E=\varnothing\) and \(|X|=\binom{d+1}{2}\), the Blokhuis and Delsarte--Goethals--Seidel bounds give\[
        |S|\le \max\{s(d)+1,\ s(d-1)+3\}.
        \]Thus the only remaining case is the following extremal suspension case:\[
        E=\varnothing,\qquad |X|=\binom{d+1}{2}.
        \]Equivalently, \(X\cup\{p\}\) is a two-distance set of size\[
        \binom{d+1}{2}+1
        \]in \(\mathbb R^d\), with \(X\) contained in a hyperplane.

        This last extremal case is the main new technical input in the new note above - see EDIT.

      As $s(d-1)+3\leq h(d)\leq \max\{s(d-1)+3,s(d)+1\}$, this is equivalent to whether $h(d)\geq s(d)+1$ for all $d$. By taking the simplex construction that lower bounds $g(d)$ and adding the centre of the sphere, we have that $h(d)\geq \frac{d(d+1)}{2}$ +1.

As $g(d)\leq \max\{s(d)+1,s(d-1)+3\}$ for $1\leq d\leq 6$, this implies that $h(d)\geq s(d)+1$ for $1\leq d\leq 39, d\not = 22,23$ and so $h(d)=\max\{s(d)+1,s(d-1)+3\}$.

  • The problem-page remarks do not seem to mention this, but in dimension \(22\) one gets \(f(22)=276\).

    Musin records that the Delsarte--Goethals--Seidel bound for spherical two-distance sets is tight in dimension \(22\), so there is a spherical two-distance set in \(\mathbb R^{22}\) of size \(22(22+3)/2=275\). Adding the centre gives an isosceles set of size \(276\). Blokhuis's general upper bound gives\[
    f(22)\le \binom{24}{2}=276.
    \]Hence \(f(22)=276\).

    This is also noted as Corollary 6.4 in Chojecki's note; the argument itself does not use the proposed reduction formula.
