Let $1\leq u_1<u_2<\cdots$ be the sequence of integers with at most $2$ prime factors. Is it true that\[\limsup \frac{u_{k+1}-u_k}{\log k}=\infty?\]

  • GPT Pro claims a solution here. I have attempted to distil the proof strategy below:

    The proof chooses an $N$ so that every offset $n\in[1,Y]$ makes $N+n$ have at least three prime factors. It sets $$M_0:=\prod_{p\le z}p^2\prod_{z<p\le Y/z}p,$$ and imposes $N\equiv 0\pmod{M_0}$. Hence, every prime $p\leq Y/z$ dividing $n$ also divides $N+n$, and if $p\leq z$ satisfies $p^2\mid n$, then $p^2\mid(N+n)$. This handles all offsets for which these forced small-prime divisors already contribute at least two prime factors. The remaining underlying target integers are mainly large primes $q$, represented by two coloured vertices, and semiprimes $sq$, represented by one vertex.
    For each modulus prime $P$, a residue class $A\pmod P$ induces the finite arithmetic progression $A+dP$, and imposing $N\equiv -A\pmod{P}$ makes $P\mid(N+(A+dP))$ for every selected index $d$. The proof chooses a residue $b\pmod W$, a set of selected indices $d$, and a "type" $\kappa(d)$ for each selected index. The type records whether $A+dP$ is intended to be a prime target $q$, or a semiprime target $sq$ with prescribed small factor $s$. The residue $b$ is chosen so that $A+dP=s_dQ_d(P,C)$, where $s_d=1$ or $s$, and $Q_d(P,C)$ is an integral primitive linear form with no forced small-prime divisor. Whenever $P$ and all the associated forms $Q_d(P,C)$ are prime, the resulting admissible hyperedge covers several target vertices at once.

    The Main Theorem (page 13) of Green and Tao on linear equations in primes then gives asymptotic counts for the simultaneous prime values of $P$ and the forms $Q_d(P,C)$. Applying it both to individual hyperedges and to pairs of hyperedges yields the required first- and second-moment estimates: The total fractional weight above a typical modulus prime is close to a fixed value below one, while the fractional load on almost every target vertex is close to a large prescribed value. This allows at most one admissible hyperedge to be chosen independently for each regular modulus prime, leaving only $o(Y/\log Y)$ vertices uncovered. Each remaining uncovered vertex, together with every offset in the sparse exceptional set, is assigned one or two distinct reserve primes from $(Y,2Y]$, according to how many additional forced prime factors it still needs. The Chinese remainder theorem then combines the congruence $N\equiv0\pmod{M_0}$, the selected residue classes modulo the modulus primes, and the reserve-prime congruences into one integer $N$. Every $N+n$ has two forced prime factors whose product is smaller than $N+n$, so a third prime factor remains. Since $(N,N+Y]$ contains no integer with at most two prime factors, if $u_k\le N<u_{k+1}$, then $$u_{k+1}-u_k\ge Y,\qquad\frac{u_{k+1}-u_k}{\log k}\geq\frac{Y}{\log N}\to\infty,$$ because the construction ensures $\log N=o(Y)$.

  • I wrote up a short covering-reduction note related to this thread:

    GitHub Gist

    Disclosure: this was prepared with AI assistance (GPT-5.5 xhigh). Claude was used as an adversarial reviewer of the reductions and caveats.

    This is not a solution of #1139. The note records two sufficient finite covering statements and one caveat about applying existing large-prime-gap technology.

    The first sufficient criterion is a threefold sparse cover. Suppose that for arbitrarily large $y$ one can choose distinct primes $P_y$ and one residue class $a_p \pmod p$ for each $p\in P_y$, such that every $1\le n\le y$ lies in at least three of the residue classes, and\[
    L(y):=\sum_{p\in P_y}\log p=o(y).
    \]Then the desired conclusion follows. The CRT gives $N\equiv -a_p\pmod p$ for all $p\in P_y$, so every $N+n$ has at least three distinct prime divisors for $1\le n\le y$. The gap length is at least $y$, while $\log k\le O(\log y+L(y))=o(y)$.

    There is also a twofold small-prime variant, closer to the discussion above. If every $1\le n\le y$ is covered at least twice by distinct primes $p\le y$, again with $\sum\log p=o(y)$, then this also suffices. Choose the CRT representative $N$ with\[
    y^2<N\le y^2+\prod_{p\in P_y}p.
    \]Then the two primes $p,q\le y$ forced to divide $N+n$ satisfy $pq\le y^2<N+n$, so $N+n$ has at least three prime factors counted with multiplicity.

    The caveat is that the apparent shortcut through applying Ford-Konyagin-Maynard-Pomerance-Tao's long gaps in sieved sets theorem separately to fixed positive-density prime classes is not justified as a black-box step. One residue class on a density-$\rho$ subset of primes gives a dimension-$\rho$ sieve:\[
    \prod_{\substack{p\le x\\p\in P}}\left(1-\frac1p\right)\asymp(\log x)^{-\rho},
    \]whereas the FKMP-T theorem assumes a one-dimensional system. Assigning multiple classes per prime can restore dimension, but then a single CRT congruence for $N$ can no longer force all of those classes to divide $N+n$.

    So the current status is: these covering reductions are valid, but the needed sparse multi-covering theorem remains the open technical point.

      • Indeed, seems like the core is the same or very similar. This is what I got while poking GPT-5.2 for the connection and a missing argument one might need for both: A convenient finite/combinatorial reformulation of our goal is the following 2-fold covering problem: for large $n$, can one choose residue classes $a_p \pmod p$ (for primes $p$ in some set, possibly all $p\le n$) so that every $j\in[1,n]$ satisfies at least two congruences $j\equiv a_p\pmod p$? If we can do this using distinct primes, then choosing $N$ by CRT so that $N\equiv -a_p\pmod p$ for all used $p$ forces each $N+j$ to be divisible by at least two distinct primes $\le n$. Since the CRT modulus is huge (typically $N\gg n^2$ when many primes $\le n$ are used), $N+j$ cannot equal a product of only those two primes, hence $\Omega(N+j)\ge 3$ for all $1\le j\le n$. Thus a 2-fold congruence cover of $[1,n]$ yields an interval $[N+1,N+n]$ with no integers of $\Omega\le 2$, giving gaps in the sequence $u_k$ of primes/semiprimes; moreover, tracking $\log N\asymp\sum_{p\in S}\log p$ is what determines the gap factor $n/\log N$ and hence $\limsup (u_{k+1}-u_k)/\log k$.

        We take $z\to\infty$ slowly, set $y:=n/z$, and run an Erdős--Rankin style multi-stage cover. Stage~0 sets $a_p=0$ for all primes $p\le y$, giving one ''touch'' for each small prime divisor. Up to negligible sets, the indices with $\le 1$ touch are (i) primes $j\in(y,n]$, of size $\sim n/\log n$, and (ii) semiprimes $j=p_1p_2$ with $p_1<z$ and $p_2>y$, of size $\sim (n/\log n)\log\log z$; semiprimes with $p_1>z^{1/A}$ contribute $\sim (n/\log n)\log A$. The numerology is that if we now use primes $p\asymp y$, then one residue class mod $p$ hits $\asymp n/p\asymp z$ indices, and among those the usable targets occur with density $\asymp 1/\log(n/p)\asymp 1/\log z$, giving $\asymp z/\log z$ useful hits per prime. This suggests that with $R\asymp \frac{y\log z}{\log n}(1+\log A)$ primes $p\asymp y$ one can add enough touches to kill most primes and most semiprimes with $p_1>z^{1/A}$, and with another comparable batch reduce the remaining ''small-$p_1$'' semiprimes by an extra factor $\approx A$.

        The modulus bookkeeping is the key link to the original $\limsup$ question. Using primes $p\asymp y$ keeps $\sum\log p$ under control: combining stages yields\[
        \log N \asymp \sum_{p\in S}\log p \asymp y\,\log z\,(1+\log A),
        \]so the resulting gap factor is\[
        \frac{n}{\log N}\asymp \frac{z}{\log z\,(1+\log A)}\to\infty
        \quad\text{for choices like}\quad
        A=\exp((\log\log\log z)^2).
        \]Cleanup must avoid using $\asymp n/\log n$ ''reserve primes'' near $n$ (which would force $\log N\asymp n$ and destroy the gap factor); instead one cleans up with primes $\asymp y$, where each prime hits $\asymp z$ indices so $|S|$ stragglers can be handled using $\ll |S|/z$ primes at modulus cost $\ll y$.

        The main bottleneck is to make the ''random sieve'' steps rigorous in this 2-fold setting. One needs a multi-covering/hypergraph-cover lemma (a 2-fold cover rather than the usual 1-fold cover for prime gaps) ensuring that for many primes $p\asymp y$ one can choose residue classes that hit about the expected number of targets (primes and the structured semiprime families attached to small $p_1$), with suitably small codegrees to drive survivor counts down by factors like $A$. This is where one expects to use distribution-in-progressions and ''linear equations in primes'' technology as in modern large prime gap work, but an explicit statement tailored to these mixed target sets appears to be the central technical gap.
