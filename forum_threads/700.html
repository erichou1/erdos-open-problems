Let\[f(n)=\min_{1<k\leq n/2}\textrm{gcd}\left(n,\binom{n}{k}\right).\]
  • Characterise those composite $n$ such that $f(n)=n/P(n)$, where $P(n)$ is the largest prime dividing $n$.
  • Are there infinitely many composite $n$ such that $f(n)>n^{1/2}$?
  • Is it true that, for every composite $n$,\[f(n) \ll_A \frac{n}{(\log n)^A}\]for every $A>0$?

A problem of Erdős and Szekeres. It is easy to see that $f(n)\leq n/P(n)$ for composite $n$, since if $j=p^k$ where $p^k\mid n$ and $p^{k+1}\nmid n$ then $\textrm{gcd}\left(n,\binom{n}{j}\right)=n/p^k$. This implies\[f(n) \leq (1+o(1))\frac{n}{\log n}.\]It is known that $f(n)=n/P(n)$ when $n$ is the product of two primes. Another example is $n=30$.

For the second problem, it is easy to see that for any $n$ we have $f(n)\geq p(n)$, where $p(n)$ is the smallest prime dividing $n$, and hence there are infinitely many $n$ (those $=p^2)$ such that $f(n)\geq n^{1/2}$.

  • The third problem is likely very hard.
    Even proving that $\gg n^{1-\epsilon}$ for small $\epsilon$ can occur, seems hard.



    So one wonders if $n/f(n)$ is the product of only a small fractions of its prime divisors (assuming they are not too different).



    Since already checking concrete divisibilities of binomial coefficients, e.g. [#376], seem widely open,
    telling that in general $\binom{n}{k}$ is a multiple of a big divisor of $n$ for all $k$, cannot be expected to be easy.



    In particular, one can wonder if $f(n)=n/P(n)$ might happen for $n$ being the product of many primes that are not too far apart.



    For the product of $5$ primes (each bounded by $29$),
    there are only a few values $n$ satisfying $f(n)=n/P(n)$. They are:
    $$2310,4830,21318,34510,73370,86710,206074.$$

    For the product of $6$ (or $7$) primes bounded by $37$ (or $47$ for $7$), there was only one value $n$ satisfying $f(n)=n/P(n)$ for each; $71677177=11 * 13 * 19 * 23 * 31 * 37$ and $34207446=2 * 3 * 7 * 13 * 31 * 43 * 47$.



    The above examples indicate even more that the characterisation in the first subquestion seems hopeless. Someone who thinks it is not?



    Anyone optimistic that something can be done on the third subquestion?



    The second subquestion may be the one where it is possible to have a rigourous proof. A different construction may do the work. But maybe this rigourous proof would not add much to the insight the conditional proofs already gave.

  • The second subquestion should definitely be answered with yes.
    Nevertheless, it was frustrating to not have a clear infinite family.
    The following two families should do the job.




    *there are likely infinitely many primes of the form $q=2^k+3$ where $k \equiv 4 \pmod {20}$ (see [oeis/A057732])


    For such a $q$ (larger than $19$, the latter being valid as well), let $n=q(q+1)$, these satisfy $f(n)=q+1.$
    Notice that $q+1 \mid \binom{n}{q}$ is the only binomial which is not a multiple of $q$.
    Now $2q \mid \binom{n}{r(q+1)}$ if $\frac{q-1}{2}\ge r$>$3$ and $5q \mid \binom{n}{r(q+1)}$ if $1\le r \le 3$.



    *(construction by Kadi Siigur)


    Let $p,q$ be two primes satisfying $2^l$ < $p$ < $q$ < $2^{l+1}$ for which $pq \equiv 1 \pmod{2^l}$ (thus $l\ge 4$).
    There should be infinitely many such pairs of primes heuristically (similar to the fact that there should be infinitely many twin primes) and computations give many solutions as such.
    Now $n=2pq$ has the property that $f(n)=2p$.
    This is since $2q \mid \binom{2pq}{pq},$ $2q \mid \binom{2pq}{2pr}$ (for $r \in [1,q]$) and $2p \mid \binom{2pq}{2rq}$ (for $r \in [1,p]$).
