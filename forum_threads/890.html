If $\omega_k(n)$ counts the number of distinct prime factors of $n$ which are $>k$, then is it true that, for every $k\geq 1$,\[\liminf_{n\to \infty}\sum_{0\leq i<k}\omega_k(n+i)\leq k?\]Is it true that\[\limsup_{n\to \infty}\left(\sum_{0\leq i<k}\omega(n+i)\right) \frac{\log\log n}{\log n}=1,\]where $\omega$ counts the number of distinct prime factors without restriction?
#890: [ErSe67,p.430]
number theory | primes

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
A question of Erdős and Selfridge [ErSe67], who observe that\[\liminf_{n\to \infty}\sum_{0\leq i<k}\omega_k(n+i)\geq k-1\]for every $k$. This follows from Pólya's theorem that the set of $k$-smooth integers has unbounded gaps - indeed, provided $n$ is large, all but at most one of $n,n+1,\ldots,n+k-1$ has a prime factor $>k$ by Pólya's theorem.

It is a classical fact that\[\limsup_{n\to \infty}\omega(n)\frac{\log\log n}{\log n}=1.\](There appears to be an error in [ErSe67], who ask the first question with $\omega_k$ replaced by $\omega$, have a bound of $k+\pi(k)$ rather than $k$, but as explained by Meza and Tao in the comments this formulation is obviously false, and the version given here is most likely what was intended.)

  • The first problem Erdös and Selfridge state is not about $\omega (n)$, but a little variation which only counts the prime factors of $n+k$ which are greater than k. Otherwise the sum $\sum_{0 \le i \le k}\omega (n) \gg \log\log(k)k$ from Turán-Kubilius inequality over the set of primes less than $\sqrt{k}$.

    The case for fixed $k$ would follow from the $k$-tuples conjecture the following way: Let $N$ be sufficiently large in terms of $k$. Then linear forms $L(n;j) = N!n + j$ for $1 \le j \le k+1$ form an admissible $k$-tuple if $N$ is sufficiently large. Then, by the $k$-tuples conjecture, there exists infinitely many $n$ such that $L(n;j)$ is prime for all $1 \le j \le k+1$. This implies that $N!n + j$ is equal to a prime $p_{j}$ times $j$ infinitely often.

    The sum that Erdös and Selfridge were considering would be equal to $k+ \pi(k)$ (each $N!n + j$ will contribute $1$ if $j-1$ is not prime and $2$ if it is). Note: the condition $j-1$ being prime or not comes from the fact that we've shifted the beginning of the string of consecutive integers to $N!n + 1$.

Then the first part of the problem is indeed a variant of the prime tuples conjecture.
