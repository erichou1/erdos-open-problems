For any $t\in (0,1)$ let $t=\sum_{k=1}^\infty \epsilon_k(t)2^{-k}$ (where $\epsilon_k(t)\in \{0,1\}$). What is the correct order of magnitude (for almost all $t\in(0,1)$) for\[M_n(t)=\max_{x\in [-1,1]}\left\lvert \sum_{k\leq n}(-1)^{\epsilon_k(t)}x^k\right\rvert?\]
#524: [Er61,p.253]
analysis | probability | polynomials

A problem of Salem and Zygmund [SaZy54]. Chung showed that, for almost all $t$, there exist infinitely many $n$ such that\[M_n(t) \ll \left(\frac{n}{\log\log n}\right)^{1/2}.\]Erdős (unpublished) showed that for almost all $t$ and every $\epsilon>0$ we have $\lim_{n\to \infty}M_n(t)/n^{1/2-\epsilon}=\infty$.

  • Following Mehtaab Sawhney remarks, I was able with GPT 5.2 (with a small input from Gemini/Grok) to complete the argument. Basically giving various estimates with $\limsup$ being straightforward and $\liminf$ being more tricky. You can find the full write-up of the arguments here. $\limsup$ and $\liminf$ parts are indepentent. Last section is the summary of results (also below), and 6.6. considers M.S. restatement in the comments below.

    For Lebesgue-a.e. $t\in(0,1)$ the signs $a_k(t)=(-1)^{\epsilon_k(t)}$ are i.i.d. Rademacher, and\[
    M_n(t)=\max_{x\in[-1,1]}\Big|\sum_{k=1}^n a_k(t)x^k\Big|
    \]admits a sharp almost-sure upper-envelope law:\[
    \limsup_{n\to\infty}\frac{M_n(t)}{\sqrt{2n\log\log n}}=1.
    \]The proof is a reduction by Abel summation (Salem-Zygmund style) to maxima of two simple random walks, followed by the classical LIL (Kolmogorov-Hartman-Wintner/Chung). The same comparison yields subgaussian tails at the $\sqrt n$ scale for each fixed $n$.

    For the lower envelope, the random-walk reduction is one-sided and gives only\[
    \liminf_{n\to\infty}\Big(\tfrac{\log\log n}{n}\Big)^{1/2}M_n(t)\le \frac{\pi}{\sqrt8},
    \]with no matching reverse bound. A more sensitive viewpoint uses the endpoint parametrization $x=\pm e^{-u/n}$, under which $M_n/\sqrt n$ becomes the supremum of two Laplace-type processes $Z_n^\pm(u)$.

    Using a two-dimensional strong invariance principle (KMT/Zaitsev-type), $(Z_n^+,Z_n^-)$ can be coupled to two independent copies of $Y(u)=\int_0^1 e^{-us}\,dB(s)$; the small-deviation asymptotics of Gao-Li-Wellner, $\log\mathbb P(\sup_{u\ge0}|Y(u)|\le\epsilon)\asymp-|\log\epsilon|^3$, then give a Borel-Cantelli dichotomy on sparse subsequences showing that the minimal values occur on the scale\[
    M_n(t)=\sqrt n\,\exp\!\big(-\Theta((\log\log n)^{1/3})\big)\quad\text{infinitely often},
    \]with threshold constants determined (up to two-sided bounds) by the small-ball constants for $Y$.

  • It took me a while to understand what exactly this problem is attempting to ask. Note that for a fixed scale $n$ that the maximum will be around size $\sqrt{n}$. To see the lower bound, one can just examine the endpoint $x = 1$. For the upper bound one can use an argument of Salem--Zygmund that\[\sum_{k=1}^{n}\varepsilon_k x^{k} = \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}(x^{k} - x^{k+1})\Big(\sum_{j=1}^{k}\varepsilon_j\Big) + x^n\Big(\sum_{j=1}^{n}\varepsilon_j\Big).\]Now for $x\in [0,1]$, these coefficients are nonnegative and together sum to $1$ so\[\max_{x\in [0,1]}\Big|\sum_{k=1}^{n}\varepsilon_k x^{k}\Big|\le \max_{k\le n}\Big|\sum_{j=1}^{k}\varepsilon_j\Big|.\]Via standard bounds for the maximum of a random walk, we have that the probability this is $\ge \lambda n^{1/2}$ scales as $e^{-\Omega(\lambda^2)}$. An essentially identical analysis applies for $x\in [-1,0]$ modulo replacing $\varepsilon_j$ by $(-1)^{j}\varepsilon_j$.

    My current guess for what Erdos is asking for is a function $f(n)$ such that for almost all $t$ we have that $M_n(t)\ge f(n)$ for all but finitely many $n$ but $M_n(t)\ge C f(n)$ for infinitely many $n$. For this problem, note that\[\mathbb{P}\Big[\max_{k\le n}\Big|\sum_{j=1}^{k}\varepsilon_j\Big|\le \varepsilon n^{1/2}\Big]\asymp \exp(-\Omega(1/\epsilon^2)).\]Noting that for scales which are sufficiently far away (e.g. $n$ vs $n(\log n)^2$ say) these two events are essentially independent. Now as there are $(\log n)^{1-o(1)}$ such distinct scales up to $n$, setting $\varepsilon \approx 1/\sqrt{\log\log n}$ we essentially recover Chung's result.

    • This problem it turns out is very likely to follow from arguments present in the literature already; the true scale of this minimum seems to be between $n^{1/2}\exp(-c(\log\log n)^{1/3})$ and $n^{1/2}\exp(-c'(\log\log n)^{1/3})$. (Determining the exact constant $c$ is likely more technical.) The crucial point is to reparameterize $x = e^{-t/n}$ (thinking of $t$ fixed as $n\to \infty$) and we are seeing to bound\[\sum_{k=1}^{n}\varepsilon_k e^{-tk/n}\]for $t\ge 0$. This is essentially equivalent to looking at $Y(t) =\Big| \int_{0}^{1}e^{-tx}dB(x)\Big|$ and looking at the maximum for $t\ge 0$. We want to understand the probability that this maximum is small; this scaling is worked out in https://arxiv.org/pdf/1001.0200. (This reference was located with help from ChatGPT 5.2 Pro.) The remaining technical work is passing between the Gaussian limit and this problem.

  • I just read [SZ54, p. 299] carefully, and I believe the current formulation of this problem on the website is incorrect; as stated (with coefficients $\epsilon_k(t)\in\{0,1\}$ and $x\in[0,1]$) it becomes essentially trivial. (indeed, the polynomial has nonnegative coefficients, so it is increasing on $[0,1]$, and thus \(M_n(t)=\max_{0\le x\le 1}\sum_{k\le n}\epsilon_k(t)x^k=\sum_{k\le n}\epsilon_k(t)\), reducing the question to the law of large numbers for the dyadic digits).

    According to [SZ54], the intended coefficients should be random signs, i.e. Rademacher functions $\varphi_k(t)\in\{\pm1\}$ rather than binary digits $\epsilon_k(t)\in\{0,1\}$. In terms of the dyadic expansion $t=\sum_{k\ge1}\epsilon_k(t)2^{-k}$, one can set for instance\[
    \varphi_k(t)=1-2\epsilon_k(t)\in\{\pm1\}
    \qquad\text{or}\qquad
    \varphi_k(t)=(-1)^{\epsilon_k(t)}.
    \]Moreover, the maximization should be over $x\in[-1,1]$, not just $x\in[0,1]$. In other words, the Salem--Zygmund version is\[
    M_n(t)=\max_{x\in[-1,1]}\left|\sum_{k\le n}\varphi_k(t)\,x^k\right|.
    \]
