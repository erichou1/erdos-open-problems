Let $k\geq 2$, and let $f_k(n)$ count the number of solutions to\[n=p_1^k+\cdots+p_k^k,\]where the $p_i$ are prime numbers. Is it true that $\limsup f_k(n)=\infty$?
#979: [Er65b,p.224]
number theory

    • Erdős [Er65b] wrote precisely 'I can also prove [the case $k=3$] (unpublished), and the proof of this seems to need some special properties of primes.'

      Which doesn't give a lot of clues. It is of course possible that Erdős' proof also had a flaw, which he later discovered.

      One idea is to adapt Mahler's disproof of Hypothesis K for $k=3$ (see this exposition for example). This relies on the fact that, if $n=m^{12}$, then\[ n = (9t^4)^3 + (m^4-9mt^3)^3 + (9m^3t-9t^4)^3.\]In particular, choosing $t\ll m$ sufficiently small gives $\gg m=n^{1/12}$ many ways to write $n$ as the sum of three positive cubes.

      So one approach would be to give a similar parametric family of solutions (for some $n$ of a special form) and then use sieve theory to show that the forms are simultaneously prime infinitely often. This looks hard, since we know very little about how to find primes among the values of polynomials of degree $>2$, but perhaps one can take advantage of a large number of variables and averaging to make this work.

  • It might well be that the answer is yes at the end.
    (average behaviour and extremal behaviour can be expected to be very different)

    Below some heuristic thinking (not made precise, since proving it in the context will be still hard)

    Notice there are at least $(\pi(\sqrt[k]{N/k}))^k =\tilde \Omega_k (N)$ and at most $(\pi(\sqrt[k]{N}))^k =\tilde O_k (N)$ many combinations of primes whose sum of k^th powers is bounded by $N.$

    Now, if this is somewhat random distributed (it is not uniform random, but should be good for heuristic estimate since one can check $[N/2,N]$..),
    then one can think about the Birthday paradox for $m$ collisions.
    See e.g. [oeis/A225852] and
    [oeis/A225871].
    Rough estimates, tell that for every bound on $m$, the critical region should be at some power of $N$ with exponent less than $1$ (similar to [stackexchange]).
    Since $\tilde\Theta_k(N)$ is linear up to polylog, one expects indeed there are values which appear at least $m$ times...
