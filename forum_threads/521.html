Let $(\epsilon_k)_{k\geq 0}$ be independently uniformly chosen at random from $\{-1,1\}$. If $R_n$ counts the number of real roots of $f_n(z)=\sum_{0\leq k\leq n}\epsilon_k z^k$ then is it true that, almost surely,\[\lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{R_n}{\log n}=\frac{2}{\pi}?\]
#521: [Er61,p.252]
analysis | polynomials | probability

Erdős and Offord [EO56] showed that the number of real roots of a random degree $n$ polynomial with $\pm 1$ coefficients is $(\frac{2}{\pi}+o(1))\log n$.

It is ambiguous in [Er61] whether Erdős intended the coefficients to be uniformly chosen from $\{-1,1\}$ or $\{0,1\}$. In the latter case, the constant $\frac{2}{\pi}$ should be $\frac{1}{\pi}$ (see the discussion in the comments).

In the case of $\{-1,1\}$ Do [Do24] proved that, if $R_n[-1,1]$ counts the number of roots in $[-1,1]$, then, almost surely,\[\lim_{n\to \infty}\frac{R_n[-1,1]}{\log n}=\frac{1}{\pi}.\]See also [522].

  • Hi Terry, Quanyu, and Vjeko. I'm effectively just stating my approach to this problem here. The ideas that the three of you contributed allowed me to paint the picture for a solution, and I would be happy to jointly work on finishing up this project. I agree that the $\lim$ should not exist, and it would be even nicer to determine the precise $\limsup$ and $\liminf$ here. The main idea is of course that if $R_n$ denotes the roots in $(-\infty -1) \cup (1, \infty)$ then one may prove\[
    \sup_{N \le m < n \le 2N} |R_n - R_m| \gg \log N.
    \]First focus on $x < 0$, writing $x = -e^{-t/n}$ for $t > 0$. Then $t$ measures the distance from $1$ at the "natural" scale of $1/n$. The point of this is that if we write $O_n(t) = (-1)^n e^{-t} n^{-1/2} f_n(e^{-t/n})$ then $O_n(t)$ becomes a Laplace transform type of object as $n$ becomes super large. When $n$ becomes large, I should expect this to approach a Gaussian process for which I may then apply analytical techniques to solve the problem with ease.

    Let us fix some sequence $U_n$ with $U_n \to \infty$ and $U_n = o(n)$. Then the "bucket" idea that Terry has is to look at $$B_n(U_n, c, C) = [-e^{CU_n/n}, -e^{cU_n/n}].$$ If one write $O_{n, U_n}(\tau) = \sqrt{U_n}O_n(U_n\tau)$ then this looks like a Riemann-sum in some sense. One may calculate $O_{n, U_n} \to G$ in $C^1[c, C]$ where $G$ is the centered Gaussian process with covariance $\mathbb{E}G(\tau)G(\sigma) = 1/(\tau + \sigma)$.

    Why is this viewpoint interesting? Well now we can use the regularity of $G$ to understand statements about the roots of $G$, and then convert these statements back to the polynomial. There are some facts that I could assign my dog to prove: With high probability, the zeros of $G$ are simple, "well-seperated" and $G$ has derivative at these points "far" away from zero. Also, if two $C^1$ functions are sufficiently close and the first has such stable zeros, then they have the same number of zeros in $[c, C]$.

    Now, I will combine this observation with the following idea/question: Which coefficients actually affect the zero count in a "bucket" as Terry defines? Let us perturb a block of reverse coefficients at some depth $R$. If we resample the coefficients in this window but impose finitely many moment constraints to that the perturbation does not create unwanted lower order effects, then after some rescaling the perturbation converges in $C^1[c, C]$ to a Gaussian field $Z$ obtained from the kernel $(\tau, \sigma) = \int_a^b e^{-x(\tau + \sigma)}~ dx$. The point is that $Z$ is independent of $G$, so we need to understand and prove something like this:\[
    \mathbb{P}(N(G + Z; [c, C]) \neq N(G; [c, C])) > 0,
    \]where $N$ denotes the number of zeros. In other words, adding a non-trivial independent Gaussian perturbation changes the number of zeros in the bucket with positive probability. We now look at three cases. $R \ll n/U_n$, $R \gg n/U_n$, and $R \asymp n/U_n$. For the first case, if we match finitely many moments then we can use Taylor expansion ideas to show that the perturbation becomes invisible in $C^1$. For $R \gg n/U_n$ can also be dealt with ease. Therefore, we only need to worry about the reverse coefficients at depth $R \asymp n/U_n$. And this coupled with all the other ideas before should give a nice proof (after we do the same for $x > 0$.

    What is not so easy is determining the precise $\limsup$ and $\liminf$. I would love to know if people have further ideas. Making this a joint project would be great, because after all, the biggest idea was actually observing that this may be false (I was always trying to prove that this is true, because if one considers the Kac-Rice formula for Gaussian processes then this $G$ actually predicts $2/\pi$).

      As for the actual outline of the proof, it also combines a few things said here on the forum: Terry's idea of carefully choosing a bucket, then, most importantly, Brayden's idea of convergence to a stationary Gaussian process, and my suggestions to concentrate on the lack of outside roots. The goal is to disprove the strong law $R_n/\log n\to2/\pi$ a.s. by showing\[ \mathbb{P}\bigg(\liminf_{n\to\infty}\frac{R_n}{\log n}
      \le \frac1\pi+\delta\Big)>0 \]for any small $\delta>0$. Instead of $\sum_{k=0}^n \xi_j x^j$ we study the reversed polynomial\[ Q_n(x):=\sum_{j=0}^n \xi_{n-j}x^j. \]We take, like, $L_n=4\log\log n$ and define a (reversed) "bucket"\[ I_n:=[-1+L_n/n,\,1-L_n/n]. \]We consider the events $B_n$ that $Q_n$ has no roots in $I_n$. The estimate\[ \mathbb P(B_n)\asymp (L_n/n)^{3/8} \]is obtained by the change of variables $x=\tanh(t/2)$, under which the normalized Gaussian power series converges to the stationary Gaussian process\[ Y_t=\sqrt{1-\tanh^2(t/2)}\,\sum_{j\ge0}\xi_j\tanh^j(t/2),
      \quad \mathbb{E} Y_sY_t=\operatorname{sech}\Big(\frac{s-t}{2}\bigg), \]and by applying the persistence asymptotics\[ \mathbb{P}(Y_t>0\ \forall |t|\le T)\asymp e^{-3T/8}. \]Next, the decorrelation bound\[ \mathbb{P}(B_n\cap B_{n+h}) \ll_\varepsilon
      (L_n/n)^{3/8}(L_h/h)^{3/8}, \qquad h\ge n^\varepsilon \]is shown using the exact decomposition $Q_{n+h}=F_h+x^h Q_n$, where the "fresh block" $F_h$ is independent of the old coefficients. Kochen-Stone then gives a positive-probability event on which $B_n$ occurs for infinitely many $n$. The remaining endpoint strips\[ [-1,-1+L_n/n]\cup[1-L_n/n,1] \]are controlled by Rolle's theorem, giving, with a large probability, that there are at most $(\delta/2)\log n$ zeros there. Thus, together,\[ \liminf_{n\to\infty}\frac{R_n(\mathbb{R}\setminus[-1,1])}{\log n}
      \leq \delta \]with a positive probability.

  • Regarding the coefficients in $\{-1,1\}$ (the mean zero setting), there is one detail that might still be missing. Namely, (my dear bro) Yen proved in the paper that msawhney mentioned:\[ \frac{N_n([-1,1])}{\log n} \to \frac{1}{\pi} \ a.s., \]where $N_n(A)$ counts the number of roots in $A\subseteq\mathbb{R}$. What’s still missing is\[ \frac{N_n((-\infty,-1)\cup(1,\infty))}{\log n} \to \frac{1}{\pi} \ a.s. \]If $N_n(\mathbb{R})=(2/\pi+o(1))\log n$ was merely to be shown "with large probability" (in the sense of terminology used, say, with random graphs), which is the ancient result, and not "almost surely", then the distribution of roots for a fixed $n$ would be symmetric under the reflection $z\to1/z$, so the other convergence claim would follow automatically. However, this reflection reverses the coefficients, so (for a fixed time segment $1,\ldots,n$) it reverses time, and the joint distribution of the full latter infinite sequence of random variables is no longer related to the former one. You can notice that Yen talks about this in Section 7. In fact, v1 of the paper on the arXiv made precisely that logical mistake, incorrectly claiming the a.s. convergence of $N_n(\mathbb{R})/\log n$ and later retracting it.

    • This looks like an important distinction [and Yen Do has privately communicated similar points to me in recent discussions]. Heuristically, for each $1 \leq j \leq \log_2 n$, there should be $O(1)$ zeroes of $f=f_n$ in the region $[-1+2^{-j-1}, -1+2^{-j}]$, and $O(1)$ zeroes in the region $[-1-2^{-j}, -1-2^{-j-1}]$. In the former case it's the coefficients $\epsilon_i$ for $i \ll 2^j$ that should largely control the zero count, but in the latter case it is the $\epsilon_i$ for $n-i \ll 2^j$. So the former is heuristically quite stable in $n$ (say for $n \in [n_0, 2n_0]$) but the latter can be quite unstable, and in particular the situation for $f_n$ and $f_{n'}$ should behave in some uncorrelated fashion for $n'-n \gg 2^j$. Assuming that each $f_n$ has some positive probability of not having a zero in $[-1-2^{-j}, -1-2^{-j-1}]$, this should cause the zero count here to be quite unstable, almost certainly fluctuating by $O(1)$ for $n \in [n_0,2n_0]$ if say one is in the regime $n_0^{0.1} \leq 2^j \leq n_0^{0.9}$. Heuristically, this would then predict the global zero count to then fluctuate by more than $O(1)$. But it may be quite challenging to make this intuition precise - there could be some "global conspiracy" that makes all these fluctuations magically cancel each other even if locally they behave like a nice process such as a Gaussian process. In particular the variance of the global count could experience some ridiculous cancellation that makes it smaller than the variance of the local zero counts. I don't see how to prevent this sort of getting extremely accurate control of the process (with error terms better than $o(1/n)$ on various pair correlation functions). Even the mean zero gaussian coefficient case, which is presumably the simplest model, looks a bit tough here.

      EDIT: maybe a switching approach? Generate two correlated samples $\epsilon_j, \epsilon'_j$ of the coefficients, which are largely the same but differ at some carefully chosen, well spaced locations, so that they have largely independent impacts on the zero statistics. If one can show a lower bound on the variance of the difference of the global zero count for the $\epsilon_j$ polynomial and the global zero count for the $\epsilon_{j'}$ polynomial after taking a supremum over some suitable sequence of $n$, then one might be able to disprove the claimed strong law thanks to the (contrapositive of the) triangle inequality. The hope here is that by carefully titrating the amount of switching, one might be able to cancel out most of the weird and hard to understand global correlation structure and work with something more like the sum of random variables with very low covariances between them, which is something we have a chance of getting variance lower bounds on. It might also be useful to design the joint distribution of the $\epsilon_j$ and $\epsilon'_j$ so that certain partial sums or moments agree, in order to damp out long range correlations.

      • Here is some more detail on the switching strategy. Pick a large $n$, then let $n < n_1 < \dots < n_m < 2n$ be $m \sim n^{0.7}$ "checkpoints" separated by $\sim n^{0.3}$. We introduce intervals $I_j = [n_j - n^{0.2}, n_j - n^{0.1}]$ to the left of each checkpoint $n_j$. If the almost sure conjecture is true, then for any fixed $\varepsilon>0$ we will have
        $$ {\bf P}( \sup_{1 \leq j \leq m} |N_{n_j}({\bf R}) - \frac{1}{\pi} \log n| \leq \varepsilon \log n ) = 1 - o(1).$$
        Now let us condition all the $\epsilon_k$ outside of $\bigcup_{j=1}^m I_j$ to be fixed, so that only the coefficients inside $I_1,\dots,I_m$ remain random. Let $\epsilon'_k$ be a resampling of $\epsilon_k$ subject to this fixing, so the signs of $\epsilon'_k$ agree with $\epsilon_k$ outside of the $I_1,\dots,I_m$ but are completely resampled within these intervals. Actually we should not do a completely independent resampling; we should fix means,
        $$ \sum_{k \in I_j} \epsilon'_k = \sum_{k \in I_j} \epsilon_k$$
        and actually I think we should fix a few more moments,
        $$ \sum_{k \in I_j} k^a \epsilon'_k = \sum_{k \in I_j} k^a \epsilon_k \quad (1)$$
        for $a=0,\dots,1000$. I believe that this should be doable with some local limit theorems - it only is costing a small amount of "entropy" - but let me ignore this issue for this sketch.

        If we let $N'_{n_j}$ be the zero counting function associated to this new coefficients then by the triangle inequality we have
        $$ {\bf P}( \sup_{1 \leq j \leq m} |N_{n_j}({\bf R}) - N'_{n_j}({\bf R})| \leq 2\varepsilon \log n ) = 1 - o(1).$$
        The point is that the way we have constructed things, the zeroes of $f_{n_j}$ and $f'_{n_j}$ are nearly identical except at $[-1-n^{-0.21}, -1-n^{-0.09}]$: any further away from $-1$ and the only coefficients that matter are those that are between $n_j$ and $I_{j}$, and any closer to $-1$ then the moment conditions (1) and some Taylor expansion should also make the polynomials agree nearly completely. So one should morally have
        $$ {\bf P}( \sup_{1 \leq j \leq m} |N_{n_j}([-1-n^{-0.21}, -1-n^{-0.09}]) - N'_{n_j}([-1-n^{-0.21}, -1-n^{-0.09}])| \leq 3\varepsilon \log n ) = 1 - o(1).$$
        Let's write this as
        $$ {\bf P}( \bigwedge_{j=1}^m E_j ) = 1-o(1)$$
        where $E_j$ is the event that $|N_{n_j}([-1-n^{-0.21}, -1-n^{-0.09}]) - N'_{n_j}([-1-n^{-0.21}, -1-n^{-0.09}])| \leq 3\varepsilon \log n$. Morally, this event depends on the signs of $\epsilon_k, \epsilon'_k$ on $I_j$, but is almost completely independent of the signs at the other $I_{j'}$, because of they are so far away (at least $n^{0.3}$ away). So, after some minor adjustments, one should be able to replace $E_j$ by completely independent events. So now this means that
        $$ {\bf P}( E_j ) = 1-o(1/m) = 1 - o(n^{-0.7})$$
        for at least one of the $j$. But hopefully some lower bound variance calculation can rule this out since $0.7$ is large compared to the exponent $0.21$ that is controlling the scale here.

        • Reply to “I believe that this should be doable with some local limit theorems – it only is costing a small amount of entropy”

          Yes — this step can be made precise using a multivariate lattice local limit theorem, and the entropy cost can be quantified explicitly.

          Let $I_j$ be one of your resampling blocks, of length $\ell_j \asymp n^{0.2}$. Let $
          \eta_k = 2\epsilon_k - 1 \in \{-1,1\}.
          $ Fix $A \ge 0$ (e.g. $A=1000$) and set $d := A+1$. For $v_k = (k^0,\dots,k^A)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^d$ write $$
          S_j(\epsilon) := \sum_{k\in I_j} \epsilon_k v_k,
          \qquad S_j^{(\pm)}(\eta) := \sum_{k\in I_j} \eta_k v_k.
          $$ If $S_j(\epsilon) = T$ for some target $T \in \mathbb{Z}^d$, this is equivalent to $$
          S_j^{(\pm)}(\eta) = 2T - \sum_{k\in I} v_k =: t'.
          $$ The map $\epsilon \mapsto \eta = 2\epsilon - 1$ is a bijection $\{0,1\}^I \to \{\pm1\}^I$ which sends the $\{0,1\}$ moment constraint $S_j(\epsilon) = T$ to the $\{\pm1\}$ moment constraint $S_j^{(\pm)}(\eta) = t'$. In particular, the feasible sets $$
          \Omega_T^{(0/1)} := \{\epsilon\in\{0,1\}^I : S_j(\epsilon) = T\},
          \qquad \Omega_{t'}^{(\pm)} := \{\eta\in\{\pm1\}^I : S_j^{(\pm)}(\eta) = t'\}
          $$ satisfy $$
          |\Omega_T^{(0/1)}| = |\Omega_{t'}^{(\pm)}|,
          $$ so counting solutions (or entropy) in the $\{0,1\}$ model is exactly the same as in the $\{\pm1\}$ model, up to an explicit shift of the target vector.


          The moment–matching constraint on $I_j$ is $$
          \sum_{k\in I_j} k^a \eta_k = \sum_{k\in I_j} k^a \epsilon_k
          \quad (a=0,\dots,A),
          $$ i.e. $S_j(\eta) = t'_j := S_j(\epsilon)$. The feasible set $$
          \Omega_j := \{ \eta \in \{\pm1\}^{I_j} : S_j(\eta) = t'_j \}
          $$ has cardinality $$
          |\Omega_j| = 2^{\ell_j} \cdot \mathbb{P}(S_j = t'_j),
          $$ where the probability is for $\eta_k$ i.i.d. uniform $\pm 1$.

          Now apply the $d$-dimensional lattice LLT for bounded independent vectors with nondegenerate covariance $$
          \Sigma_j := \sum_{k\in I_j} v_k v_k^\top.
          $$ Uniformly for $t'_j$ in the central region, $$
          \mathbb{P}(S_j = t'_j)
          = (2\pi)^{-d/2} (\det\Sigma_j)^{-1/2} e^{ -\frac12 t'_j{}^\top \Sigma_j^{-1} t'_j }
          + o\big((\det\Sigma_j)^{-1/2}\big).
          $$ A Cauchy–Binet + Vandermonde computation gives $\det\Sigma_j \asymp_d \ell_j^{\,d^2}$, so $$
          \mathbb{P}(S_j = t'_j) \asymp_d \ell_j^{-d^2/2}.
          $$ Thus $$
          |\Omega_j| \asymp_d 2^{\ell_j} \, \ell_j^{-d^2/2}.
          $$ Since the conditional distribution on $\Omega_j$ is uniform, its Shannon entropy is $$
          H(\Omega_j) = \log_2 |\Omega_j|
          = \ell_j - \frac{d^2}{2} \log_2 \ell_j + O_d(1).
          $$ Compared to the $\ell_j$ bits of an unconstrained block, the entropy loss is $\frac{d^2}{2} \log_2 \ell_j = O(\log n)$ per block. Over $m \asymp n^{0.7}$ blocks, the total loss is $O(n^{0.7} \log n) = o(n^{0.9})$, which is indeed “a small amount of entropy” in the sense of your switching argument.
