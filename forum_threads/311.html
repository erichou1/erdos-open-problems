OPEN This is open, and cannot be resolved with a finite computation.
Let $\delta(N)$ be the minimal non-zero value of $\lvert 1-\sum_{n\in A}\frac{1}{n}\rvert$ as $A$ ranges over all subsets of $\{1,\ldots,N\}$. Is it true that\[\delta(N)=e^{-(c+o(1))N}\]for some constant $c\in (0,1)$?
#311: [ErGr80,p.40]
number theory | unit fractions

Comment activity that has not yet been incorporated into the remarks
There are claims of partial results and/or a path to a solution in the comments.
It is trivial that\[\delta(N)\geq \frac{1}{[1,\ldots,N]}=e^{-(1+o(1))N},\]where $[1,\ldots,N]$ is the least common multiple of $\{1,\ldots,N\}$.

The formulation in [ErGr80] has the additional condition that $A$ contain no $S$ such that $\sum_{n\in S}\frac{1}{n}=1$, but Kovac in the comments has shown that the simpler formulation above is equivalent.

Tang has shown that\[\delta(N) \leq \exp\left(-c\frac{N}{(\log N\log\log N)^3}\right)\]for some constant $c>0$.

  • Two random observations:

    1) Even proving $\delta(N)> \frac{1}{[1,\dots, N]}$ seems nontrivial. For this to occur, we must have either $\frac{[1,\dots, N]}{p} \equiv 1 \bmod p$ for all primes $p$ in $(N/2,N]$ or $\frac{[1,\dots, N]}{p} \equiv -1 \bmod p$ for all primes $p$ in $(N/2,N]$, which seems incredibly unlikely, but may be hard to rule out.

    Proving this would also establish a special case of problem #317, which is open.

    2) Has anyone produced a heuristic guess for the value of $c$? If we assume that the values $\sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n}$ are locally distributed something like randomly, than the largest gap from $0$ should be something like $\epsilon/ |\{ A \subseteq [N] \mid | \sum_{n\in A} \frac{1}{n} -1 |< \epsilon \}|$ and the denominator should be estimable by entropy methods: I believe it is $e^{(c+o(1))n }$ where $c=\int_0^1 h(e^{-\alpha/t})dt$ where $\alpha$ solves $\int_0^1 e^{ -\alpha/t} dt/t=1$ and $h$ is the binary entropy function. So this value of $c$ is plausible.

    • Mehtaab Sawhney pointed out to me over email that the number of distinct values of $\sum_{n \in A } \frac{1}{n}$ over $A \subseteq \{1,\dots,N\}$ is $e^{ o(N)}$. Indeed, let $P(n)$ be the largest prime factor of $n$ we can write $ \sum_{n\in A} \frac{1}{n} = \sum_{\substack{n\in A \\ P(n) > \frac{N}{\log N}}} \frac{1}{n} + \sum_{\substack{n\in A \\ P(n) \leq \frac{N}{\log N}}} \frac{1}{n}$. The first term takes $2^{ O ( \frac{ N \log \log N}{\log N } )}$ distinct values since there are $O ( \frac{ N \log \log N}{\log N } )$ numbers $n \leq N$ with $P(n)> \frac{N}{\log N}$ (multiples of $p$ for $p \in [X,2X]$ contribute $O(N/\log N)$, and there are $O(\log \log N)$ such dyadic intervals). The second term has denominator dividing $[1,\dots,\frac{N}{\log N}] = e^{ O(\frac{N}{\log N})}$ times a lower-order factor coming from prime powers and thus takes $e^{ O(\frac{N}{\log N})}$ distinct values. Thus the total number of distinct values is $e^{ O ( \frac{ N \log \log N}{\log N } )}$.

      Based on this, it is reasonable to guess that $\delta(N) \geq e^{- O ( \frac{ N \log \log N}{\log N } )}$ and therefore that the question has a negative answer.

      If $\delta(N) \geq e^{- O ( \frac{ N \log \log N}{\log N } )}$ then the most natural route to prove the negative answer is to prove that for each $c>0$ we have $\delta(N) > e^{-cN}$ infinitely often. I don't know how to do this, but I can at least make some progress: I claim $\delta(N)> e^{-cN}$ infinitely often for all $c> \frac{5}{6}$.

      To see this, let $\epsilon_N\in \{+1,-1\}$ be such that $1+ \epsilon_N \delta_N = \sum_{n\in A} \frac{1}{n}$ for some $A \subseteq [N]$. Consider $N$ such that $\delta(N) < \delta(N-1)$, of which infinitely many certainly exist. For each prime $p> \frac{N}{2}$, regardless of whether $\frac{1}{p}$ contributes to the sum with value $1+\epsilon_N \delta(N)$, $\frac{1}{p}$ contributes to the sum with value $1- \epsilon_{N-1} \delta(N-1)$, or both, $p$ cannot divide the denominators of all three of $\delta(N), \delta(N-1), \epsilon N \delta(N) - \epsilon_{N-1} \delta(N-1)$. Hence the product of these three denominators is at most $ [1,\dots, N]^3 \prod_{ N/2< p \leq N} p $ and thus one of these three denominators is at most $ [1,\dots, N] \prod_{ N/2< p \leq N} p^{1/3} $, so that one of these three quantities is at least $\frac{1}{ [1,\dots, N] \prod_{ N/2< p \leq N} p^{1/3} }$, which implies that either $\delta(N)$ or $\delta(N-1)$ is at least $\frac{1}{ 2[1,\dots, N] \prod_{ N/2< p \leq N} p^{1/3} }= e^{-(5/6+o(1)) N} = e^{-( (5/6)+o(1))(N-1)}$, as desired.

      • By a similar argument, I can show that if the asymptotic $\delta(N) = e^{-(c+o(1))N}$ holds, then $c \leq 1- \sum_{\ell=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1)|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell\}\}|}\approx .69$

        To see this. Let $\epsilon\in \{+1,-1\}$ and $A_N\subseteq \{1,\dots, N\}$ be such that $1+\epsilon_N \delta_N= \sum_{n\in A_N} \frac{1}{N}$. Note that this asymptotic implies that if $\delta(N_1)=\delta(N_2)$ then $N_2 = (1+o(1)) N_1$. Take $\alpha>0$ small and $X$ large and sample integers $N_1,N_2$ independently and uniformly at random from the interval $[(1-\alpha) X, X]$. The probability that $\delta_{N_1}=\delta{N_2}$ is $o(1)$.

        For any prime $p$, for $p$ to divide the denominator of $\epsilon_{N_1} \delta(N_1) - \epsilon_{N_2} \delta(N_2)$, we must have $\sum_{\substack{n\in A_{N_1} \\ p \mid n}} \frac{1}{n} \neq \sum_{\substack{n\in A_{N_2} \\ p \mid n}} \frac{1}{n}$ which occurs with probability at most $1- \frac{1}{|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell_p\}\}|}$ where $\ell_p = \lfloor \frac{X}{p} \rfloor$ since two independent random samples of a random variable with at most $C$ values agree with probability at least $\frac{1}{C}$.

        Thus the expected value of the logarithm of the denominator of $\epsilon_{N_1} \delta(N_1) - \epsilon_{N_2} \delta(N_2)$ (where by convention the denominator of $0$ is $1$) is at most $\sum_{p \leq \sqrt{X}} \lfloor \frac{\log X}{\log p} \rfloor \log p + \sum_{\sqrt{X} < p \leq X} (1- \frac{1}{|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell_p\}\}|})\log p = (1- \sum_{\ell=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1)|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell\}\}|} +o(1)) X$ since the $p$ with $\ell_p=\ell$ are the $p$ in the interval $(X/(\ell+1),X/\ell]$ and the sum of $\log p$ over all such $p$ is $(1+o(1) )\frac{X}{\ell (\ell+1)}$.

        Since probability that $\delta_{N_1}=\delta{N_2}$ is $o(1)$, the same asymptotic holds if we switch convention that the denominator of $0$ is $[1,\dots, N]$. Thus there must be sum $N_1, N_2$ where $\epsilon_{N_1} \delta_{N_1} -\epsilon_2 \delta_{N_2}$ has logarithm of denominator at most $ (1- \sum_{\ell=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1)|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell\}\}|} +o(1)) X$ and thus is at least $ e^{-(1- \sum_{\ell=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1)|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell\}\}|} +o(1)) X}$. Then either $\delta_{N_1}$ or $\delta_{N_2}$ must be at least that big, and taking $\alpha\to 0$, we get $N$ arbitrarily large with $ \delta(N) \geq e^{-(1- \sum_{\ell=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1)|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell\}\}|} +o(1)) N}$. It follows that we must have $c\leq 1- \sum_{\ell=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1)|\{ \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n} \mid A \subseteq\{1,\dots,\ell\}\}|}$, as desired.

  • Using a result of Liu--Sawhney (arXiv:2404.07113v1), I obtained the following nontrivial upper bound for the quantity $\delta(N)$ in this problem: for some absolute $c_0>0$ and all large $N$,\[
    \delta(N)\le \exp \left(-c_0\,\frac{N}{(\log N)^3(\log\log N)^4}\right).
    \]Details are given in this note.

  • This formulation, which appeared in the Erdős-Graham problem book, is slightly awkward, because the asked minimal quantity, call it $\delta(N)$, is easily seen to be the same as\[ \delta’(N) := \min \Big( \Big\{ \Big|1-\sum_{n\in A}\frac{1}{n}\Big| : A\subseteq\{1,2,\ldots,N\} \Big\} \cap (0,\infty) \Big). \]Namely, if $A$ contains a strict subset $S$ with reciprocals of its elements summing to $1$, then\[ \sum_{n\in A} \frac{1}{n} - 1 \geq \frac{1}{N} + \sum_{n\in S} \frac{1}{n} - 1 = \frac{1}{N}, \]which is “quite large”. It certainly does not make the asymptotics of $\delta(N)$ and $\delta’(N)$ different, but with a bit of brute force verification for small $N$, one can show that, in fact, $\delta(N)= \delta’(N)$ for every $N\geq1$.
    The first $25$ values of $\delta(N)$ can be computed as:
    \begin{align*}
    & 1,\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{6},\frac{1}{12},\frac{1}{30},\\
    & \frac{1}{30},\frac{1}{105},\frac{1}{120},\frac{1}{252},\frac{1}{360},\\
    & \frac{1}{2310},\frac{1}{2310},\frac{1}{2310},\frac{1}{2772},\frac{1}{2772},\\
    & \frac{1}{6160},\frac{1}{30940},\frac{1}{30940},\frac{5}{298452},\frac{5}{298452},\\
    & \frac{11}{1244880},\frac{23}{17907120},\frac{1}{1105104},\frac{1}{1105104},\frac{1}{1593900}.
    \end{align*}
    From this modest amount of data one can see\[ e^{-0.71N} \leq \delta(N) \leq e^{-0.52 N} \quad \text{for }3\leq N\leq 25, \]so the conjecture is somewhat plausible. Also note that\[ \frac{1}{[1,\ldots,N]} = e^{-(1+o(1))N}, \]so the conjecture claims that $\delta(N)$ is exponentially small, but still much larger than the trivial bound.
    All this is quite trivial, but I wrote it down anyway, before I forget. :-)
