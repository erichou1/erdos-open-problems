Suppose $A\subseteq \{1,\ldots,N\}$ is such that if $a,b\in A$ and $a\neq b$ then $a+b\nmid ab$. Can $A$ be 'substantially more' than the odd numbers?

What if $a,b\in A$ with $a\neq b$ implies $a+b\nmid 2ab$? Must $\lvert A\rvert=o(N)$?

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
The connection to unit fractions comes from the observation that $\frac{1}{a}+\frac{1}{b}$ is a unit fraction if and only if $a+b\mid ab$.

Wouter van Doorn has given an elementary argument that proves that if $A\subseteq \{1,\ldots,N\}$ has $\lvert A\rvert \geq (25/28+o(1))N$ then $A$ must contain $a\neq b$ with $a+b\mid ab$ (see the discussion in [301]).

See also [302].

These include the numbers with prime factorization $p_1 \times \dots \times p_k$ such that $p_1\times \dots \times p_{i-1} \leq \frac{p_i}{2}$ for all $i$.   • (This comment has been moved here from 1063.)

    We obtain $f_1(N) \leq (0.7769 + o(1))N$ and $f_2(N) \leq (0.7630 + o(1))N$. Both improve van Doorn's $25/28$ bound. Writeup at https://leon2k2k2k.github.io/erdos327.pdf

    The idea is a smooth--rough decomposition. Fix a finite set of primes $P$ and write each integer $n = m \cdot c$ with $c$ $P$-smooth and $m$ $P$-rough; this partitions any candidate set $A$ into fibers $A_m = \{c : mc \in A\}$, and the $k$-admissibility constraint passes through (each fiber is itself $k$-admissible). The global bound on $|A|$ then reduces to bounding $k$-admissible subsets of the smooth integers, weighted by the density of rough multipliers --- a finite quantity for any cutoff. The headline numbers above are computed at $P = \{2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13\}$ and cutoff at $2000$. Running the same machinery on the $a + b \mid 3ab$ variant gives $f_3(N) \leq (0.6089 + o(1))N$, hinting that $f_2(N) \geq (1/2 + o(1))N$. Found with the help of GPT-5.5 + Rethlas; verified by hand.

  • An observation, more general states for sets with no $a<b$ satisfying $a+b \mid k ab$, which seemed important at some point, but didn't do the job at the end:

    Let $P(x)$ denote the largest prime divisor of $x$.

    Let $A=\{1 \le x \le N \colon P(x) \ge (kN)^{1/2} \}$.
    Now if $a<b$ both belong to $A$, where $P(a)=p$ and $P(b)=q$ are the largest prime divisors.
    If $p \not =q$, then $a+b$ is relative prime with both $p$ and $q$ and thus $pq(a+b) \nmid kab$ since $pq(a+b)>kN (a+b)>kab.$
    If $p=q$, the property repeats on $a/p,b/p$.

  • If $A$ satisfies the condition such that for $a,b\in A$ and $a\neq b$ then $a+b \nmid ab$, we say $A$ satisfies (1). If $B$ satisfies the condition such that for $a,b\in B$ and $a\neq b$ then $a+b \nmid 2ab$, we say $B$ satisfies (2). Then there exists an $A\subseteq\{1,\ldots,2N\}$ satisfying (1) and a constant $c>0$ such that $A$ contains $cN$ even numbers if and only if there exists a $B\subseteq\{1,\ldots,N\}$ satisfying (2) such that $|B|=cN$. So a positive answer to the first question implies a negative answer to the third question.

    Let $A\subseteq\{1,\ldots,2N\}$ satisfy (1) and let $B\subseteq A$ be the set of even numbers in $A$. Assume there exists $c>0$ such that $|B|=cN$. Then for any $2a,2b\in B$ where $2a\neq 2b$, we have $2a+2b\nmid 4ab$. Equivalently, $a+b\nmid 2ab$. Then letting $C=\{a\mid 2a\in B\}$, we see that $C$ satisfies (2), $C\subseteq \{1,\ldots,N\}$ and $|C|=cN$.

    Let $B\subseteq\{1,\ldots,N\}$ satisfy (2) such that $|B|=cN$. Then for $a,b\in B$, $a\neq b$, we have $a+b\nmid 2ab$. Equivalently, $2a+2b\nmid 4ab$. Let $A=\{2a\mid a\in B\}$. Then we have $A\subseteq\{1,\ldots, 2N\}$, $A$ satisfies (1) and $A$ contains $cN$ even numbers.
