Let $h(n)$ count the number of powerful (if $p\mid m$ then $p^2\mid m$) integers in $[n^2,(n+1)^2)$. Estimate $h(n)$. In particular is there some constant $c>0$ such that\[h(n) < (\log n)^{c+o(1)}\]and, for infinitely many $n$,\[h(n) >(\log n)^{c-o(1)}?\]
#942: [Er76d,p.35]

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
Erdős writes it is not hard to prove that $\limsup h(n)=\infty$, and that the density $\delta_l$ of integers for which $h(n)=l$ exists and $\sum \delta_l=1$.

A proof that $h(n)$ is unbounded is provided by van Doorn in the comments.

De Koninck and Luca [DeLu04] have proved, for infinitely many $n$,\[h(n) \gg \left(\frac{\log n}{\log\log n}\right)^{1/3}.\]They also give the density ($\approx 0.275$) of those $n$ such that $h(n)=1$.

The argument of [DeLu04] in fact makes two inefficient choices, as observed by Hughes (with AI assistance). Appropriately optimised, as described in the comment section, the construction of [DeLu04] (see also [DLS05]) yields infinitely many $n$ such that\[h(n) \gg \frac{\log n}{\log\log n\log\log\log n}.\]

  • Two updates, including an answer to Will's question on the upper bound.

    Upper bound. The elementary $h(n)\ll n^{2/5}$ can be sharpened by the classical integer-points-close-to-a-curve technology. Writing each powerful $m=a^2b^3$ with $b$ squarefree, $h(n)$ counts integer points within $\approx b^{-3/2}$ of the curve $a^2b^3=n^2$; applying the Filaseta-Trifonov estimate (PLMS 1996, Thm. 4.1) in both the $a$- and $b$-aspects, dyadically, with the worst block at the symmetric scale $a\asymp b\asymp n^{2/5}$, gives
    $$h(n)\ll_\varepsilon n^{6/25+\varepsilon}.$$
    The exponent $6/25$ is exactly Swinnerton-Dyer's on-curve value $N^{3/5}$ at $N=n^{2/5}$, and it is the natural limit of this divided-difference (Filaseta-Trifonov / Swinnerton-Dyer) framework: the higher-order ($k\ge 3$) tests that would go below it need a window narrower than the interval $(n^2,(n+1)^2)$, of length $\asymp n$, allows (the admissibility condition $\delta\le c_k N^{-(k-1)}$ fails at the critical scale), and reaching $n^{1/5}$ would require a positive-width analogue of Bombieri-Pila's on-curve count in the regime $T\ge M$, which Trifonov (1998, 2002) records as open. So, as Will anticipated, arguments of this kind give a power of $n$ rather than a power of $\log n$; the truth is presumably still $(\log n)^{1+o(1)}$ (empirically $\max_{n\le 10^7} h(n)=9$).

    Lower bound, beyond infinitely-often. Using the same construction (De Koninck-Luca-Shparlinski with a primorial modulus, which, as Thomas pointed out, is not new), one can extract a frequency bound. An Erdos-Turan-Koksma discrepancy estimate together with a Liouville lower bound for $\sum_d a_d\sqrt d$ in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p_1},\dots,\sqrt{p_h})$ gives, unconditionally and uniformly in $V$,
    $$\#\{n\le x : h(n)\ge V\}\ \ge\ x^{1-o(1)} \qquad\text{for } 2\le V\le(\log x)^{1/2-\varepsilon}.$$
    This is a counting statement valid as $V\to\infty$; the known distribution results (Shiu; Xiong-Zaharescu) are for fixed $\ell$.

    On novelty: neither the construction nor the upper-bound method is new. The contributions are the reduction of the frequency question to simultaneous equidistribution of the directions $\{1/\sqrt d\}$ (made effective by the Liouville bound), and the explicit worst-case ($\theta=0$, interval length $\asymp\sqrt x$) upper exponent, which the short-interval literature, having optimized the asymptotic $\theta>0$ regime, did not record. The elementary cores of both sides are formalized in Lean 4 / Mathlib, with the three analytic inputs (the discrepancy estimate, the degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p_1},\dots,\sqrt{p_h}):\mathbb{Q}]=2^h$, and the Filaseta-Trifonov count) as explicit named axioms.

    Write-up and formalization: $\href{https://github.com/scottdhughes/erdos942}{\text{repository}}$ (paper: $\href{https://github.com/scottdhughes/erdos942/blob/main/paper/main.pdf}{\text{main.pdf}}$).

  • Any powerful number $\leq (n+1)^2$ can be represented as $a^2 b^3$ for some $a,b$, and we must have either $a \leq (n+1)^{\frac{2}{5}}$ or $b \leq (n+1)^{\frac{2}{5}}$. Moreover, only one $a$ can produce a number in $[n^2, (n+1)^2)$ for a given $b$, since the ratio of $(a+1)^2b^3$ to $a^2 b^3$ is $(a+1)^2/a^2 > (n+1)^2/n^2$, and similarly, only one $b$ can work for a given $a$. Thus the number of powerful numbers in $[n^2, (n+1)^2)$ is at most $2 (n+1)^{\frac{2}{5}}$.

    Surely this very simple argument can be improved, but it seems likely that any argument along these lines will give a power in $n$ rather than a power in $\log n$.

  • An improvement to the recorded lower bound. The remark above gives De Koninck–Luca's $h(n)\gg(\log n/\log\log n)^{1/3}$ infinitely often (extended by De Koninck–Luca–Shparlinski to all fixed $\kappa\ge2$); we are not aware of a previous improvement. Writing $h_\kappa(n)$ for the number of $\kappa$-full integers in $(n^\kappa,(n+1)^\kappa)$ (so $h_2=h$), we sharpen the exponent to $1-o(1)$:\[
    h_\kappa(n)\ \gg_\kappa\ \frac{\log n}{\log\log n\,\log\log\log n}\qquad\text{for infinitely many }n,
    \]for every fixed $\kappa\ge2$.

    As in De Koninck–Luca — and the unboundedness argument above — the engine is simultaneous Diophantine approximation; the new ingredient is the auxiliary numbers. Take $D=p_1\cdots p_h$ a primorial ($h$ minimal with $2^h-1\ge2\ell$) and $d_1,\dots,d_{2\ell}$ any $2\ell$ of its $2^h-1$ squarefree divisors $>1$. The box principle gives one $q$ with $\|q\,d_j^{-1/\kappa}\|\le\delta_j$ for all $j$; then $m_j=d_jD^\kappa r_j^\kappa$ ($r_j$ nearest to $q\,d_j^{-1/\kappa}$) are $\kappa$-full, distinct, and within $n^{\kappa-1}$ of $n^\kappa$ for $n=Dq$, the sign of $q\,d_j^{-1/\kappa}-r_j$ choosing the side; pigeonhole keeps $\ge\ell$ on one side. Since $D$ need only be a common multiple of the $d_j$, $h\asymp\log\ell$ primes supply $2^h-1$ directions at cost $\log D\asymp\log\ell\,\log\log\ell$ — which, with the fixed tolerance replacing the Roth-type condition of the earlier proofs, is what turns $\tfrac13$ into $1-o(1)$.

    The arithmetic core ($\kappa$-fullness, distinctness, and the smallest case $D=6$: two powerful numbers in $(2909^2,2910^2)$) is vibe formalized in Lean 4/Mathlib — zero sorries, standard axioms — here.

    One-sided, though: any $c$ as in the question must satisfy $c\ge1$ ($c=1$ the natural guess), but the upper bound $h(n)\le(\log n)^{1+o(1)}$ for all $n$ remains open.

    • Thanks - in fact, looking at the proof, this is almost identically the proof of De Koninck, Luca, and Shparlinski (which in turn is very similar to that in [DeLu04] for the $\kappa=2$ case) - indeed, the notation used is identical, so the LLM was clearly working off this. One difference is that one takes $D$ to be the product of the first $\asymp \log \ell$ primes, which suffices to ensure that $D$ has $\asymp \ell$ squarefree divisors, but now $D\leq \ell^{O(\log\log \ell)}$, an improvement over the bound $D\leq \ell^{O(\ell)}$ used in [DLS].

      (There is an interesting subtlety here - if one wants 'a small $D$ with $\ell$ distinct squarefree divisors' then one might be tempted to take the product of the $\ell$ smallest squarefree integers (as [DLS] do), but this grows much faster than the product of the first $\asymp \log \ell$ primes, so the latter is always a better choice. This, of course, is not a new observation and has been used many times, but seems to have been overlooked in this case.)

      Furthermore, the argument in [DLS] goes via an unnecessary appeal to Roth's theorem, which actually makes the final optimisation weaker.

      In particular, I'm not sure what you mean by 'the new ingredient is the auxiliary numbers' - everything in the argument (except this weaker choice of $D$) is already present in [DLS], except that for some reason they take an unnecessary detour via Roth's theorem rather than just applying Dirichlet directly to bound the size of $q$. (Looking at their paper I cannot tell why they do this; the LLM here is basically writing their argument in a more direct form.)

      Here is the entire argument. Let $D$ be the product of the first $h$ primes and let $d_i$ range over all $\ell=2^h$ many distinct divisors of $D$. Set $n=Dq$ ($q$ chosen later) and $m_j=d_j(Dr_j)^2$, where $r_j$ is the nearest integer to $qd_j^{-1/2}$. This is a collection of $\ell$ distinct powerful numbers, and (using the trivial bound $d_j\leq D$)\[m_j-n^2=D^2(d_jr_j^2-q^2)\ll n D^{3/2}\|q d_j^{-1/2}\|.\]By Dirichlet's principle there exists $q\leq Q$ such that $\| qd_j^{-1/2}\|\ll Q^{-1/\ell}$ for all $j$, whence if we choose $Q=D^{O(\ell)}$, say, then $\lvert m_j-n^2\rvert \ll n$, and hence by the pigeonhole principle there is some $m=n+O(n^{1/2})$ such that $h(m) \gg \ell$.

      Now $n\leq DQ\leq D^{O(\ell)}$, and by standard estimates $\log D\asymp \log \ell\log\log \ell$, so\[\log n\ll \ell \log D\ll \ell\log \ell\log\log \ell,\]and\[h(m)\gg \ell \gg \frac{\log m}{\log \log m\log\log\log m}.\](In [DLS] they do a strange detour, which basically amounts to saying $\| qd_j^{-1/2}\| \leq Q^{-1/\ell}$ for some $q\leq Q$, and then using Roth's theorem to deduce that $q>Q^{\frac{1-\epsilon}{\ell}}$, and then using (in a disguised form) the inequalities $\| qd_j^{-1/2}\| \leq Q^{-1/\ell}\leq q^{-1/\ell}\leq Q^{-\frac{1-\epsilon}{\ell^2}}$, so losing an extra $1/\ell$ in the exponent. So they lose two extra $\ell$ in their exponent - one here, and one with their inefficient choice of $D$, which is why they get an exponent of $1/3$ rather than $1$.)

  • ChatGPT's Deep Research tool gave me the following reference, where they prove much more than the unboundedness of $h(n)$:

    Jean-Marie De Koninck and Florian Luca, Sur la proximité des nombres puissants, Acta Arithmetica, Volume 114, Issue 2, 149-157, 2004.

    Their Theorem 1 states that $h(n)$ is infinitely often larger than $\frac{9}{20} \left(\frac{\log n}{\log \log n} \right)^{1/3}$, which is more or less a lower bound in the form that Erdős expected. Their proof makes use of Diophantine approximation results as well, so it seems that these techniques are important for this problem. De Koninck and Luca furthermore give the specific density of $n$ (roughly $0.275$) for which $h(n)$ is equal to $1$ (i.e. those $n$ for which the only powerful integer in the interval $[n^2, (n+1)^2)$ is $n^2$).

    Another (less relevant) reference that I found with ChatGPT is

    Tsz Ho Chan, A note on powerful numbers in short intervals, Bulletin of the Australian Mathematical Society, Volume 108, issue 1, 99-106, 2023.

    This paper gives a uniform upper bound of $\ll \frac{y}{\log y}$ on the number of powerful integers in any interval of length $y$, with a further $\log^c y$ improvement in the denominator if one assumes the abc conjecture. However, by the nature of its uniformity such results can never establish an upper bound of the form required here.

    All other results on powerful integers in short intervals that I managed to find (both with and without AI) concern intervals $(x, x + x^{c})$ with $c > \frac{1}{2}$, which are therefore not directly relevant to the question at hand.

  • The following proof that $h(n)$ is unbounded was found with the help of Gemini (but written down by me in my own words, so any mistakes are mine).

    Let $k \in \mathbb{N}$ be arbitrary, let $2 = p_1 < p_2 < \cdots < p_k$ be the first $k$ primes, and define $\beta_i = p_i \sqrt{p_i}$ for $1 \le i \le k$. We then define $\theta_i = \frac{\beta_k}{\beta_i}$ and $\alpha_i = 0$ for $1 \le i \le k-1$, while we let $\theta_k = \beta_k$ and $\alpha_k = \frac{1}{2}$. Finally, set $\epsilon = \frac{1}{4\beta_k}$.

    It follows from a theorem by Besicovitch (see here) that $1, \theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k$ are rationally independent so that we may apply Kronecker's approximation theorem. We therefore deduce that there exist integers $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k$ and $q$ such that $$\left|q \theta_i - x_i - \alpha_i \right| < \epsilon \hspace{10pt} (1)$$holds for all $1 \le i \le k$.

    Using $(1)$ for $i = k$ we obtain $$x_k + \frac{1}{4} < q \beta_k < x_k + \frac{3}{4},$$while using $(1)$ for some $i$ with $1 \le i \le k-1$ and multiplying by $\beta_i$ we obtain $$q \beta_k - \frac{1}{4} < x_i \beta_i < q \beta_k + \frac{1}{4}.$$In particular, by writing $n = x_k$ we have $$q \beta_k \in (n, n + 1)$$ and $$x_i \beta_i \in (n, n + 1)$$ for all $1 \le i \le k-1$. Squaring all of these values and we deduce $$(q \beta_k)^2 = q^2 p_k^3 \in (n^2, (n+1)^2)$$ and $$(x_i \beta_i)^2 = x_i^2 p_i^3 \in (n^2, (n+1)^2).$$In other words, there are at least $k$ powerful integers between $n^2$ and $(n+1)^2$, so that $h(n)$ is indeed unbounded as $k$ was arbitrary.
