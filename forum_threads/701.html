Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a family of sets closed under taking subsets (i.e. if $B\subseteq A\in\mathcal{F}$ then $B\in \mathcal{F}$). There exists some element $x$ such that whenever $\mathcal{F}'\subseteq \mathcal{F}$ is an intersecting subfamily we have\[\lvert \mathcal{F}'\rvert \leq \lvert \{ A\in \mathcal{F} : x\in A\}\rvert.\]
#701: [Er81,p.26]
combinatorics | intersecting family

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
A problem of Chvátal [Ch74], who proved it replacing the closed under subsets condition with the (stronger) condition that, assuming all sets in $\mathcal{F}$ are subsets of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$, whenever $A\in \mathcal{F}$ and there is an injection $f:B\to A$ such that $x\leq f(x)$ for all $x\in B$, then $B\in \mathcal{F}$.

Sterboul [St74] proved this when, letting $\mathcal{G}$ be the maximal sets (under inclusion) in $\mathcal{F}$, all sets in $\mathcal{G}$ have the same size, $\lvert A\cap B\rvert\leq 1$ for all $A\neq B\in \mathcal{G}$, and at least two sets in $\mathcal{G}$ have non-empty intersection.

Frankl and Kupavskii [FrKu23] have proved this when $\mathcal{F}$ has covering number $2$.

Borg [Bo11] has proposed a weighted generalisation of this conjecture, which he proves under certain additional assumptions.

    • $\textbf{potentially wrong}$ (it might be that one cannot pick an element which has the largest cardinality and so a counterexample is given on Math Overflow)

      The infinite case might be trivial:

      Assume $\lvert \mathcal{F}'\rvert$ is infinite.

      If the largest cardinality a particular trivial intersecting set can have is $|B|$, and a largest set has cardinality $|A|,$
      then $\lvert \mathcal{F}' \rvert \le |A| \times |B| = \max\{ |A|, |B|\}$.
      But there is an element belonging to $2^{|A|-1}$ many elements, which is a larger cardinality.

      So either $|B| \ge \lvert \mathcal{F}'\rvert$, or $2^{|A|-1}>\lvert \mathcal{F}'\rvert$.

  • A minor detail note: I believe Frankl and Kupavskii have proven the case where $\mathcal{F}'$ has covering number $2$, not $\mathcal{F}$ (their paper uses $\mathcal{G}$ and $\mathcal{F}$ to denote the sets referred to here as $\mathcal{F}$ and $\mathcal{F}'$, respectively). This result also seems to have been proven in 1972 by Kleitman and Magnanti [KM72] (p. 9, Theorem 2), as referenced in [EGP18].

    [KM72] D. J. Kleitman and T. L. Magnanti. On the Number of Latent Subsets of Intersecting Collections. Operations Research Center Working Papers (Oct. 1972). url: http://hdl.handle.net/1721.1/5343.

    [EGP18] Eifler, Leon, Ambros M. Gleixner, and Jonad Pulaj. Chvátal's Conjecture Holds for Ground Sets of Seven Elements. Zuse Institute Berlin, 2018.

  • Here is the short proof in the special case where the rank of \(\mathcal F\) is at most \(2\). using Chatgpt 5.5

    Assume every member of \(\mathcal F\) has size at most \(2\). Then the \(2\)-element members of \(\mathcal F\) may be viewed as the edge set of a graph \(G\), and the \(1\)-element members as its vertices. Let \(\mathcal I\subseteq \mathcal F\) be an intersecting subfamily.

    If \(\emptyset\in\mathcal I\), then \(\mathcal I=\{\emptyset\}\), so the desired inequality is trivial as soon as \(\mathcal F\) has a nonempty member. Thus assume \(\emptyset\notin\mathcal I\).

    If \(\mathcal I\) contains a singleton, say \(\{x\}\in\mathcal I\), then every member of \(\mathcal I\) must contain \(x\). Hence\[
    \mathcal I\subseteq \{A\in\mathcal F:x\in A\},
    \]and therefore\[
    |\mathcal I|\leq |\{A\in\mathcal F:x\in A\}|.
    \]It remains to consider the case in which all members of \(\mathcal I\) have size \(2\). Then \(\mathcal I\) is a pairwise intersecting family of edges in a graph. Such a family is either a star of edges with a common endpoint, or it contains a triangle. Indeed, if two edges \(ab\) and \(ac\) are in \(\mathcal I\), then any further edge intersecting both is either incident with \(a\), or is the edge \(bc\). Thus either all edges share \(a\), or the only possible non-star configuration is the triangle \(ab,ac,bc\).

    In the star case, all edges in \(\mathcal I\) contain some vertex \(x\), and hence\[
    \mathcal I\subseteq \{A\in\mathcal F:x\in A\}.
    \]So the desired inequality follows.

    In the triangle case, say\[
    \mathcal I=\{\{a,b\},\{a,c\},\{b,c\}\}.
    \]Since \(\mathcal F\) is closed under taking subsets, it also contains \(\{a\}\). Hence the star at \(a\) contains at least\[
    \{a\},\quad \{a,b\},\quad \{a,c\}.
    \]Therefore\[
    |\{A\in\mathcal F:a\in A\}|\geq 3=|\mathcal I|.
    \]Thus, in all cases, there is an element \(x\) such that\[
    |\mathcal I|\leq |\{A\in\mathcal F:x\in A\}|.
    \]This proves the conjecture for hereditary families of rank at most \(2\).
