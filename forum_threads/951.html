Let $1<a_1<\cdots$ be a sequence of real numbers such that\[\left\lvert \prod_i a_i^{k_i}-\prod_j a_j^{\ell_j}\right\rvert \geq 1\]for every distinct pair of non-negative finitely supported integer tuples $k_i,\ell_j\geq 0$. Is it true that\[\#\{ a_i \leq x\} \leq \pi(x)?\]
#951: [Er69,p.82][Er77c,p.68]
number theory
The open status of this problem reflects the current belief of the owner of this website. There may be literature on this problem that I am unaware of, which may partially or completely solve the stated problem. Erdős [Er77c] said this question was asked 'during [his] lecture at Queens College [by] one member of the audience (perhaps S. Shapiro)'. (Although in [Er80] he seems sure it was Shapiro, but also recalled that he had himself asked this in [Er69].) In [Er80] he further asks whether equality holds if and only if the $a_i$ are the set of primes.

Such a sequence of $a_i$ is sometimes called a set of Beurling prime numbers (and the sequence of products called the associated generalised integers).

Beurling conjectured that if the number of reals in $[1,x]$ of the form $\prod a_i^{k_i}$ is $x+o(\log x)$ then the $a_i$ must be the sequence of primes.

It is unclear whether this is intended to hold for all $x$ or just all sufficiently large $x$. The former question can be disproved by finite calculation (since any finite sequence of $a_i$ can be extended to a suitable infinite sequence via a greedy algorithm). A finite counterexample for $x=10$ was found by ChatGPT-5.2 Pro (prompted by Leeham).

  • This note by me+ChatGPT shows, for each $3 \le n \le 7$, existence of $n$ generators less than $p_n$, the $n$th prime, for a free multiplicative semigroup whose elements differ by at least $1$.

    This is basically an operationalized version of Tao's probabilistic sketch in the comments.

    By the Barreto-Price extension step, this shows that the inequality in the problem can fail for $x = 5-\varepsilon, 7-\varepsilon, 11-\varepsilon, 13-\varepsilon, 17-\varepsilon$ for small $\varepsilon > 0$. We think it can fail for arbitarily large $x$ but do not know how to prove.

    $$ a_1 = 101/42 \approx 2.40, \quad a_2 = 367/103 \approx 3.56, \quad a_3 = 113/24 \approx 4.71 $$
    generate a free multiplicative semigroup whose elements differ by at least $1$.

    The approach here uses tools from computational Diophantine approximation, by Matveev + LLL reduction by de Weger.

    By using the extension step in Barreto-Leeham, we can extend to an infinite sequence. Since $a_3 < p_3 = 5$, the primes are not everywhere minimal, which is the same version of the problem disproved by Barreto-Leeham. The eventually minimal version appears more difficult and cannot be directly tackled by this method.

    • Nice result. The numerical computations in particular need to be verified, but there does not seem to be any theoretical reason why this would not work, though as Thomas points out the proof technique relies heavily on being able to produce a counterexample in the bounded $x$ regime (in fact $x=10$ in this argument) rather than the asymptotic regime $x \ggg 1$. This changes the game considerably, and the AI proof observes that one can now convert the problem to one in Diophantine approximation.

      The first observation is that once one is committed to producing a counterexample at a single value of $x$, the choices of $a_i$ larger than $x$ are pretty much irrelevant; they can be constructed by a standard greedy algorithm. So now one is looking at a multiplicative semigroup generated by a bounded number of generators $\{ a_i \leq x \}$, which is expected to become exponentially sparse at infinity (cf. Størmer's theorem or Baker's theorem). But as the AI observes, one does not need the fanciest transcendence theorems such as Baker's theorem involving logs of algebraic numbers, because the $a_i$ are not required to be algebraic; instead, by taking the $a_i$ to be exponentials of algebraic numbers, one now just needs good lower bounds on Diophantine approximation by algebraic numbers, for which the most classical of bounds - Liouville's bound - is already powerful, giving lower bounds that are polynomial in the height (after extending to higher-dimensional approximation in a standard fashion), whereas to get unit separation one needs lower bounds that are at worst exponentially decaying in the height. This is a lot of room, and already guarantees unit separation for all sufficiently large products, reducing things to a finite check. (This already shows by the way that merely assuming asymptotic unit separation would not be enough to get the required bound; if the problem had any hope of a positive answer, one would need unit separation at all scales, not just the large ones.)

      A human at this point would perhaps balk at the significant numerical experimentation one would now have to do to find a set of generators for which the required finite number of separation conditions actually hold; but apparently an AI (prompted sufficiently many times) has much less reluctance to attempt some sort of search (possibly brute force or trial-and-error in nature). What is not currently clear is how much "room" there is here - would any reasonable set of generator choices in a reasonable algebraic number field have had a good chance to succeed? Perhaps further numerical exploration will clarify this.

      As this question is not directly from Erdos, but from someone in the audience at a talk he gave (see [p. 68, Er77c]) it is harder than usual to divine intent for this question, and in particular whether $x$ was intended to range over all natural numbers or only sufficiently large ones. Both formulations of the question are interesting, but for different reasons: the latter is more in the spirit of "Beurling prime number theory", but the former has the flavor of a rigidity theorem (in the spirit of Hamburger's rigidity theorem for the zeta function). Another variant which could be interesting if one requires the $a_j$ to be algebraic, which rules out the AI construction and would require one to use the more transcendental Diophantine approximation theorems if one wanted to mimic the same strategy. (But perhaps one could still win by performing an infinitesimal perturbation of the transcendental example.)

      Given the social media circus from past 1(a) candidates, I think we can afford to take a more measured approach with evaluating this particular submission, so that we have time for literature review, Lean formalization, and numerical exploration. I will move it to the "pending assessment" category for now.

  • For $s>1$, one has $\sum_{k_1,k_2,\dots} \frac{1}{(\prod_i a_i^{k_i})^s} \leq \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^s}$, hence $\prod_i (1-\frac{1}{a_i^s})^{-1} \leq \zeta(s)$, so on taking logarithms $\sum_i \sum_{j=1}^\infty \frac{1}{j a_i^{js}} \leq \log \zeta(s)$. This may be enough to get a Mertens type bound, but the prime number theorem type bound seems out of reach by this method.
