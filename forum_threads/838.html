Let $f(n)$ be maximal such that any $n$ points in $\mathbb{R}^2$, with no three on a line, determine at least $f(n)$ different convex subsets. Estimate $f(n)$ - in particular, does there exist a constant $c$ such that\[\lim \frac{\log f(n)}{(\log n)^2}=c?\]
#838: [Er78c]
geometry | convex

A question of Erdős and Hammer. Erdős proved in [Er78c] that there exist constants $c_1,c_2>0$ such that\[n^{c_1\log n}<f(n)< n^{c_2\log n}.\]See also [107].

  • I believe one can optimise the hard lower bound to $c_1=1/8$ without the asymptotic o(1) using a similar counting argument.
    Let $f(n)$ be the maximum number of convex subsets in a set X of n points.
    ES theorem says that there exists a integer $m$ such that any subset of $m$ points guarantees the existence of at least one convex $r$-gon.

    The total number of of all possible subsets of size $m$ subsets is given by $\binom{n}{m}$

    accounting for overcounting, each unique convex $r$-gon is counted exactly\[
    \binom{n-r}{m-r}
    \]times. Therefore,\[
    f(n) \ge \frac{\binom{n}{m}}{\binom{n-r}{m-r}}
    \]Simplifying:\[
    f(n) \ge \frac{\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}}{\frac{m!}{r!(m-r)!}} = \frac{\binom{n}{r}}{\binom{m}{r}}
    \]Using $\frac{\binom{n}{r}}{\binom{m}{r}} >(\frac{n}{m})^{r}$
    This gives\[
    f(n) \ge \left(\frac{n}{m}\right)^r
    \]Substitute the E-Sz upper bound $m \le 4^r$,\[
    f(n) \ge \left(\frac{n}{2^{2r}}\right)^r = \frac{n^r}{2^{2r^2}}
    \]To optimise this, we set $r = \frac{1}{4} \log_2 n$ which gives\[
    f(n) \ge 2^{\frac{1}{8} (\log_2 n)^2}
    \]

  • This may be a standard observation, but I do not see it in the remarks. It gives an explicit lower constant using the present best form of the Erdős--Szekeres theorem and a short entropy/double-counting argument.

    I write all logarithms below in base $2$. By Suk's theorem, $ES(k)=2^{k+o(k)}$. Equivalently, every $T$-point set in general position contains a convex subset of size

    $$
    r=(1-o(1))\log_2 T.
    $$

    Let $P$ be an arbitrary set of $n$ points in the plane, with no three collinear. Choose uniformly a $T$-subset $R\subset P$, where

    $$
    T=\lfloor n^\alpha\rfloor,\qquad 0<\alpha<1.
    $$

    For each $R$, choose one convex $r$-subset $C(R)\subset R$. In entropy form,

    $$
    H(C(R))\ge H(R)-H(R\mid C(R)).
    $$

    Since

    $$
    H(R)=\log_2 {n\choose T}
    $$

    and, after $C(R)$ is known, there are at most ${n-r\choose T-r}$ possible choices for $R$, we get

    $$
    H(C(R))\ge \log_2 {n\choose T}-\log_2 {n-r\choose T-r}.
    $$

    Thus, if $N_r(P)$ denotes the number of convex $r$-subsets of $P$, then

    $$
    \log_2 N_r(P)\ge \log_2 {n\choose T}-\log_2 {n-r\choose T-r},
    $$

    or equivalently

    $$
    N_r(P)\ge { {n\choose T}\over {n-r\choose T-r}}
    = { {n\choose r}\over {T\choose r}}.
    $$

    Therefore

    $$
    \log_2 N_r(P)
    \ge r\log_2(n/T)-o((\log n)^2).
    $$

    Since $T=n^\alpha+O(1)$ and $r=(\alpha-o(1))\log_2 n$, this gives

    $$
    \log_2 N_r(P)
    \ge (\alpha(1-\alpha)-o(1))(\log_2 n)^2.
    $$

    Optimizing at $\alpha=1/2$, every $n$-point set in general position determines at least

    $$
    2^{(1/4-o(1))(\log_2 n)^2}
    $$

    convex subsets. Hence

    $$
    f(n)\ge 2^{(1/4-o(1))(\log_2 n)^2},
    $$

    and so

    $$
    \liminf_{n\to\infty}
    {\log_2 f(n)\over (\log_2 n)^2}
    \ge {1\over 4}.
    $$

    For comparison, the classical Erdős--Szekeres construction with $2^{k-2}$ points and no $k$ points in convex position gives, by taking $k=\lceil \log_2 n\rceil+O(1)$,

    $$
    f(n)\le \sum_{j<k}{n\choose j}
    \le \left({en\over k}\right)^k
    =
    2^{(1+o(1))(\log_2 n)^2}.
    $$

    Thus the current simple bounds, with base-$2$ logarithms, are

    $$
    {1\over 4}
    \le
    \liminf_{n\to\infty}
    {\log_2 f(n)\over(\log_2 n)^2}
    \le
    \limsup_{n\to\infty}
    {\log_2 f(n)\over(\log_2 n)^2}
    \le
    1.
    $$

    With natural logarithms this becomes

    $$
    {1\over 4\log 2}
    \le
    \liminf_{n\to\infty}
    {\log f(n)\over(\log n)^2}
    \le
    \limsup_{n\to\infty}
    {\log f(n)\over(\log n)^2}
    \le
    {1\over \log 2}.
    $$
