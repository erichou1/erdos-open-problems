Determine the infimum and supremum of\[\lvert \{ x\in \mathbb{R} : \lvert f(x)\rvert < 1\}\rvert\]as $f\in \mathbb{R}[x]$ ranges over all non-constant monic polynomials, all of whose roots are real and in the interval $[-1,1]$.
#1038: [EHP58,p.131]

A problem of Erdős, Herzog, and Piranian [EHP58], who proved that the measure of the set in question is always at most $2\sqrt{2}$ under the assumption that all the roots are in $\{-1,1\}$, and conjecture this is the best possible upper bound.

They also note that the infimum of the set in question is less than $2$, as witnessed by $f(x)=(x+1)(x-1)^m$ for $m\geq 3$. They further note that if the roots are restricted to $[-2,2]$ then the infimum is zero, as witnessed by a small perturbation of the Chebyshev polynomials.

They further conjectured that, if the roots are restricted to $[-2,2]$, then\[\lvert \{ x\in \mathbb{R} : \lvert f(x)\rvert < 1\}\rvert\geq n^{-c}\]for an absolute constant $c>0$. This was proved by Pommerenke [Po61], who in fact showed that this set must contain an interval of width $\gg n^{-4}$.

The current best known bounds (see the discussion in the comments) are\[1.519\approx 2^{4/3}-1\leq \inf \leq 1.835\cdots\]and\[\sup = 2\sqrt{2}\approx 2.828.\]

  • Following jspier's finite-atom approach, and using the standard reduction recorded in Tao/natso's notes, I have a small formalized update: the tail part of the previous $1.8$-type lower-bound argument can be pushed to $M=1.806304$ with a five-atom certificate.

    The method is the usual dual-forcing one. For each moving parameter $a$, construct a positive finite measure $\lambda_a$ with $U_{\lambda_a}>0$ on the normalized support $\{-1\}\cup[0,1]$. Then

    $$
    \int U_\mu\,d\lambda_a=\int U_{\lambda_a}\,d\mu
    $$

    forces at least one point of $\operatorname{supp}\lambda_a$ to lie in $\{U_\mu>0\}$, and sweeping $a$ gives extra length.

    For the tail block I use

    $$
    a\in[-1.806304,-1.708],
    $$

    and

    $$
    \lambda_a=
    \delta_a
    +1.174168821\,\delta_{a+1.80650001}
    +0.025921118\,\delta_{a+2.57053197}
    +0.118647936\,\delta_{a+2.68367709}
    +0.180553554\,\delta_{a+2.79017717}.
    $$

    Writing $y=x-a$, the check becomes a one-variable positivity problem for $V(y)$ on

    $$
    0.708\le y\le2.806304.
    $$

        • AI-assisted note: this was explored with Codex 5.5 xhigh. Please treat it as a computational/structural sanity check, not as a proof or certified update.

          I tried a piecewise-shift variant of your finite-atom route, since your piecewise weights seem to be doing the real work.

          The first naive version allowed the atom shifts d_j to vary independently from block to block, only requiring each shifted atom to remain inside its global swept lane. This gives very tempting but false numerical “certificates” around M=1.82 and higher. The failure mode is overlap: many blocks choose essentially the same favorable point near the right endpoint, so the sweep no longer produces new length.

          So I think the admissible piecewise-shift version needs a tiling/permutation constraint. If block i is a in [-A_i,-C_i], then the j-th shifted atom sweeps the interval [d_{j,i}-A_i, d_{j,i}-C_i]. For the sweep argument to count length, these intervals should tile the j-th lane, or at least be disjoint in a controlled way. With equal block width h, this suggests a form like d_{j,i}=A_i+s_j+pi_j(i)h, where pi_j is a permutation of the K block slots. The fixed-shift certificate is the special case pi_j(i)=i.

          This turns the next search into a coupled assignment problem: for each block i and each lane slot k, solve the small max-margin LP with d_{j,i}=A_i+s_j+kh, then ask whether there is a matching/permutation keeping all block margins positive. That might be a useful way to search beyond the current fixed-shift geometry without accidentally double-counting swept length.

          As a sanity check on tightness: using the posted fixed-shift geometry, pushing M from 1.814600 to 1.814650 already gives a negative margin about -7.9e-5 near block 192 in my checker. Increasing the number of blocks did not remove that obstruction. So any real improvement probably needs a new lane geometry or the permutation/assignment version above, not just more subdivision.

    • The current commentary accurately records what we actually know for sure: the sup is known to be exactly $2\sqrt{2}$, and the inf is between 1.519 and 1.835; these notes basically contain these results. The upper bound is likely to be sharp, but we do not yet have a proof. One needs to construct a dual measure for each candidate set $E$ of measure at most $1.835$, namely a probability measure $\nu$ supported outside $E$ for which $U_\nu$ is non-negative on $[-1,1]$. We can do this when $E$ is an interval, and it seems from numerics and theory that we can also do it when $E$ is the union of a large interval and a small interval (which was the most likely candidate for a counterexample to the conjecture to be located), but we don't have a clear way forward to construct this dual measure for general $E$. (But in the case where $E$ is the union of finitely many intervals, it seems the candidate measure should have a Cauchy-Stieltjes transform that looks something like a square root of a rational function with singularities at the endpoints of the intervals.)

  • I have succeeded in doing a variational analysis for general measures. Some parts may be "handwaved".

    By the coarea formula in geometric measure theory,
    $$ |\{U > 0\}| = \int 1_{U > 0} \, dx = \int_{t > 0} \int_{U = t} \frac{1}{|U'(x)|} \, dx \, dt. $$
    The inner integral is just the counting measure. Now I want to replace $U$ with $U + \varepsilon V$ and take $d/d\varepsilon$ at $\varepsilon = 0$. The result is, at $\varepsilon = 0$,
    $$ \frac{d}{d\varepsilon} |\{U + \varepsilon V > 0\}| = \int_{t > 0} \int_{U = t} \frac{d}{d\varepsilon} \frac{1}{|(U + \varepsilon V)'(x(\varepsilon))|} \, dx \, dt = \int_{t > 0} \int_{U = t} -\frac{\text{sgn } U'(x)}{U'(x)^2} \frac{d}{d\varepsilon}(U + \varepsilon V)'(x(\varepsilon)) \, dx \, dt. $$
    To compute the remaining quantity, I want the derivative of $U'(x) + \varepsilon V'(x)$ where $x = x(\varepsilon)$ varies as $U(x) + \varepsilon V(x) = t$. This works out to be $-U''V/U' + V'$. So
    $$ \frac{d}{d\varepsilon} |\{U + \varepsilon V > 0\}| = \int_{t > 0} \int_{U = t} -\frac{\text{sgn } U'}{U'^2} \left( -\frac{U''V}{U'} + V' \right) \, dx \, dt. $$

    Now apply coarea formula in reverse:
    $$ \frac{d}{d\varepsilon} |\{U + \varepsilon V > 0\}| = \int -1_{U > 0} \frac{1}{U'} \left( -\frac{U''V}{U'} + V' \right) \, dx = \int -1_{U > 0} \left(\frac{V}{U'}\right)' \, dx. $$
