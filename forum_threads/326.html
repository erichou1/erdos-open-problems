Does there exist $A=\{a_1<a_2<\cdots\}\subset \mathbb{N}$ which is a minimal basis of order $2$ (i.e. every large integer is the sum of $2$ elements from $A$, and no proper subset of $A$ has this property), such that\[\lim_{k\to \infty}\frac{a_k}{k^2}=c\]for some $c\neq 0$?
#326: [ErGr80,p.47]
number theory | additive basis

Erdős originally asked this for any basis (not necessarily minimal); such a basis was constructed by Cassels [Ca57].

Erdős and Graham conjectured a negative answer to this question.

      I hope you will forgive me writing at length about this proof - I'm taking this as a single example of a wider trend, and thought it would be interesting/useful to try and clearly pinpoint where this style of writing goes wrong, and why it is much harder to read (at least for me) than almost any human-written proof.

      Let me take the first paragraph of the 'Idea of the construction' section for example. Now the main result promises a construction of a minimal basis $A$ such that $A(x)\sim cx^{1/2}$ for some $c>0$. This implicitly contains three distinct claims about the set $A$, so what I'd hope for in a 'sketch' section is a summary of (a) how the set $A$ is actually built and (b) why it satisfies those three claims. The italics are from your text; after each sentence I have written what I am thinking, as the reader who is eager to understand this construction.

      The construction is organised around one active private witness at a time.

      OK, presumably 'the construction' refers to the set $A$ we're after. I don't know what it means by organised yet, but this suggests some kind of iterative procedure (as typical in these kind of basis constructions), where at each stage there is something called an 'active private witness'. This gives no clue what it refers to (a number? a set?), but presumably this will shortly be made clear.

      During epoch $j$, a large integer $X_j$ is reserved as the future private witness for a previously constructed element $a_j$.

      OK, another new non-standard word now, 'epoch'. From context I guess it means the $j$th stage of the construction, so presumably this is referring to how we build $A_j$ having already built $A_1,\ldots,A_{j-1}$ (and then $A=\cup A_j$). (Note that I'm already having to rely on my existing knowledge of similar arguments to make guesses like this.) Now we know what a private witness is - some integer $X_j$. The reason for why it is called a private witness is still mysterious. From the choice of letter I guess that $a_j$ is some element of $\cup_{i<j}A_i$, but I don't know which.

      Ordinary blocks cover the next range of sums while avoiding all representations of $X_j$.

      This is very confusing. The next range of sums? Next after what? I guess this means we are trying to satisfy the basis property at this point, so have some interval (presumably, from the use of the word 'range') we are trying to include in $A_{\leq j}+A_{\leq j}$. How this interval is defined I have no idea. I also have no guesses what it means by an 'ordinary block'. Yet another new, non-standard, phrase, which is not explained. And we are meant to avoid all representations of $X_j$. Avoid how? Avoid including any new elements into $A_j$ that, together with some element of $\cup_{i<j}A_i$ (or maybe also including the elements of $A_j$ chosen so far?) produce a pair which sums to $X_j$ perhaps.

      A lower seam is inserted just below $X_j$, then the release element $b_j = X_j − a_j$ is added, giving the unique representation $X_j = a_j + b_j$.

      Smoothing then fills the square shells below $X_j$ to their prescribed capacities while preserving this unique representation. The prefix $\leq X_j$ is then closed permanently, so later epochs cannot create another representation of $X_j$. Finally an upper seam covers the boundary region around $2X_j$.

      Smoothing? Of what, and how? What is a square shell? Prescribed capacities? What does it mean to 'close a prefix' (which presumably means the closed interval $[1,X_j]$?)? Now there is an 'upper seam' as well?

      I come away after reading this section with no real clue how the set $A$ is constructed, other than some vague ideas about representing something uniquely, and that it is an iterative procedure, constructed to be a basis.
