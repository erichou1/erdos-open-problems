Let $F(n)$ be maximal such that every graph on $n$ vertices contains a regular induced subgraph on at least $F(n)$ vertices. Prove that $F(n)/\log n\to \infty$.
#82: [Er93,p.340][Er95][Er97d]
graph theory

Conjectured by Erdős, Fajtlowicz, and Stanton. It is known that $F(5)=3$ and $F(7)=4$.

Ramsey's theorem implies that $F(n)\gg \log n$. Bollobás observed that $F(n)\ll n^{1/2+o(1)}$. Alon, Krivelevich, and Sudakov [AKS07] have improved this to $n^{1/2}(\log n)^{O(1)}$. Dyson and McKay [DyMc26] have improved this further to $F(n) \ll n^{1/2}$.

In [Er93] Erdős asks whether, if $t(n)$ is the largest trivial (either empty or complete) subgraph which a graph on $n$ vertices must contain (so that $t(n) \gg \log n$ by Ramsey's theorem), then is it true that\[F(n)-t(n)\to \infty?\]Equivalently, and in analogue with the definition of Ramsey numbers, one can define $G(n)$ to be the minimal $m$ such that every graph on $m$ vertices contains a regular induced subgraph on at least $n$ vertices. This problem can be rephrased as asking whether $G(n) \leq 2^{o(n)}$.

Fajtlowicz, McColgan, Reid, and Staton [FMRS95] showed that $G(1)=1$, $G(2)=2$, $G(3)=5$, $G(4)=7$, and $G(5)\geq 12$. Boris Alexeev and Brendan McKay (see the comments and this site) have computed $G(5)=17$, $G(6)\geq 21$, and $G(7)\geq 29$. Dyson and McKay [DyMc26] have proved $G(7)\geq 30$, and $G(k)\geq \frac{9}{163}k^2$ for all large $k$.

See also [1031] for another question regarding induced regular subgraphs.

  • Define $G(k)=\min\{ n : F(n)\ge k \}$, that is $G(k)$ is the smallest order on which every graph has a regular induced subgraph of order $k$ or greater. This is the same way Ramsey numbers are defined, and I find it easier on the brain. $G$ determines $F$ due to monotonicity.

    In 1997, I computed G(2)=2, G(3)=5, G(4)=7, G(5)=17 but didn't publish it. I just repeated that computation now with fresh code and got the same result. The lexicographic product $P_4[P_4]$ is a short proof that $G(5)\ge 17$ but I don't know of a non-computational proof that $G(5)=17$.

    Here are the counts, from 1 vertex to 16 vertices, of graphs which do not have a regular induced subgraph of order 5 or more: 1, 2, 4, 11, 31, 130, 728, 6027, 66308, 818276, 8336902, 45933753, 79888458, 23814804, 512906, 954.

    I have examples to show that $G(6)\ge 19$ and $G(7)\ge 28$ and I'm still searching for larger examples. I don't know why there should be a small leap from $G(5)$ to $G(6)$ then a large leap from $G(6)$ to $G(7)$, but the fact that there are 8 regular graphs of order 6 compared to only 6 of order 7 might have something to do with it

    My simple code for $G(5)$ took about 25 hours to show $G(5)=17$ but it cannot find $G(6)$ in a reasonable amount of time. I'm thinking about how to design a smarter algorithm. (Brendan McKay).

  • here are some amusing weaker problems where I still do not a know how to get a $\omega(\log(n))$ lower bound.

    1) let $\alpha(n)$ be the largest $k$ so that every $n$-vertex graph $G$ contains an induced subgraph $G'$ on at least $k$ vertices, so that all the degrees of $G'$ are equal mod 3 (the analogous problem mod 2 has a linear lower bound via a very slick old argument of Gallai, see also the semi-recent work of Ferber and Krivelevich (link) which shows that if $G$ has minimum degree then you can in fact find an induced subgraph of linear size with all degrees odd);
    2) let $\beta(n)$ be the largest $k$ so that every $n$-vertex graph $G$ contains an induced subgraph $G'$, and a set $S\subset V(G')$ of size $k$, all having the same degree in $G'$.

      • $\alpha(n)$ (and the higher order modular analogues) should all be linear I believe.

        meanwhile, $\beta(n)$ has a $O(n/\log(n))$ bound. the construction is take one clique of size $n/\log(n)$, two cliques of size $n/2\log(n)$, 4 cliques of size $n/4\log(n)$, and so on. this was observed in this paper by Caro, Shapira, and Yuster; the same construction also appears in this paper of Noga, Michael, and Benny (where they study $F(n)$ and related problems).

        I'm inclined to believe this upper bound for $\beta$ is also tight (though I am a little less confident, compared to $\alpha$).
