Let $n$ be sufficiently large. Is there some choice of congruence class $a_p$ for all primes $2\leq p\leq n$ such that every integer in $[1,n]$ satisfies at least two of the congruences $\equiv a_p\pmod{p}$?
#689: [Er79d][Er80,p.108]

  • I have a proposed proof of Problem 689 for all sufficiently large \(n\). A short PDF note is here:

    PDF note

    **Disclosure:** this write-up was prepared with AI assistance in finding literature and reviewing claims. I am posting it as a proposed proof and request for verification, not as a refereed result.

    This is meant as an attempt to make precise the route suggested in the earlier comments: use linear equations in primes to control averaged prime-pattern counts, and use hypergraph matching/nibble technology to turn the resulting fractional cover into a genuine disjoint cover. The new ingredient in the note is the robust \(P>n/5\) cleanup setup and the finite-core fractional matching formulation.

    The strategy is as follows.

    Start with \(a_2\equiv 1\pmod 2\), leave \(3\) in the zero class, and switch a fixed finite set\[
    S\subset\{7,11,13,\ldots\}
    \]to nonzero residues. After these fixed switches, the main residual demand consists of even numbers\[
    x=2^k u q,\qquad k\ge1,
    \]where \(u\) is odd \(S\)-smooth and \(q\notin S\) is prime, subject to fixed congruence exclusions. Fixed-modulus PNT gives\[
    |A_S(n)|=(1+o(1))\frac n{\log n},
    \]with half of this mass at \(v_2(x)=1\) and half at \(v_2(x)\ge2\).

    The cleanup primes are primes \(P>n/5\). Call \(P\) robust if\[
    H_S(P)\ge1,\qquad H_S(2P)\ge2,\qquad H_S(4P)\ge2,
    \]where \(H_S(x)\) counts the switched \(S\)-classes hitting \(x\). Switching a robust \(P\) creates no new unresolved debt: the only multiples \(P,2P,3P,4P\le n\) are covered by robustness plus parity and the unchanged \(0\pmod3\) class. By choosing \(S\) large enough, the robust residue density \(\delta_S\) can be made \(>0.94388\ldots\).

    Choose\[
    \delta_S^{-1}-3/5<\beta<\tfrac12(1-\tfrac35 e^{-2}),
    \]and set \(\Delta=(\beta+3/5)\delta_S-1>0\) for the surplus margin used below. Build a 3-partite hypergraph with vertex classes\[
    X=X_n\subset A_1(n),\qquad Y=Y_n\subset A_2(n),\qquad Z=\{P\in(n/5,\beta n]:P\text{ robust}\},
    \]where \(X_n,Y_n\) are finite coefficient cores capturing fractions \(\alpha_X,\alpha_Y>G(\beta)+\eta\) of the \(A_1,A_2\) coefficient mass, and edges\[
    (x,y,P)\quad\text{when}\quad |y-x|=2P.
    \]A matching covering \((1-o(1))|Z|\) labels gives enough paired covers; the remaining residual targets, including the coefficient tails outside the finite cores and the \(o(n/\log n)\) exceptional residual tokens, are covered singly by unused robust primes. The strict surplus \(\Delta>0\) is exactly what leaves enough unused robust primes for the singleton cleanup once the cores are chosen so that the discarded coefficient-tail mass plus exceptional tokens is at most \(\Delta N/10\).

    The matching is obtained by a finite-core fractional construction. In half-residue coordinates \(A\equiv aq\pmod W\), \(B\equiv bq'\pmod W\), the residual classes are a product set \(\mathcal C\), and for every unit label residue \(\pi\),\[
    \#\{A\in\mathcal C:A\pm\pi\in\mathcal C\}=\prod_{s\in S}(s-2).
    \]This gives an explicit continuum transport kernel with exact label load \(1\) and side load bounded by\[
    G(\beta)=\int_{1/5}^{\beta}\frac{dt}{1-2t}<1.
    \]Finite coefficient cores scale this by the captured core mass, giving side bounds \(G(\beta)/\alpha_X\) and \(G(\beta)/\alpha_Y\), both strictly less than \(1\) by the choice of cores. The aggregate transport then lifts to bounded typed kernels on the finitely many admissible typed polygons, with limiting label load \(1\) and bounded side loads.

    The analytic input is the finite-complexity Green-Tao-Ziegler theorem for affine-linear forms in primes. In a detailed proof this can be formulated using an auxiliary growing \(W_{\mathrm{GTZ}}\)-trick while keeping the fixed modulus \(W=\prod_{s\in S}s\) as residue data; equivalently, one can work with fixed-modulus singular series and verify the corresponding local-factor disintegrations in the first and second moment systems. It supplies the first and second weighted moment estimates for the edge loads. Importantly, no pointwise Hardy-Littlewood / Bateman-Horn estimate for fixed \(P=bq'-aq\) is used; all estimates are averaged finite-complexity linear-form counts.

    The deletion step uses the standard \(L^2\)-to-mass-loss argument: vertices with normalized side load above \(1\) have \(|L_X(x)-L_X^{\mathrm{lim}}(x)|\ge 2\gamma\), so the side \(L^2\) estimate forces \(|B_X|=o(|X_n|)\), and a Cauchy-Schwarz finish bounds the deleted mass by \(o(|Z_n|)\) on each side.

    The final rounding input is Kahn's fractional Frankl-Rödl-Pippenger theorem (Random Structures and Algorithms 8 (1996), 149-157), applied with the single statistic \(C(e)=1\). The hypergraph has codegree at most \(2\): a pair \((x,y)\) determines \(P=|y-x|/2\), and a pair \((x,P)\) or \((y,P)\) has at most two extensions. Hence\[
    a(t)=\max_{u\ne v}\sum_{e\supset\{u,v\}}t_e\le 2\max_e t_e=o(1).
    \]**What I would especially appreciate help with.**

    The two interfaces I would most like checked are:

    (1) **Kahn 1996, Theorem 1.5.** I have not been able to access the printed paper directly. Public metadata/abstracts for Kahn's paper confirm the pair co-load parameter\[
    \alpha(t)=\max\Big\{\sum\{t(A): x,y\in A\in\mathcal H\}:x,y\in V,\ x\ne y\Big\}
    \]exactly as I use it. What I cannot verify from public sources is the precise form of the conclusion in the non-perfect case: specifically, that for a fractional matching \(t\) with total mass \(\sum_e t_e=(1-o(1))|Z_n|\), \(\max_e t_e=o(1)\), and \(\alpha(t)=o(1)\), Theorem 1.5 produces an integral matching \(M\) with \(|M|=(1-o(1))|Z_n|\). If anyone with access to the printed paper can confirm that this is the right shape of the conclusion, or flag a hypothesis I have missed, I would be very grateful.

    (2) **The GTZ moment formulation.** I use one edge-total system and three second-moment systems on a fixed finite coefficient core. The note identifies the linear forms used in each system and verifies that no two are rationally affinely dependent after diagonal removal. The local-factor disintegration of the second-moment main terms, under either an auxiliary \(W_{\mathrm{GTZ}}\)-trick or fixed-modulus singular series, is asserted rather than written out in full. I would welcome any flag if this is not the right shape, or if the systems require additional admissibility checks I have missed.

    I do not currently see a hidden pointwise prime-pair input, no Hardy-Littlewood, Bateman-Horn, Elliott-Halberstam, or Goldbach-type pointwise estimate is used in the argument as written though I would welcome being shown otherwise.

    • In [1139] I've added a missing argument that would probably also solve this problem. This is a shortened version on the core from my discussions with GPT-5.2:

      If one could carry out the 2-fold covering construction in its “strong form” --- namely, for each sufficiently large $n$ choose residue classes $a_p \pmod p$ for \emph{every} prime $2\le p\le n$ so that each $j\in[1,n]$ lies in at least two of these classes --- then taking $N$ by CRT with $N\equiv -a_p\pmod p$ for all $p\le n$ would force every $N+j$ ($1\le j\le n$) to be divisible by at least two distinct primes $\le n$. Since the CRT modulus $\prod_{p\le n}p$ is astronomically large, we have $N\gg n^2$, so $N+j$ cannot equal a product of just those two primes; it must have an additional factor, hence $\Omega(N+j)\ge 3$ for all $j\le n$. In other words, this 2-fold congruence cover would directly produce length-$n$ intervals with no primes or semiprimes, giving a very strong form of the “$P_2$-free gaps” phenomenon.

      Conversely, the strategy we discussed for large gaps between consecutive $\Omega\le2$ numbers is essentially an attempt to build such a 2-fold cover, but it allows two relaxations: (i) it may use only a carefully chosen subset of primes up to $n$ (to keep $\log N=\sum\log p$ small enough to make $n/\log N$ large), and (ii) it may leave a tiny exceptional set of “stragglers” handled separately. If one succeeds even with these relaxations, one gets $\limsup (u_{k+1}-u_k)/\log k=\infty$. If one succeeds in the stronger “use all primes $p\le n$ and cover everything twice” form, then it also answers your finite question outright (and in fact implies a stronger interval construction than what is needed for the $\limsup$).

  • Here are a few more details on the numerology that suggests that a construction is possible. Let $z$ be a sufficiently large constant, and let $A$ be a parameter growing slowly with $z$, e.g., $A = \exp( (\log\log\log z)^2 )$ should work. Assume $n$ extremely large depending on $z$.

    We hold the primes $n/2 < p \leq n$ in reserve to clean up any stragglers; if we can sieve $\{1,\dots,n\}$ down to less than $n / 4 \log n$ such stragglers then we win. Accordingly, any set of cardinality $o(n/\log n)$ we view as negligible.

    We first sieve out $0 \pmod{p}$ for all $p \leq n/z$. Up to negligible sets, this leads the primes $n/z < p_1 \leq n$ (which are completely untouched), as well as semiprimes $p_1 p_2$ where $p_1 < z$ and $n/z <p_2 \leq n/p_1$ and $p_1 < z$ (which were touched once). (There are also a few elements of the form $p_1^j p_2$ but these are unlikely to cause trouble). There are about $n/\log n$ primes in the first category and $n \log\log z / \log n$ in the second category.

    To eliminate most of the primes in the first category, as well as semiprimes $p_1 p_2$ in the second category with $p_1 > z^{1/A}$ (of which there are about $n \log A / \log n$), we use the primes $p \geq n/z^{1/A}$. A congruence class mod such a prime hits about $n/p$ elements in $\{1,\dots,n\}$, of which about $n/(p \log (n/p))$ are usable for this purpose (one has to sieve out multiples of any primes less than $n/p$). We have $\sum_{n/z^{1/A} \leq p \leq z} n/(p \log (n/p)) \sim n \log\log(z^{1/A}) / \log n$ which is somewhat larger than the number $n \log A/\log n$ of targets, so a random sieve here should work well using the linear equations in primes technology (one has to design the random construction so that all primes and semiprimes have a decent chance of being hit, but this should be standard albeit tedious calculations).

    To touch the semiprimes $p_1 p_2$ with $p_1 \leq z^{1/A}$ we use the primes $n/z \leq p \leq n/z^{10/A}$. A congruence associated to such a prime $p$ can touch about $n/(p p_1 \log(n/p))$ semiprimes $p_1 p_2$ attached to $p_1$; there are about $n / p_1 \log n$ such primes, and the sum of the $n/(p p_1 \log(n/p))$ is about $n \log A / p_1 \log n$, so we (barely) win here (random constructions should then cut down the number of survivors by a factor of $A$ or so, which brings us to within range of being cleaned up by the reserve primes). Again one needs linear equations in primes technology to show that most targets get a reasonable chance of being touched at least once.

    I *think* something similar can work for larger $r$ than $r=2$, although one has to split into more cases than the two outlined above.

    If anyone is interested in developing these ideas into a full proof, I'd be happy to talk further.

      • This is a really minor remark in the direction of formalizing the obstruction which Terry points out.

        We first sieve all the primes $\le n^{1/2-\varepsilon}$; this leaves $\gg n(\log\log n)^{r-1}/\log n$ survivors. We now consider the efficiency of primes between $n/\exp((\log n)^{o(1)})$ and $n$. Note that for a fixed prime $p$ we have $n/p$ targets. Furthermore these $n/p$ targets should satisfy at most $r-2$ of the residue class conditions less than $n/p$; the set of integers which satisfy $r-1$ targets below $n/p$ are an atypical subset of those which survive until $\le n^{1/2-\varepsilon}$. The total contribution therefore from primes between $n/\exp((\log n)^{o(1)})$ and $n$ is $\ll \sum_{n/\exp((\log n)^{o(1)})\le p \le n}(n/p)\log\log(n/p)^{r-2}/\log(n/p) = o(n/\log n \cdot (\log\log n)^{r-1})$ if I computed correctly. (We are essentially sieving an AP of length $z$ by primes less than $z$ so we get an accurate upper bound.)

        The question of course now is the primes between $n^{1/2-\varepsilon}$ and $n/\exp((\log n)^{o(1)})$; the upper bounds above seems really inaccurate as $n/p$ is much much bigger than $\log n$ so a random model would suggest out upper bound is far from sharp.

      • Yes, that was basically the back of the envelope calculations I was using. In some ways the problem here has a simpler numerology than the long gaps one, and one can see the potential for a win here a bit more easily as follows. I tend to visualize these covering congruence problems as that of knocking down a progression of toy ducks at a carnival shooting gallery: each prime $p \leq n$ represents a "spraying bullet" in one's gun that can hit all the ducks that lie in one congruence class mod $p$. In this particular "game", each duck requires two "hits" to get knocked down, and the objective is to shoot in such a way that all ducks get knocked down. (I apologize for the violent nature of this metaphor; if someone can propose a more neutral one, I'll be happy to switch over to it.)

        Suppose one "shoots" at the congruence classes $0 \pmod{p}$ for all $p \leq n/2$. Then almost all the ducks are already hit twice by these congruences; the only remaining ducks (that received only zero or one hits) are primes, or powers of primes - so about $n / \log n$ ducks in all. On the other hand, we have about $n/2\log n$ congruences left in reserve (coming from primes $n/2 < p \leq n$) to position judiciously. Each congruence can be used to hit any specific duck once, so this can knock out about $n/4\log n$ of the remaining $n/\log n$ ducks: not enough to win, but tantalizingly close. If, as you say, one could use each of the large primes $p$ to hit many ducks simultaneously, we would have a good chance of actually winning.

        Unfortunately, each prime $p > n/2$ can only hit at most two ducks in $\{1,\dots,n\}$, and in fact probably only one in practice since almost all of the remaining ducks are odd. So this doesn't directly work, which is why I suggested the modified strategy in which one shoots out $0 \pmod{p}$ for $p \leq n/z$, leaving about $n \log\log z / \log n$ ducks remaining, but now one has as remaining ammunition some primes as small as $n/z$, each of which could potentially hit up to $z$ targets at once. If one could indeed hit the maximum number $n/p$ of ducks with each such shot then we would be in very good shape as indicated above since $n \log z / \log n$ can be made significantly larger than $n \log\log z/\log n$. Unfortunately one cannot quite do this well because many of the ducks are not divisible by small primes, in particular a prime $p \sim n/z$ can only hit about $O(z/\log z)$ $z$-rough targets by standard sieve theory. If one went with this bound then the total number of hits one can hope to make is $\sum_{n/z \leq p \leq z} \frac{n}{p \log(n/p)} \sim n \log\log z /\log n$ so one is now in a similar situation to before (off by a factor of 2 rather than 4, but still not enough). But on the other hand, only $n/\log n$ or so of the $n \log\log z/\log n$ remaining ducks are actually $z$-rough. I did some back of the envelope estimates of the number of "ducks" at different levels of roughness, and what each remaining "bullet" can hope to "hit" in each category, and I think there is actually enough "ammunition" to hit every duck twice.

        In the modern long gaps literature, there are three basic tools to try to hit a lot of primes with progressions as efficiently as possible: the Maynard sieve; the linear equations in primes theory of Green, Ziegler, and myself; and the hypergraph covering lemmas of Pippenger-Spencer (and later authors). Here I think the Maynard sieve will not be sufficient as it only picks up about $\log z$ primes in a length $z$ progression. This leaves the linear equations in primes theory, which as you know has significantly worse quantitative dependence on $z$ (even with your recent advances), but for this problem I think we can take $z$ to be a large absolute constant and $n$ extremely large (if we only care about the asymptotic version of the problem) and so the error terms in the linear equations in primes results should not be too important. (As a first approximation we can try to establish the result conditionally on the prime tuples conjecture; the linear equations in primes theory roughly speaking asserts that this conjecture holds for almost all shift parameters, which should be sufficient.) The hypergraph covering tools (using the "Rodl nibble") offer some efficiencies in making sure that different "bullets" shoot mostly disjoint sets, but there is enough room in the numerology that I think a simpler probabilistic construction will probably suffice, but one can certainly keep this tool in one's pocket in case the analysis gets too delicate.

(In principle one might be able to bash this out with heavy AI assistance, but I think I'd actually prefer a human collaboration (perhaps in a Polymath style) for this one.)

        • This is a really minor comment explaining why random sieving seems not to help. The one very minor advantage potentially of using the initial primes as a random sieving step is that there seem to be less issues of $z$-smoothness to think about. (I will also ignore the use of a $W$ trick; it will be required for $W\ll z$ when working through the problem.)

          Fix $z \le \log n$ for now and consider setting for all primes between $[z,n/z]$ the residue class to be $0 \pmod p$. There are two categories of number left.
          (1) Numbers which have not been touched at all. These are only the $z$ smooth numbers and primes larger than $n/z$; the second term is dominant. This gives $n/\log n$ integers which will be handled at the end.
          (2) There are numbers which are only touched exactly once. These integers are precisely of the form $p q$ where $p$ is a prime in $[z,n/z]$ and $q$ is $z$-smooth. The dominant term (from my computations) is when $p$ is near maximal so there are $\approx n\log(z)/\log(n)$ integers remaining. (In fact if one runs through the numerics one can really only pay attention to $pq$ with $p$ prime and $q\le z^{A}$ with $A\to \infty$ arbitrarily slowly at the end.)

          We now run the random sieve using primes less than $z$. We will try and be "efficient" in the end with primes of the form between $n/z$ and $n/2$; the primes between $n/2$ and $n$ will be used to handle stragglers.

          This leaves us with $O(n/(\log n \log z))$ integers in the first category and therefore reserving a vanishing small portion of primes in the final category we can handle these integers. For the final category, we have a $\lesssim n/\log n$ integers which survive this sieving so we are back at the "constant" factor off regime in the above post.

          We now consider using primes between $n/z$ and $n/(2z)$; due to the random sieving we only need to control the integers coming from (2). Initially for each such prime a given shift would have given us $z$ chances to hit the integers; however the random sieving gives us a "random penalty" factor of $\log z$ and so we really need to detect more than $\log z$ primes among the $z$ so the small--gaps technology is unsuitable (this is exactly what Terry says above).

          For the linear equations in primes technology, it is basically sufficient to try and use primes between $n/z$ and $n/(2z)$ to handle the $pq$ products in (2) with $p\in [n/z^{A},n/z]$. This amounts to solving $p_1 = (\epsilon z) q + t$, $p_2 = (\epsilon z + 1) q + t$,... $p_{\ell} = zq + t$ with $t$ an integer, $q$ is a prime between $n/z$ and $n/(2z)$ and $p_i$ has a prime factor between $n/z^A$ and $n/z$. The set $p_i$ formed by taking products can be proven to be Gowers uniform (up to major arc issues which would be corrected by a $W$-trick) and so the result should follow. (It is also rather difficult to make this quantitative; this requires uniformity in the $k$-aspect of various $U^{k}$-Gowers norms which have not been worked out in any detail.)
