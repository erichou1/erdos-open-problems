Show that for $k\geq 3$\[\mathrm{ex}(n;C_{2k})\gg n^{1+\frac{1}{k}}.\]
#572: [Er64c][Er71,p.103][Er74c,p.78]

It is easy to see that $\mathrm{ex}(n;C_{2k+1})=\lfloor n^2/4\rfloor$ for any $k\geq 1$ (and $n>2k+1$) (since no bipartite graph contains an odd cycle). Erdős and Klein [Er38] proved $\mathrm{ex}(n;C_4)\asymp n^{3/2}$.

Erdős [Er64c] and Bondy and Simonovits [BoSi74] showed that\[\mathrm{ex}(n;C_{2k})\ll kn^{1+\frac{1}{k}}.\]Benson [Be66] has proved this conjecture for $k=3$ and $k=5$. Lazebnik, Ustimenko, and Woldar [LUW95] have shown that, for arbitrary $k\geq 3$,\[\mathrm{ex}(n;C_{2k})\gg n^{1+\frac{2}{3k-3+\nu}},\]where $\nu=0$ if $k$ is odd and $\nu=1$ if $k$ is even. See [LUW99] for further history and references.

See also [765].

  • J. Ma, T. Yang [Upper bounds on the extremal number of the 4-cycle, Bull. Lond. Math. Soc. 55 (2023), no. 4, 1655-1667.] proved that
    There exist some real $\epsilon > 0$ and a positive density of integers $n$ such that
    $$ex(n, C_4)\leq \frac{1}{2}n^{\frac{3}{2}}+ (\frac{1}{4}- \epsilon)n.$$

    See also J.A. Bondy and U.S.R. Murty [ Graph Theory with Applications (Macmillan, New York, 1976).], p.247, Problem 8.

  • The sequence $(c_1,c_2,\cdots,c_n)$ is the cycle length distribution of a graph $G$ of order $n$， where $c_i$ is the number of cycles of length $i$ in $G$. Let $f(a_1,a_2,\cdots,$ $a_n)$ denote the maximum possible number of edges which satisfies $c_i\leq a_i$ for all $1\leq i \leq n $, where $a_i$ is a nonnegative integer. Given a graph $H$, the Tur\'an number $ex(n, H)$ is defined as the maximum number of edges in an $n$-vertex graph not containing $H$ as a subgraph. Denote by $m_i(n)$ the numbers of cycles of length $i$ in the complete graph $K_n$ on $n$ vertices. Then
    \begin{align*}
    ex(n,C_{k})&=f(0,0,m_3(n),
    \cdots,m_{k-1}(n),0,m_{k+1}(n),
    \cdots,m_n(n))\\
    &=f(0,0,2^{\frac{n(n-1)}{2}},\cdots,2^{\frac{n(n-1)}{2}},
    0,2^{\frac{n(n-1)}{2}},
    \cdots, 2^{\frac{n(n-1)}{2}}).
    \end{align*}
    Thus $ex(n,C_k)$ is a special case of $f(a_1,a_2,\cdots,a_n)$.

  • Building on the work of [BoSi74], J. Verstraete in [Ve00] improved the bound to $\mathrm{ex}(n, C_{2k}) ≤ 8(k − 1)n^{1+1/k}.$

    Pikhurko in [Pi12] improved the bound further to $\mathrm{ex}(n, C_{2k}) ≤(k − 1)n^{1+1/k} + O(n).$

    Bukh and Jiang in [BuJi14] have improved the bound to $\mathrm{ex}(n, C_{2k}) ≤ 80 \sqrt{k} \log k · n^{1+1/k} + 10k^2n$

    Z. He in [He21] has improved the bound to $\mathrm{ex}(n, C_{2k}) \leq (16 \sqrt{5} \sqrt{k \log k}+o(1))· n^{1+1/k}$
