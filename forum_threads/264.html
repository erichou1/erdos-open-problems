Let $a_n$ be a sequence of positive integers such that for every bounded sequence of integers $b_n$ (with $a_n+b_n\neq 0$ and $b_n\neq 0$ for all $n$) the sum\[\sum \frac{1}{a_n+b_n}\]is irrational. Are $a_n=2^n$ or $a_n=n!$ examples of such a sequence?
#264: [ErGr80,p.63][Er88c,p.105]
irrationality

A possible definition of an 'irrationality sequence' (see also [262] and [263]). One example is $a_n=2^{2^n}$. In [ErGr80] they also ask whether such a sequence can have polynomial growth, but Erdős later retracted this in [Er88c], claiming 'It is not hard to show that it cannot increase slower than exponentially'.

Kovač and Tao [KoTa24] have proved that $2^n$ is not such an irrationality sequence. More generally, they prove that any strictly increasing sequence of positive integers such that $\sum\frac{1}{a_n}$ converges and\[\liminf \left(a_n^2\sum_{k>n}\frac{1}{a_k^2}\right) >0 \]is not such an irrationality sequence. In particular, any strictly increasing sequence with $\limsup a_{n+1}/a_n <\infty$ is not such an irrationality sequence.

On the other hand, Kovač and Tao do prove that for any function $F$ with $\lim F(n+1)/F(n)=\infty$ there exists such an irrationality sequence with $a_n\sim F(n)$.

    • This is very nice and quite impressive!

      Regarding $a_n=2^n$: You and Aristotle are right. It is sufficient to range $b_n$ over $\{1,2,3,4\}$; I haven't observed this before. The range $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ was simply more convenient, since one only needs a single term from the tail to dominate the "gap" from the preceding term.

      Regarding $a_n=2^{2^n}$: Correct, this was not discussed in the paper [KoTa24]; it is a more classical result. Namely, Erdős and Straus in this paper from 1964 (Theorem 1) proved the following:
      If a sequence of positive integers $(c_n)$ satisfies\[ \limsup_{n\to\infty}\frac{c_n^2}{c_{n+1}}\leq1,\quad
      \limsup_{n\to\infty}\frac{LCM(c_1,\ldots,c_n)}{c_{n+1}}<\infty,\quad
      \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{c_n}\in\mathbb{Q}, \]then it has to satisfy the recurrence relation $c_{n+1}=c_n^2-c_n+1$ for sufficiently large indices $n$.
      Now just apply this to $c_n=2^{2^n}+b_n$ for a bounded sequence $(b_n)$, also estimating trivially $LCM(c_1,\ldots,c_n)\leq c_1\cdots c_n$. If the sequence $(c_n)$ satisfied the recurrence relation for large $n$, we would have that $(2b_n-1)2^{2^n}$ is bounded, but $|2b_n-1|\geq1$, so it is not.
      However, it is still impressive that Aristotle figured it all out just by itself!

      By the way, yesterday I ran Aristotle for several hours with the same irrationality question but for $a_n=n!$ (entered manually as text), but there was no luck. I believe that this part of the problem is still open.

          import Mathlib

          0 ∉ Set.range (a + b) → 0 ∉ Set.range b → Irrational (∑' n, (1 : ℝ) / (a n + b n))

    • FWIW, the statement is formalised correctly. Taken literally, it reads: there exists a sequence $(b_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}_0}$ taking values in $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ for all $n\in\mathbb{N}_0$ and a number $q\in\mathbb{Q}$ s.t. $\sum_{k\in\mathbb{N}_0}\frac{1}{2^{k+1}+b_{k+1}}=q$, i.e. so that the sum is rational. The prime notation on $\Sigma'$ in Lean 4 is necessary for denoting an infinite sum. Since the summand here is defined for all $k\in\mathbb{N}_0$, it gives what you intend it to be.

      Should one want a more human-readable translation of Aristotle's formalisation, see here.

    • Regarding using an LLM to translate back from Lean to English ("de-formalize"): in my experience looking at a bunch of misformalized statements, ChatGPT Pro was able to identify approximately half of the misformalizations. So perhaps there is still noticeable risk in that regard.

      However, if you present an LLM with a misformalized statement AND a proof that "cheats" by taking advantage of the misformalization, I have found a very high success rate with the LLM catching that.

      The remaining case would be a misformalized statement but with a "proper" proof (for the "real" statement). I'd guess that the LLM would be more likely to miss the misformalization there, because it's accompanied by a proof that doesn't "cheat", but I haven't actually tried this configuration because it hasn't come up.
