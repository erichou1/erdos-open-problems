For $0\leq k\leq n$ write\[\binom{n}{k} = uv\]where the only primes dividing $u$ are in $[2,k]$ and the only primes dividing $v$ are in $(k,n]$.

Let $f(n)$ be the smallest $k$ such that $u>n^2$. Give bounds for $f(n)$.
#684: [Er79d]
number theory | primes | binomial coefficients

There are claims of partial results and/or a path to a solution in the comments.
A classical theorem of Mahler states that for any $\epsilon>0$ and integers $k$ and $l$ then, writing\[(n+1)\cdots (n+k) = ab\]where the only primes dividing $a$ are $\leq l$ and the only primes dividing $b$ are $>l$, we have $a < n^{1+\epsilon}$ for all sufficiently large (depending on $\epsilon,k,l$) $n$.

Mahler's theorem implies $f(n)\to \infty$ as $n\to \infty$, but is ineffective, and so gives no bounds on the growth of $f(n)$.

One can similarly ask for estimates on the smallest integer $f(n,k)$ such that if $m$ is the factor of $\binom{n}{k}$ containing all primes $\leq f(n,k)$ then $m > n^2$.

Tang and ChatGPT have proved that\[f(n)\leq n^{30/43+o(1)}.\]The same proof would prove $f(n) \leq n^{2/3+o(1)}$ assuming the Riemann Hypothesis (or Density Hypothesis).

A heuristic given by Sothanaphan and ChatGPT in the comments suggests that, at least for most $n$, $f(n)\sim 2\log n$.

An internal OpenAI model [APSSV26] has given an elementary argument which shows $f(n) \ll (\log n)^2$, and constructs arbitrarily large $n$ such that $f(n)\geq (1/2-o(1))\log n$.

  • The OpenAI proof seems to have been optimized for brevity rather than explainability, and in general reads like "here is the first working proof that we found" rather than "here is a streamlined proof highlighting the key ideas", but it follows broadly similar lines to previous (successful or unsuccessful) attempts to upper bound $f(n)$ in the comments here. In all cases, the first step is to use Kummer's theorem to observe that a prime $p$ will divide $u$ whenever $n \% p < k \% p$, where I use $n \% p = p \{\frac{n}{p} \}$ to denote the remainder of $n$ after dividing by $p$. (Kummer's theorem also gives contributions when $n \% p^r < k \% p^r$ for higher prime powers $p^r$, but these contributions are lower order and so not of much additional use.) So to upper bound $f(n)$, one has to find a $k$ that is not too large such that $n \% p < k \% p$ for a lot of primes $p$. One should presumably focus on primes $p$ that are not much larger than $k$, otherwise the right-hand side $k \% p$ is small and the condition is unlikely to be satisfied. So one should think of $k$ and $p$ as both being small. We get to pick the $k$ and $p$ that we want, so we can take advantage of the probabilistic method and average (or take expectations) in these parameters with regard to suitably chosen probability distributions.

    The main enemy comes from the fact that $n \% p$ could be very close to the maximal value of $p$, in which case the condition is unlikely to be satisfied. If one had some good equidistribution theory on $\{ n/p\}$ then this would be easy to avoid, and this is basically why one can get bounds on $f(n)$ of polynomial type (actually using Vinogradov exponential sum estimates one could probably get a bound of the shape $f(n) \ll \exp(\log^{2/3+o(1)} n)$ from an equidistribution approach). But when $k$ or $p$ is only polylogarithmic in size then such equidistribution results are not known currently (the difficulty level here is comparable to the Riemann hypothesis).

    The new idea of the OpenAI proof is to use an elementary upper bound on the rare event that $n \% p$ is close to $p$ that is weaker than equidistribution, but still gives something useful. Specifically, if $n\% p \geq p-A$ for some $A$, then $p$ divides $(n+1) \dots (n+A)$. In particular, if $A$ and $Y$ are of polylogarithmic size, then the number of primes $p \asymp Y$ for which $n \% p \geq p-A$ is at most $O( A \log n / \log Y)$. Comparing this with the prime number theorem, we see that for a given scale $Y$, about $\gg Y / \log Y$ of the primes $p \asymp Y$ will have $n \% p < p - A$ for some $A \asymp Y / \log n$. (Equidistribution predicts that this $\log n$ denominator can be eliminated; this loss of $\log n$ is ultimately why the OpenAI paper gets a $\log^2 n$ type bound on $f$ rather than the conjectural $\log^{1+o(1)} n$ bound.)

    Anyway, for a prime $p$ of the above form, the condition $n \% p < k \% p$ would now hold with probability $\gg A / p \asymp 1 / \log n$ for $k$ drawn randomly in the range $k = O(Y)$. Summing over such primes, we conclude that the expected value of $\log u$ is $\gg \frac{Y}{\log Y} \frac{1}{\log n} \log Y$. Setting $Y = C \log^2 n$ for a large $C$, one can then ensure that $\log u > 2 \log n$ for some $k = O(Y)$, which gives the upper bound $f(n) \ll \log^2 n$. The actual proof is a bit more intricate (in particular introducing a new summation variable $j$ that is not present in the above discussion) because it is trying to optimize the explicit constant in this bound.

    One should be able to mostly eliminate this logarithmic loss if one is also permitted to average in $n$. In particular I expect these methods should be able to show that $f(n) \ll \log^{1+o(1)} n$ for almost all $n$, or something like this.

        • Yes, this looks sensible. To summarise here for convenience, the observation is that\[\log v(n,k)=\sum_{k<p\leq n}\log p\sum_{j\geq 1}1_{n\pmod{p^j}<k},\]and assuming the indicator function here to be binomial with probability $k/p^j$ gives the heuristic\[\log v(n,k)=k\sum_{k<p\leq n}\frac{\log p}{p-1}=k\log(n/k)+o(k).\]Since $\log\binom{n}{k}=k\log(n/k)+k+O(\log k)$ by Stirling, this means we expect $u>n^2$, i.e. $\log\binom{n}{k}-\log v>2\log n$, at $k\sim 2\log n$.

          This certainly makes sense as an 'average' observation, but I do worry that the worst case behaviour (which is the problem here) might behave a little different - one can imagine some conspiracy between $n$ and 'medium-range' primes which forces $v$ to be measurably larger than this suggests. There is also a suspicious spike in the numerical data you give at $n\approx 10^5$, much higher than the predicted $2\log n$.
