Let $a_n$ be an increasing sequence of positive integers such that for every sequence of positive integers $b_n$ with $b_n/a_n\to 1$ the sum\[\sum\frac{1}{b_n}\]is irrational. Is $a_n=2^{2^n}$ such a sequence? Must such a sequence satisfy $a_n^{1/n}\to \infty$?
#263: [ErGr80,p.63][Er88c,p.105]
irrationality

One possible definition of an 'irrationality sequence' (see also [262] and [264]). A folklore result states that $\sum \frac{1}{a_n}$ is irrational whenever $\lim a_n^{1/2^n}=\infty$.

Kovač and Tao [KoTa24] have proved that any strictly increasing sequence such that $\sum \frac{1}{a_n}$ converges and $\lim a_{n+1}/a_n^2=0$ is not such an irrationality sequence. On the other hand, if\[\liminf \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n^{2+\epsilon}}>0\]for some $\epsilon>0$ then the above folklore result implies that $a_n$ is such an irrationality sequence.

This problem was originally wrongly stated without the assumption that the sequence be increasing; DeepMind has given a counterexample without this assumption (see the comments).

Koizumi [Ko25c] has proved that $a_n=\lfloor \alpha^{2^n}\rfloor$ is an irrationality sequence of this type for all but countably many $\alpha>1$.

    • Here's the key idea. The argument is quite simple.

      We are concatenating blocks of consecutive numbers $[M_k, M_k + L_k)$. That is, the sequence $(a_n)$ is
      $$ M_1, M_1 + 1, \dots, M_1 + L_1 - 1, \quad M_2, M_2 + 1, \dots, M_2 + L_2 - 1, \quad \dots. $$
      To meet the irrationality condition, it suffices to get sufficiently fast decay in the sense that $L_k/M_k \le f_k(M_1, L_1, \dots, M_{k-1}, L_{k-1})$, where $f_k > 0$ is some function that can be explicitly worked out. To falsify the growth condition, it suffices to do, say, $L_k/\log M_k \to \infty$ and $L_k$ growing sufficiently fast.

      The observation is that these are not contradictory: if we recursively choose $M_k$ large, it is easy to make $L_k/\log M_k$ large while $L_k/M_k$ small, which gives the problem.

  • Our DeepMind prover agent has falsified the second question in Lean. The proof was found fully autonomously; the only problem-specific human input was the formal problem statement, drawn from Formal Conjectures. The proof (compilable with Lean 4.22) is available here (theorem erdos_263.parts.ii).

    Here's an informal summary of the proof (due to Adam Zsolt Wagner):

    Let $a_n = 2^{3^n}$, except on a very sparse subset of indices where we set $a_n = 2^n$. For this sequence, the limit of $a_n ^ {1/n}$ does not exist, but standard arguments show that for every sequence $(b_n)$ as above, $\sum 1/b_n$ is irrational.

    Looking at the proof and the problem more closely, we realized that Erdos’s original problem statement (https://renyi.hu/~p_erdos/1988-22.pdf; p.105) required $a_n$ to be increasing. Assuming you agree, it would be good to correct the statement. That said, the counterexample still seems somewhat interesting, so we thought we would share it with the community.
