Let $z_1,\ldots,z_n\in \mathbb{C}$ with $\lvert z_i-z_j\rvert\leq 2$ for all $i,j$, and\[\Delta(z_1,\ldots,z_n)=\prod_{i\neq j}\lvert z_i-z_j\rvert.\]What is the maximum possible value of $\Delta$? Is it maximised by taking the $z_i$ to be the vertices of a regular polygon?
#1045: [EHP58,p.143]

There are no solutions, partial or complete, claimed in the comments.
A problem of Erdős, Herzog, and Piranian [EHP58], who proved that, for any monic polynomial $f$, if $\{ z: \lvert f(z)\rvert <1\}$ is connected and $f$ has roots $z_1,\ldots,z_n$ then $\prod_{i\neq j}\lvert z_i-z_j\rvert <n^n$.

The value of $\Delta$ when the $z_i$ are the vertices of a regular polygon is $n^n$ when $n$ is even and\[\cos(\pi/2n)^{-n(n-1)}n^n \sim e^{\pi^2/8}n^n\]when $n$ is odd.

Pommerenke [Po61] proved that $\Delta \leq 2^{O(n)}n^n$ for all $z_i$ with $\lvert z_i-z_j\rvert \leq 2$.

Hu and Tang (see the comments) found examples when $n=4$ and $n=6$ that show that vertices of a regular polygon do not maximise $\Delta$. Cambie (also in the comments) showed that, in general, the vertices of a regular polygon are not a maximiser for all even $n\geq 4$.

There is a lot of discussion of this problem in the comments (see also the papers of Sothanaphan [So25] and Cambie, Decadt, Dong, Hu, and Tang [CDDHT26]). It is now known that, for even $n$,\[\liminf \frac{\max \Delta}{n^n}\geq C\]for some $C>0$. The best value of $C$ available is $C\approx 1.268$, proved in [CDDHT26]. If we restrict to $n$ divisible by $6$ this can be improved to $C\approx 1.304$.

It remains possible that the regular polygon is a maximiser for odd $n$; this is explicitly conjectured in [CDDHT26]. It is not yet known whether, for odd $n$,\[\lim \frac{\max \Delta}{n^n}=e^{\pi^2/8}\approx 3.433.\]The paper [CDDHT26] also contains a number of structural results about the form of an extremal set of points.

    • I think this is correct.

      From what I've been thinking about, the reason this seems hard is that the regular polygon is *not* always optimal here. If it's optimal you'd expect some kind of symmetry argument to do the job. But now we don't know what *shape* the optimal one has in some cases.

      Now it's more intuitive to think about $\log \Delta$. This is a sum of $\Theta(n^2)$ log-distances. But due to symmetry, these distances tend to cancel out. So it's nontrivial even to get $C > 1$ there, which means the *sum* of these log-distances increases by $\Omega(1)$ without canceling out too much. This seems like it should be easy, but simple examples cancel out extraordinarily and do not give this bound.

      On the other hand, there is no easy reason why we can't arrange for something that cancels out very little! The Pommerenke's bound there says that you can't increase the average log-distance by more than $O(1/n)$, which is nontrivial. The proof is very clever and uses a polynomial mapping which is uniformly bounded on the convex hull of the points. With this technique, you probably can't go below $O(1/n)$. Hence the answer is that the bound is a natural limit of Pommerenke's technique.

      I do not have intuition on whether you'd expect $\max \Delta \asymp n^n$. That'd correspond to average log-distance increasing from the regular polygon case by $\Theta(1/n^2)$. But why should this be the right order? I don't have a clear reason either way.

  • I finally succeeded in proving that there is $C > 1$ such that for all sufficiently large even $n$, $\Delta \geq C n^n$ !

    The proof is as follows:

    Consider the "new diameter construction" in my comment at [11:17 on 10 Dec 2025]. But instead of having a single $\delta_k$, I'm having $\delta_k(t) = (1 - 4k/n) t$.

    In other words, these are "velocity vectors", and I'm increasing from $t=0$ to $t=O(1/n)$.

    Formally, we're looking at $z_k(t) = z_k + v_k t$, where $z_k$ here are the original vertices of the regular polygon, and $v_k$ is some velocity vector.

    So we must differentiate
    $$ \log |(z_i-z_j) + (v_i-v_j)t| = \text{const} + \log |1 + \rho_{ij} t|, \quad \rho_{ij} = \frac{v_i-v_j}{z_i-z_j}. $$

    With help of Wolframalpha, I found that
    $$ \left( \log \sqrt{(1 + ax)^2 + (bx)^2)} \right)'(0) = a. $$
    However, by symmetry of the construction, the same $v_i,v_j$ will match with all four of $\pm z_i, \pm z_j$. So the sum of the first derivative is zero. We must look at the second derivative.
    $$ \left( \log \sqrt{(1 + ax)^2 + (bx)^2)} \right)''(0) = b^2 - a^2. $$
    This one doesn't trivially cancel. Plugging in, if $\rho = a + bi$, I simplify this to $- \text{Re} (\rho^2)$. So we must show that
    $$ \sum_{0 \leq i,j < n/2} \frac{(v_i-v_j)^2}{(\pm z_i \pm z_j)^2} $$
    has negative real part with magnitude at least $O(n^2)$. Why $O(n^2)$: this is the second derivative and the distance to move is $O(1/n)$, so that will cancel out out to $O(1)$.

    Now I'm getting rid of the $\pm$'s:
    $$ \sum_{0 \leq i,j < n/2} \frac{(v_i-v_j)^2(z_i^2 + z_j^2)}{(z_i^2 - z_j^2)^2}. $$

    Now here's the magic. I have $v_j = (1 - 4j/n)z_j$. Now $z_j^2$ here are the $n/2$th roots of unity. If its angle is $\theta$, actually $4j/n = \theta/\pi$. So what I'm looking at is actually a Riemann sum of a double integral involving $\theta$! To say the sum has negative real part with $O(n^2)$ is equivalent to saying the integral has negative real part (since we have $O(n^2)$ terms).

    Writing everything out correctly, I must show that
    $$ \text{Re} \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{((1 - \frac{x}{\pi})e^{ix/2} - (1 - \frac{y}{\pi})e^{iy/2})^2 (e^{ix} + e^{iy})}{(e^{ix} - e^{iy})^2} \, dx \, dy < 0. $$

    By Wolframalpha, this integral seems to be a real number with value
    $$ -0.481436 < 0. $$

    This completes the proof!

    Note: sadly this proof as written doesn't give explicit $C$ or the sufficiently large threshold for $n$. But it works.

    It's also not something of the form I would "expect"...

    • Follow up:

      1) About the value of $C = \lim_{n\to\infty} \Delta/n^n$ in this construction.

      Calculating back, we should expect, if $I \approx -0.481436$ is the value of that integral,
      $$ C = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\Delta}{n^n} = \exp\left( -I \frac{\pi^2}{128} \right) \approx 1.0378. $$

      This checks out perfectly with the numerics (which came out as $1.037...$)! Hence, this bolsters the confidence that the proof is correct.

      2) How fast does it converge?

      Numerically seems to be $\Delta/n^n = C(1 + O(1/n))$.

      I will also prove this below, again to strengthen the confidence of the argument.

      3) Is the integral singular (dividing by $0$)?

      No. There is factor of $(x-y)^2$ in the denominator, which also appears in the numerator. In fact the integrand is bounded! Thus, convergence of the Riemann sum to the integral is assured.

      4) There is a gap: I calculated the second derivative at $t=0$; but we need all the way to $t=O(1/n)$.

      Yes. I will now patch that as follows.

      First, notice
      $$ \log |1 + \rho t| = \text{Re} \log(1 + \rho t) = \text{Re} \left( \rho t - \frac{\rho^2 t^2}{2} + \frac{\rho^3 t^3}{3} - \frac{\rho^4 t^4}{4} + \dots \right). $$
      (This means I actually don't need those Wolframalpha's calculations; the Taylor expansion is explicit.) By symmetry, the odd powers will cancel. So the error term is the $t^4$ term. Also, I must consider nonzero $t=O(1/n)$. (Explictly: we're using the Lagrange form of the remainder in Taylor's theorem.) Hence we must estimate
      $$ \sum_{0 \leq i,j < n/2} \frac{(v_i-v_j)^4}{((\pm z_i \pm z_j) + (v_i-v_j)t)^4} = \sum_{0 \leq i,j < n} \frac{\rho_{ij}^4}{(1 + \rho_{ij} t)^4}. $$
      Here
      $$ \rho_{ij} = \frac{v_i - v_j}{z_i-z_j}. $$
      (Note on summing $n/2$ or $n$ terms: $z_{n/2+i} = -z_i$ but $v_{n/2+i} = v_i$.)

      Now here's the key point: $\rho_{ij}$ is in fact bounded (independent of $n$). So
      $$ \sum |\rho_{ij}^4| = O(n^2). $$
      (This is like we're doing that integral, but put the absolute value in the integrand instead i.e. we're using that the integrand is absolutely integrable.)

      This means that if I can get $|\rho_{ij} t| < \varepsilon$, the estimate will work:
      $$ |\text{fourth derivative}| = \text{const} \left| \text{Re} \sum_{0 \leq i,j < n} \frac{\rho_{ij}^4}{(1 + \rho_{ij} t)^4} \right| \leq \text{const} \sum_{0 \leq i,j < n} \frac{|\rho_{ij}^4|}{(1 - \varepsilon)^4} = O(n^2). $$
      And then the contribution in log-space of the error term will be
      $$ O(n^2)t^4 = O(1/n^2). $$
      (We only need $o(1)$ so this is stronger.)

      But: $\rho_{ij}$ is bounded and $t=O(1/n)$, so actually $|\rho_{ij} t| < \varepsilon$ is obtained easily.

      This fixes the gap and proves that the error does not derail the desired growth rate.

      5) Now, let's prove $\Delta/n^n = C(1 + O(1/n))$.

      We must notice there are three error terms:

      1. The fourth derivative: already shown to contribute $O(1/n^2)$.

      2. The Riemann sum. The error is $O(1/n)$ per term, so the integral $I$ can be off by a factor of $O(1/n)$. From the $C$'s expression in terms of $I$, it contributes error of $O(1/n)$.

      3. The $t=O(1/n)$, distance to move. It is not difficult (see below) to show
      $$ t = \frac{\pi^2}{4n} \left(1 + O(1/n)\right) $$
      is the maximum you can go to maintain diameter of $2$. Since we're going $(\text{second derivative}) t^2/2$, this comes out to $O(1/n)$ error as well.

      Together this shows $\log (\Delta/n^n) - \log C = O(1/n)$, which gives the claim.

      How to estimate $t$: there are probably many ways. Here is one way. We solve
      $$ |(z_i-z_j) + (v_i-v_j)t| = 2 $$
      Now I pull factor of $z_i-z_j$ out, rewriting to $\rho$ and rearranging to get
      $$ t = -\log |(z_i-z_j)/2|(1 + O(t))/\text{Re}(\rho). $$
      By explicit calculation $\text{Re}(\rho) = 1 - (\text{angle between } z_i,z_j)/\pi$. It's most restrictive if you take that angle to be $\pi - 2\pi/n$ (almost-diameter). Also $O(t) = O(1/n)$. Now use all that and it comes out right in the end.

      6) Conclusion. What was proved?

      There is a construction for all even $n$ in which one has
      $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\Delta}{n^n} = C \approx 1.0378 > 1. $$
      More specifically, for this construction, $\Delta = Cn^n (1 + O(1/n))$.

      This implies that for even $n$, $\liminf_{n\to\infty} \Delta_{\max}/n^n \geq C > 1$.

  • Here's a record of another of my failed attempt, for other (at least 3?) people working on this problem.

    In the original [EHP58] paper, it was shown
    $$ \frac{\Delta}{n^n} = \prod_{z \in \{\text{roots of f'}\}} |f(z)|. $$
    Here $f$ is monic polynomial with roots $z_i$ and $f'$ is the derivative.

    This is directly used in their proof that if $\bar{E} = \{z: |f(z)| \leq1\}$ is connected then $\Delta \leq n^n$. It's a really short proof so I'll reproduce it.

    $\textbf{Proof}$ ([EHP58]). It suffices to show $|f(z)| \leq 1$ when $z$ is root of $f'$. We consider the lemniscates $\{z: |f(z)| = B\}$ and increase $B$ starting from $0$. A lemniscate has a multiple point iff $f'$ vanishes at that point. So if $\bar{E}$ is connected, there is no multiple point for $B > 1$. Hence a root of $f'$ cannot satisfy $|f(z)| > 1$. $\blacksquare$

    So the attempt is to find a polynomial $f$ that is close to $z^n - 1$ but has easy explicit coefficients. Then that formula should allow for easy computation.

    However, I have been trying polynomials such as $z^n \pm \varepsilon z^m - 1$ and $z^n \pm \varepsilon (z^{n-1} + \dots + z) - 1$. All of these that I tried are worse than the original $z^n - 1$ (the regular polygon). Note when computing we must scale like so: $\Delta / (n^n \text{diam}^{n(n-1)})$.

    This presents a conundrum: the polynomial description (via coefficients) is easy to compute, but I can't perturb it to get a good one. In contrast, the geometric description (via roots) has explicit constructions that are good, but I can't compute with them. It seems I can't do "explicit construction" and "easy to compute" together currently! It's also known that there is no simple way to translate between the two descriptions: even though there is continuous dependence, the problem can be ill-conditioned see e.g. Wilkinson's polynomial.

    The thought process still continues...

  • I have been making progress towards proving $\Delta \geq Cn^n$ for large $n$ even and some fixed $C>1$. This has proven to be more challenging than expected. In particular, I have discovered some Fourier-type(?) phenomenon that may illustrate where the difficulty lies:

    $\textbf{A new "diameter" construction}$. First, following my heuristic (which will later shown to be flawed) that we should get $(1 + O(1/n))^{O(n)}$, I devised the following arrangement for even $n$ that is hopefully easier to get a closed form.
    $$ \delta_k = c \left(\frac{1}{n} - \frac{4k}{n^2}\right), z_k = \left(1 + \delta_k \right) e^{\frac{2\pi i k}{n}}, z_{k+n/2} = -\left(1 - \delta_k \right) e^{\frac{2\pi i k}{n}}, \quad k=0,1,\dots,\frac{n}{2}-1. $$
    Here $c = c_{\max}(n)$ is the maximum quantity that maintains the diameter of $2$. It is found numerically that $c_{\max}(n) \to \pi^2/4$ for $n$ large (probably not hard to prove).

    The idea here is that I'm moving diameters radially by $\delta_k$. I want $O(1/n)$ perturbation, but to maintain max distance of $2$ the consecutive $\delta_k$ can only differ by $O(1/n^2)$. Finally, $c/n$ has to become $-c/n$ on the other side to maintain consistency - this forces that we must decrease by $4c/n^2$ each time if we want linear interpolation of $\delta_k$.

    So far so good. Also, numerical evidence suggests $\Delta/n^n \to 1.037...$ for large even $n$. That's promising. I still can't prove that, though.

    $\textbf{Trouble with delicateness}$. However, here lies the trouble. I naively thought that any $\delta_k$ that is $O(1/n)$ and decreases by $O(1/n^2)$ should work, so I now observe that we can take
    $$ \delta_k = \frac{c}{n} \cos \left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right), $$
    which satisfies those conditions and also interpolates from $c/n$ to $-c/n$. The advantage is that if we let $\omega = e^{2\pi i/n}$, then
    $$ z_k = \left(1 + \frac{c}{n} \left(\frac{\omega^k + \omega^{-k}}{2}\right) \right) \omega^k $$
    is polynomial in $\omega^k$. This simplifies calculations. We also have numerically $c_{\max}(n) \to \pi/2$. Nevertheless, it is found $\Delta/n^n \to 1$ (very rapidly in fact), destroying any hoped-for gain.

    This shows that the manner in which the radii interpolate matters in delicate details...

    $\textbf{Refined intuition?}$. At first, I thought that we can perhaps make "local" estimates and combine them piece by piece; my $(1 + O(1/n))^{O(n)} \geq C$ heuristic reflects that. But the new construction and the "bad" variant show that the "global" arrangement of the whole configuration matters in ways that may be difficult to understand.

    In particular, this seems reminiscent of the phenomenon that convergence of sums can depend very much on how we choose to "space" points. I'm reminded of the Clenshaw–Curtis quadrature, which is a numerical integration method where we make a change of variable $x = \cos \theta$, and how that becomes more accurate and stable than Newton-Cotes simply by spacing points differently.

    Note this perspective is not so alien because we're actually looking at a quadrature of
    $$ \int_{z(s),z(t) \in \text{ curve}} \log |z(s) - z(t)| \, ds dt, $$
    where both the curve and sampling points change with each $n$. (I don't think viewing it this way helps with the calculations, though.)

    So: any proof/approach that ignores the global spacing won't work - that's at least useful to know.

    $\textbf{Conclusion (for now)}$. There are now at least two candidates for $\Delta \geq Cn^n$ for $n$ even.

    1. Stijn's construction for $n=6k$ based on $6$ circular arcs in my description above. We now see that the "circular" part probably matters; I didn't think it matters before.

    2. The diameter construction in this comment where we interpolate radii "smoothly". We also know that the exact interpolation matters.

    Still, no proof :( !

  • Here are some counterexamples with improved asymptotics. First we look at Stijn's "yanking a diameter" construction for even $n$:

    $\textbf{Claim}$. Let $n$ be even. From a regular $n$-gon, take any diameter and translate that radially as far as possible so as to still retain the same diameter of the set. Then $\Delta/n^n = 1 + O(1/n^2)$.

    So, contrary to TFBloom's expectation, the improvement to regular polygons is extremely modest.

    $\textbf{Proof}$. Let $1,\omega,\dots,\omega^{n-1}$ be the roots of unity. We're looking at
    $$ f(z) := \prod_{k \neq 0, n/2} |z - \omega^k| = \frac{|z^n - 1|}{|z^2-1|}. $$
    Now
    $$ f(1+\varepsilon)f(1-\varepsilon) = \frac{n^2}{4} + \frac{n^2(n^2-6n+8)}{48} \varepsilon^2 + O(\varepsilon^4) $$
    (via Wolframalpha). So if we're yanking out by $\varepsilon$, the improvement is $1 + O(n^2\varepsilon^2)$. Direct calculation shows we can take at most $\varepsilon = O(1/n^2)$. That gives the claim. $\blacksquare$

    Of course, the question is: can this be improved? It turns out the answer is "yes" - why not yank out all diameters at the same time? If we do that, we can optimistically expect improvement of
    $$ (1 + O(1/n^2))^{O(n)} = 1 + O(1/n). $$
    Here's one way that seems "good". We alternate between pushing and pulling consecutive diameters. To make this consistent we must have $n = 4k+2$ (not just $n$ even). This turns out to be "Reuleaux polygon plus arc midpoints". Incidentally, this is also Quanyu's construction for $n=6$ - so it's one of the strongest candidates for that case.

    $\textbf{Claim}$. Let $n = 4k+2$. Consider a Reuleaux $(2k+1)$-gon with arc midpoints. Then $\Delta/n^n = 1 + O(1/n)$.

    $\textbf{Proof}$. Let $z_1,\dots,z_{2k+1}$ be the polygon vertices on the unit circle and $w_1,\dots,w_{2k+1}$ the arc midpoints. Let the diameter be $2(1 - \varepsilon)$. We have $\varepsilon = O(1/n^2)$. Note $|w_i| = 1 - 2\varepsilon$. Then
    $$ \begin{align}
    \Delta_{z_i \text{ only}} &= (2k+1)^{2k+1} \\
    \Delta_{w_i \text{ only}} &= (1-2\varepsilon)^{2k(2k+1)} (2k+1)^{2k+1} \\
    \Delta_{z_i \text{ to } w_j} &= \prod |w_i^{2k+1} - 1|^2 = ((1-2\varepsilon)^{2k+1}+1)^{4k+2}
    \end{align} $$
    (Note $w_i^{2k+1} = - |w_i|^{2k+1}$.) Hence the ratio is
    $$ \frac{\text{prod of those three}}{(1 - \varepsilon)^{(4k+1)(4k+2)} (4k+2)^{4k+2}} = 1 + 4k^2(2k+1) \varepsilon^2 + O(\varepsilon^3) $$
    (again by Wolframalpha). For $\varepsilon = O(1/n^2)$, we get $1 + O(1/n)$. $\blacksquare$

    It could be that this asymptotics is optimal. Otherwise, the heuristic above suggests we need constructions that differ substantially from "yanking diameters"; so far all constructions (even the kite for $n=4$) are of this form.

          2. The remaining $n/6-1$ points have orbits of size $6$.

Of course there are cancellations, but we might think heuristically as follows.

    For maximum area, width, perimeter and sum of diagonals, it turns out that for particular values of $n$, the best polygon is also an approximation or behaves complex (so one only has a numeric approximation)

    $\underline{General\ background}$

    The diameter graph of a polygon with diameter $1$, has vertex set equal ot the vertices of the polygon, and edges equal ot the pairs of vertices at distance $1.$

    It is known that a diameter graph $G$ satisfies $|V(G)| \ge |E(G)|.$

    $\textbf{Area}$
    [ Foster, Szabo '07]
